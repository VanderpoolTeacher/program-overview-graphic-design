# Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

> M. David Merrill (2002). The framework that says: instruction works
> when five things are present, and works less well when any of them
> are missing. Use this to test whether an assignment is *teaching* or
> just *assigning*.

## Overview

Merrill synthesized recurring patterns across many instructional design
theories into five "first principles" — necessary conditions for
effective learning. Instruction is most effective when learners (1)
engage with a real-world *problem*; (2) *activate* existing knowledge as
a foundation; (3) see new knowledge *demonstrated*; (4) *apply* the new
knowledge themselves; and (5) *integrate* it into their existing world.

The framework is explicitly prescriptive — a checklist for evaluating and
building instruction — and it's particularly well-suited to project-based
studio courses where authentic problems drive skill development.

## Core concepts — the five principles

### 1. Problem-centered

Learning is promoted when learners engage with a **whole, authentic
problem** — not abstract concepts disconnected from real-world use.
"Whole" means the problem has the messiness of real practice (multiple
constraints, ambiguity, stakeholders), not a sanitized exercise.

For graphic design: a real-or-realistic client brief, a stakeholder with
opinions, an audience that may misread the work — these all count.
"Make a poster about X" doesn't.

### 2. Activation

Learning is promoted when relevant prior knowledge is **activated** as
a foundation for new knowledge. Without activation, new knowledge has
nothing to attach to. Common moves: a quick recall exercise, a "before"
artifact, a discussion of recent work, asking students to predict what's
hard about the upcoming task.

### 3. Demonstration

Learning is promoted when new knowledge is **demonstrated** — shown,
not just told. Demonstration includes worked examples, expert
think-alouds, case studies, and exemplars. Critically: demonstration
should make the *invisible* visible (process, decision points,
trade-offs), not just the final artifact.

### 4. Application

Learning is promoted when learners **apply** the new knowledge with
**increasing complexity** and with **corrective feedback**. One-shot
application is weak; iteration with feedback is strong. The "minimum
viable" version of application is one round; better instruction
sequences multiple rounds with progressively less scaffolding.

### 5. Integration

Learning is promoted when learners **integrate** the new knowledge into
their everyday world — by demonstrating it publicly, defending it,
critiquing it, transferring it to a new context, or creating their own
variations. Integration is what makes learning durable.

## How it applies to DSGN course design

Use Merrill's principles as a **structural check** on each assignment.
A well-designed DSGN assignment has all five present, in roughly this
order:

| Merrill principle | Where it lives in the assignment template |
|---|---|
| 1. Problem-centered | "Why this matters" + the specific problem statement in Instructions |
| 2. Activation | "Why this matters" sections that connect to prior coursework + "Real-world connection" hooks |
| 3. Demonstration | "Tools & resources" (templates, exemplars, case studies) |
| 4. Application | "Instructions" with multi-part deliverables + iteration cycles |
| 5. Integration | "Reflection" + portfolio inclusion + presentation/critique steps |

When an assignment feels weak, run the principles like a checklist —
the missing one is usually the cause.

### Concrete example — DSGN 220 (Design Thinking)

For a "design thinking process" assignment in DSGN 220:
- **Problem-centered (1):** A real client (could be a campus org) with
  a real ambiguous problem.
- **Activation (2):** Have students surface what they already know
  about the user group / problem domain before research begins.
- **Demonstration (3):** Walk through 1–2 historical case studies
  (IDEO shopping cart redesign, or similar) showing the full process
  including the pivots.
- **Application (4):** Students do their own end-to-end cycle — but
  with a checkpoint after empathy/define and after ideate/prototype,
  with feedback before they continue.
- **Integration (5):** Final pitch to the client + reflection on what
  they'd do differently next time + add to portfolio.

If any of those five are missing, students will execute the activity
without learning the underlying skill.

## Questions for assignment authors (Merrill checklist)

When filling out the assignment template, ask:

- [ ] **Problem-centered:** Is there an authentic, whole problem at
      the center? Not "an exercise that resembles a problem" — a real
      one or a high-fidelity simulation.
- [ ] **Activation:** What prior knowledge does this assume, and what
      does the assignment do to surface it? (If the answer is "nothing
      explicit," activation is missing.)
- [ ] **Demonstration:** Is there an exemplar, case study, walkthrough,
      or expert think-aloud that *shows* the work being done well? Is
      the *process* visible, not just the outcome?
- [ ] **Application — first try:** Do students apply the new knowledge
      themselves? With what scaffolding?
- [ ] **Application — iteration:** Is there a feedback cycle built
      into the assignment, or is it a one-shot? One-shot application
      is the weakest form.
- [ ] **Integration:** What does the student do with this knowledge
      *after* the deliverable is graded? (Portfolio? Pitch? Critique?
      Transfer to a different context?)
- [ ] **Critique check:** If a colleague said "this assignment is
      activity, not instruction," which of the five would they be
      pointing at?

## A note on critique

Merrill's framework is widely cited and intuitively compelling, but
much of the empirical evidence supporting it has historically been
generated by his own research group. Independent peer-reviewed
validation is thinner than the framework's adoption suggests
(Bichelmeyer 2004 in the bibliography raises this point).

The principles still function well as a checklist — most experienced
educators recognize what they describe — but cite specific outcome
claims with care.

  *College student perceptions of teaching and learning quality.*

  *Annotation (critique):* A substantive reflection noting that
