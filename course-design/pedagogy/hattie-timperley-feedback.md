# Hattie & Timperley's Feedback Model

> Hattie & Timperley (2007), *The Power of Feedback*. The framework
> that says: feedback is only effective when it answers three
> questions, at the right level. Use this to redesign critique.

## Overview

Hattie and Timperley's 2007 synthesis proposes that effective feedback
must answer three questions: **Where am I going?** (feed-up — goals),
**How am I going?** (feed-back — current status relative to goals),
and **Where to next?** (feed-forward — next steps). They also
identify four levels at which feedback can target — **task**,
**process**, **self-regulation**, and **self as a person** — with
the first three being productive and the fourth (praise/criticism of
the person) largely ineffective for learning.

This framework maps almost directly onto the design studio critique,
where feedback too often stays at the task level ("the kerning is
off") or collapses into person-level evaluation ("you're really
talented"). Neither produces transferable learning.

## Core concepts

### The three feedback questions

| Question | Function | What it sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| **Feed-up:** Where am I going? | Clarifies the goal | "The brief asks for a brand identity that signals approachability. Re-read the criteria." |
| **Feed-back:** How am I going? | Names current status against the goal | "Your current logo reads as serious, not approachable. Here's why: rigid grid, dark palette, condensed type." |
| **Feed-forward:** Where to next? | Prescribes the next move | "Try one variant with a softer typeface and one with a warmer palette. Bring both back next week." |

A critique that does only one of these is incomplete. **Feed-back
without feed-up** leaves the student unsure what they were aiming at.
**Feed-back without feed-forward** leaves them with a verdict and no
move. Many critiques skip feed-up entirely (the goal is "obvious")
or skip feed-forward (the next move is "your problem").

### The four feedback levels

| Level | What it targets | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|
| **Task** | Whether the work is correct/done | Useful for clear-answer tasks; weak for design |
| **Process** | The methods/strategies used to do the work | **Most powerful for design** — addresses the underlying skill |
| **Self-regulation** | How the student monitors and adjusts their own work | Builds independence and metacognition |
| **Self (person)** | Praise/criticism of the person ("you're talented" / "you're lazy") | Largely ineffective; can be actively harmful |

Effective design crit lives mostly at **Process** and
**Self-regulation**. Task-level feedback is fine for surface fixes
but doesn't transfer. Self-level feedback feels good but doesn't
teach; it can also entrench fixed mindsets ("I'm just not visual").

### Feedback works in the *gap* between current and goal

Hattie and Timperley note that feedback is meaningful only when there
is a clear gap between current performance and the goal — and when
the student has the means to close it. If the goal is unclear, or
the gap is too large to close with available resources, feedback
becomes noise.

## How it applies to DSGN course design

### Redesign the studio crit explicitly around the three questions

Most poor crits skip feed-up. Try this structure:

1. **Re-state the goal aloud** (feed-up). 30 seconds. The presenter
   names the brief's success criteria before showing the work.
2. **Peers and instructor offer feed-back at the process level.** Not
   "I like the colors" — instead "Your colors signal approachability
   well; the typography is fighting that signal."
3. **The presenter restates the next move** (feed-forward). The
   *student* names what they'll change next, not the instructor.
   This builds self-regulation.
4. **Optional: written feed-forward** — a short note from peers or
   instructor naming 1–2 specific next moves the student should try.

This format:
- Keeps feedback away from the self-level (no "you're talented")
- Forces feed-up to be present at the start
- Forces feed-forward to be explicit (and student-led, building
  self-regulation)
- Concentrates the feed-back portion on Process

### Where to weave it into the assignment template

The template's **Rubric** is feed-up at the moment the assignment is
distributed — students see the criteria before starting.

Add an explicit **feedback structure** to assignments that include
peer or instructor crit:
- Brief: 1-line goal restatement (feed-up).
- Peer/instructor remarks: structured by process-level prompts
  (feed-back).
- Student response: 1–2 next moves (feed-forward).

Avoid "give general feedback" prompts. They invite Self-level praise
or Task-level surface fixes.

### Concrete example — DSGN 320 (Motion Graphics) crit

CLO 5: *"Critique and revise motion designs based on audience needs
and platform context."*

A Hattie & Timperley-aligned crit:

- **Feed-up:** "The brief is a 15-second platform-aware promo for
  Instagram. Success = audience completion, brand consistency, clear
  CTA."
- **Feed-back (process):** Peer/instructor describe what they
  observed: pacing held attention until 0:09 then dragged; brand
  color is consistent; CTA is buried in the last 0.5s.
- **Feed-forward:** Student says: "I'll re-cut to 12s and move the
  CTA to 0:08 so it has 4s of dwell."

Notice: nobody said "your motion graphic is great" or "you're a
natural at this." That would be Self-level — feel-good and
instructively useless.

## Questions for assignment authors (Hattie & Timperley checklist)

- [ ] **Feed-up explicit:** Does the assignment make the goal
      *crystal clear* before students begin? (The rubric helps but
      isn't sufficient — the goal must be restate-able in 1 sentence.)
- [ ] **Feed-back at the right level:** Does the critique structure
      push feedback to *Process* (or higher), not *Task* alone, and
      not *Self*?
- [ ] **Feed-forward present:** Does every crit produce a concrete
      next move? Is the student the one naming it?
- [ ] **Closeable gap:** Is the gap between current and goal
      bridgeable with the time, tools, and skill the student has
      available? If not, the feedback won't help.
- [ ] **Self-level audit:** Are you (the instructor) catching
      yourself when feedback drifts to "you're so talented" or
      "this is amazing"? Those land warm but teach nothing.
- [ ] **Written feedback structure:** When feedback is written
      (margin comments, rubric remarks, email), does it include all
      three feed-up / feed-back / feed-forward questions?

## A note on the evidence

The 2020 meta-analysis (Wisniewski, Zierer, & Hattie) of 435 studies
finds an overall feedback effect of d = 0.48 — moderate. But
high-information feedback (process- and self-regulation-level) shows
d ≈ 0.99, much larger than simple corrective feedback. The framework
is well-supported empirically.

The model has been applied successfully in higher-education
contexts beyond design — Lipsch-Wijnen & Dirkx (2022) found that
even with a known framework, instructors default to task-level
feedback unless deliberately structured otherwise. Translation: don't
expect "use Hattie & Timperley" to change practice without explicit
crit-format redesign.
