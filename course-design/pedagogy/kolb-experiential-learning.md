# Kolb's Experiential Learning + Kolb & Kolb's Learning Spaces

> Kolb (1984) + Kolb & Kolb (2005). The framework that says: learning
> is a *cycle*, and you're only halfway there if students just do the
> deliverable. Use this to make sure assignments include reflection
> and reapplication, not just production.

## Overview

David Kolb's 1984 Experiential Learning Theory (ELT) proposes that
learning happens through a four-stage cycle. A learner has a
**concrete experience** (does something), engages in **reflective
observation** (thinks about what happened), develops **abstract
conceptualization** (extracts a principle or theory), and then engages
in **active experimentation** (applies the principle to a new
situation, starting the cycle over).

Individual learners tend to enter the cycle at different points and
have preferred modes — Kolb originally proposed four learning styles
(Diverging, Assimilating, Converging, Accommodating) based on which
adjacent stages they favor.

In 2005, Alice Kolb and David Kolb extended the theory to **learning
spaces** — the environment of a course must be designed to support all
four cycle stages, not just the ones that come naturally to either
the instructor or the student. The studio critique format maps very
naturally onto this cycle.

## Core concepts

### The four-stage cycle

```
            Concrete Experience (CE)
                  ↓ doing / feeling
   Reflective Observation (RO) ←  →  Active Experimentation (AE)
        watching / reflecting        planning / doing
                  ↑ thinking / theorizing
            Abstract Conceptualization (AC)
```

The cycle has two axes:
- **Vertical (CE ↔ AC):** how you grasp experience — by feeling vs.
  by thinking.
- **Horizontal (RO ↔ AE):** how you transform experience — by
  watching vs. by doing.

A complete learning cycle visits all four. Skipping any stage weakens
the learning.

### The four learning styles (use with caution)

Kolb's original LSI categorizes learners by which adjacent stages they
prefer:
- **Diverging** (CE + RO) — generates ideas, sees situations from many angles.
- **Assimilating** (RO + AC) — synthesizes information into theory.
- **Converging** (AC + AE) — applies theory to solve problems.
- **Accommodating** (AE + CE) — learns by doing, adapts on the fly.

**Important caveat:** Coffield et al. (2004) and others have found the
Learning Style Inventory has poor reliability and weak predictive
validity. Use the *cycle model* with confidence; treat the *style
typology* as a loose heuristic, not a categorization tool. Don't
assign students a "style" and tailor instruction to it — the evidence
doesn't support that.

### Learning spaces (Kolb & Kolb 2005)

A "learning space" is the physical, social, and pedagogical
environment of a course. The argument: a course must afford all four
cycle stages — concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract
conceptualization, active experimentation — through its assignments,
schedule, physical space, and norms.

A studio with no time built in for reflection is not a complete
learning space. Neither is a lecture course with no concrete
experience.

## How it applies to DSGN course design

### The studio critique maps to the Kolb cycle

A traditional studio crit structure already cycles through Kolb:

| Studio moment | Kolb stage |
|---|---|
| Student does the work | Concrete Experience |
| Student presents; peers respond | Reflective Observation |
| Instructor frames patterns / principles | Abstract Conceptualization |
| Student revises or applies to next project | Active Experimentation |

When a studio course feels weak, often one of these is collapsed —
e.g., critique happens but no one extracts principles, or principles
are extracted but never applied to the next round.

### Assignment-level implications

A single assignment should aim to traverse all four stages:

- **Concrete Experience:** the deliverable / making.
- **Reflective Observation:** explicit reflection (the template's
  optional Reflection section makes this real).
- **Abstract Conceptualization:** something that asks the student to
  state the *principle* they extracted — this can be in the
  reflection or in a critique session.
- **Active Experimentation:** a *next* application — a follow-on
  iteration, a portfolio entry that synthesizes lessons, a transfer
  to a new brief.

The assignment template's "Reflection" section directly supports RO
and AC. If you skip Reflection, you're cutting half the cycle.

### Course-level implications

At the course level, sequencing matters. A course where every
assignment is a fresh CE → CE → CE without RO/AC/AE in between
produces students who can produce work but can't articulate why.
A course where lectures dominate (RO + AC) without CE/AE produces
students who can talk about design but can't make it.

The Plan of Study positions DSGN 410 *alongside* DSGN 491
(Internship) deliberately — the internship is heavy on CE and AE,
and DSGN 410 is where the reflection (RO) and conceptualization (AC)
about business practice happen. Cross-pollinate the two.

## Questions for assignment authors (Kolb checklist)

When filling out the assignment template, ask:

- [ ] **CE — Concrete Experience:** What is the student *making* or
      *doing*? Is it a real thing, not a hypothetical?
- [ ] **RO — Reflective Observation:** Where in the assignment does
      the student step back and reflect? (Reflection section, peer
      critique, journal entry, presentation Q&A — any of these work.)
- [ ] **AC — Abstract Conceptualization:** Does the assignment ask
      the student to articulate a *principle*, *takeaway*, or
      *transferable insight*? Or does reflection stop at "what I did"?
- [ ] **AE — Active Experimentation:** Where do the lessons go next?
      (Portfolio addition, follow-on assignment, application to a new
      brief, peer feedback they then incorporate.)
- [ ] **Learning space check:** Does the *course environment* —
      schedule, physical space, norms — afford all four stages? Or is
      one stage chronically squeezed out?
- [ ] **Style critique check:** Am I assuming students learn one
      "style"? If so, stop. Design for the cycle, not the typology.

## A note on critique

The Kolb cycle is widely accepted as a useful model of how
experiential learning unfolds. The Kolb *learning styles* (the LSI
typology) are not — Coffield et al. (2004) systematically reviewed
71 learning-style models and found Kolb's LSI had poor reliability
and low predictive power. Bergsteiner et al. (2010) raise more
focused methodological critiques of the cycle's graphical
representation.

**Recommendation:** Use the cycle for assignment and course design.
Don't categorize students by "style" or design instruction around
fixed style preferences — the evidence doesn't support that
intervention.
