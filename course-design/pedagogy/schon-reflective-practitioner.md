# Schön's Reflective Practitioner

> Donald Schön (1983, 1987). The framework that says: professional
> expertise is enacted through practice and refined through real-time
> reflection — and the design studio is its theoretical home. The most
> design-specific framework in this folder.

## Overview

Schön argues that professional competence is not "applied technical
knowledge" but a kind of **knowing-in-action** — tacit competence
enacted through practice. When practitioners hit a surprise or
failure, they engage in **reflection-in-action** (thinking on the fly,
reshaping the situation as it unfolds) and later in
**reflection-on-action** (retrospective analysis after the work).

Schön's primary illustration of this is the architecture studio — a
master practitioner coaches by demonstrating, probing, and offering a
"hall of mirrors" dialogue with students' work. That makes him the
theoretical cornerstone for any studio-based design pedagogy, including
graphic design crits.

## Core concepts

### Knowing-in-action (tacit knowledge)

Skilled practice runs on knowledge that the practitioner cannot fully
articulate. A senior designer adjusts kerning by feel, balances a
composition by intuition, recognizes a typeface mismatch in a glance.
This tacit "knowing-in-action" is invisible to novices and difficult to
teach by lecture. Studio pedagogy works precisely because it makes
tacit knowing observable — through demonstration, dialogue, and
critique.

### Reflection-in-action

When a problem surprises the practitioner — a layout doesn't work,
a color choice fights the message — they reframe the situation in real
time. They "have a conversation with the materials," testing
hypotheses, listening for back-talk from the work itself. This is
reflection happening *during* the action, not after.

### Reflection-on-action

After the work, the practitioner steps back and analyzes: what
happened, what surprised me, what would I do differently? This is the
slower, more deliberate sibling of reflection-in-action — and it's
what most "reflection" assignments actually elicit.

### The reflective practicum

Schön's model of design education: a setting where students take on
real or simulated practice problems, are coached by a master
practitioner, work iteratively with their materials, and engage in
reciprocal dialogue with their coach. This is studio crit, properly
done.

## How it applies to DSGN course design

### Studio crit is the canonical Schön format

When a graphic design crit is going well, it's an instance of
reflection-on-action mediated by an experienced practitioner. When
it's going badly, usually one of these is missing:

- **Tacit knowing isn't being made visible.** The instructor reacts
  ("this doesn't work") without articulating *what* they're seeing or
  *why*. Students can't learn from a verdict.
- **Reflection-in-action isn't being demonstrated.** The instructor
  arrives at conclusions but doesn't show the path. Schön's
  "think-aloud" demonstration is what teaches the move.
- **The student's voice is absent.** A monologue critique is not a
  reflective practicum. The "hall of mirrors" requires dialogue.

### Where to weave Schön into the assignment template

The template's existing **Reflection** section is the right home for
reflection-on-action. But Schön's framework also implies:

- **"Why this matters" / Problem framing** — should plant the
  uncertainty that *prompts* reflection-in-action. A neat brief with
  a known answer doesn't require it.
- **Iteration cycles** — Schön argues practice is dialogue with
  materials. Single-pass deliverables don't allow this conversation.
  Build in at least one iteration loop with feedback before final.
- **Demonstration before independent work** — a designer think-aloud
  on a related problem, recording the *moves* not just the artifact,
  before students attempt the equivalent.
- **Crit structure** — frame critiques as Schön's "hall of mirrors":
  the student presents, peers and instructor respond by reflecting
  back what they see, the student refines understanding through the
  exchange. Avoid "instructor delivers verdict" formats.

### Concrete example — DSGN 412 (Portfolio Design) case studies

CLO 2: *"Write clear and compelling case studies that explain design
intent, research, and outcomes."*

The deliverable (a case study) is itself an act of reflection-on-action.
But the *teaching* of how to do it well is a Schön move:

- **Demonstrate** by showing two case studies side-by-side — one that
  surfaces the designer's reflection-in-action moves ("I tried X, the
  client pushed back, I tried Y, that opened the real question"),
  one that just lists what was made.
- **Practicum** — students draft a case study on past work, share in
  pairs, get back-talk, revise.
- **Reflection-on-reflection** — after the case study is written, ask
  students what they noticed about *their own process* that they
  hadn't seen before. That's the meta-move.

## Questions for assignment authors (Schön checklist)

- [ ] **Tacit knowing made visible:** Does the assignment surface the
      *invisible* parts of doing the work — process moves, decision
      points, trade-offs? Or does it only show finished artifacts?
- [ ] **Reflection-in-action prompt:** Does the assignment include
      enough uncertainty / surprise that students will *have* to
      reframe in the middle of the work? (A sanitized brief defeats
      this.)
- [ ] **Iteration with back-talk:** Is there a cycle where students
      can hear what the work is "saying back" to them — through peer
      crit, instructor feedback, or their own reflection — before the
      final deliverable?
- [ ] **Reflection-on-action structure:** Is the reflection
      open-ended ("what happened, what surprised you") or scripted
      ("describe your process step by step")? The first elicits
      Schön; the second often doesn't.
- [ ] **Crit format check:** Is the planned crit a dialogue (hall of
      mirrors) or a monologue (verdict)?
- [ ] **Coach modeling:** Are you, as instructor, demonstrating
      reflection-in-action somewhere in the course? Or only ever
      delivering finished judgments?

## A note on critique

Schön's empirical base is thin — most of the foundational examples
are case studies from one architecture studio (Caceres 2017 critique
in the bibliography expands this point). The framework's power is
conceptual, not empirical. Use it as a lens for *thinking about*
studio pedagogy, not as a body of evidence to cite for outcomes.

The model also assumes a competent practitioner-coach. In contexts
where the instructor lacks deep practice expertise, the "hall of
mirrors" can fail to teach.

## Sources

- **Schön, D. A. (1983).** *The Reflective Practitioner: How
  Professionals Think in Action.* Basic Books. ISBN 0-465-06878-2.
  *Annotation:* The seminal text introducing reflection-in-action,
  tacit knowledge, and the design studio as a "reflective practicum."
  Directly analogous to graphic-design crits and iterative project
  work.

- **Schön, D. A. (1987).** *Educating the Reflective Practitioner:
  Toward a New Design for Teaching and Learning in the Professions.*
  Jossey-Bass.
  <https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1987-97655-000>
  *Annotation:* Extends the 1983 argument into concrete studio
  pedagogy — the coach's moves, the student's difficulties, and the
  structure of a reflective practicum. Architecture chapters translate
  almost directly to a graphic-design studio syllabus.

- **Ali, A. (2022).** *Critically reflective practice in visual
  communication design teaching for higher education undergraduate
  program.* International Journal of Technology and Design Education,
  32, 1063–1078. <https://doi.org/10.1007/s10798-020-09626-6>
  *Annotation:* Qualitative phenomenological study of VCD
  instructors applying Schön's framework; surfaces four themes
  (perception, methods, significance, challenges) that map directly
  to graphic-design teaching contexts. Paywalled.

- **Fleischmann, K. (2024).** *Making tacit knowledge explicit: The
  case for online peer feedback in the studio critique.*
  International Journal of Technology and Design Education.
  <https://doi.org/10.1007/s10798-024-09911-8>
  *Annotation:* Empirical study with 90 undergraduate VCD students
  showing that structured online peer critique surfaces and transfers
  tacit knowledge. A direct operationalization of Schön's "tacit
  knowing" in a digital studio. Paywalled.

- **Tan, L., Kocsis, A., & Burry, J. (2023).** *Advancing Donald
  Schön's Reflective Practitioner: Where to next?* Design Issues,
  39(3), 3–18. <https://doi.org/10.1162/desi_a_00722>
  *Annotation:* Extends Schön to team-based design practice, asking
  how *collective* reflection functions — a refinement essential for
  collaborative graphic-design courses and capstones. Paywalled.

- **Caceres, C. H. (2017).** *Re-educating the reflective
  practitioner: A critique of Donald Schön's reflective practice and
  design education for engineering.* ResearchGate preprint.
  <https://doi.org/10.13140/RG.2.2.31642.24004>
  *Annotation (critique):* Argues Schön's framing of "technical
  rationality" is a rhetorical overreach and his empirical base is
  thin; useful for tempering over-reliance on the model. Open access.
