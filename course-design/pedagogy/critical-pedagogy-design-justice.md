# Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice

> Freire (1970) + hooks (1994) + Costanza-Chock (2020) +
> Decolonising Design (2018). The cluster of frameworks that says:
> design education is not a neutral container of "good design" — it
> encodes values, power relations, and aesthetic defaults. Use this
> to interrogate *who* the program is designing for and *whose voice*
> shapes its standards.

## Overview

This is the most political — and most contemporary — body of work in
this folder. Three threads converge:

1. **Critical pedagogy** (Freire, hooks): the classroom is a site of
   power, not a neutral container of content. Teaching either
   reinforces existing hierarchies or interrupts them.
2. **Design justice** (Costanza-Chock and the Design Justice
   Network): the defaults of "universal" design encode the values
   and bodies of dominant groups; equitable design centers
   community-led practice and explicit reckoning with whose lives
   are improved or harmed by design choices.
3. **Decolonising design** (Ansari, Kiem, Prado, Vieira de Oliveira,
   et al.): graphic-design canons (Bauhaus, Swiss, Modernism) are
   imperial inheritances; alternative aesthetic traditions and ways
   of knowing have been systematically excluded from "the canon."

Together these frameworks ask: *whose* design is good design? *Whose*
problems are worth solving? *Whose* aesthetics are taken as default?

## Core concepts

### Freire's "banking model" critique

Education that treats students as empty containers to be filled with
expert knowledge ("banking education") reproduces the existing social
order. Freire's alternative is **dialogical, problem-posing**
education: students and teachers as co-investigators of real
problems, with critical reflection on the world as the goal.

For studio pedagogy: the master/apprentice instructor-as-final-judge
model is closer to banking. The Schön "hall of mirrors" is closer to
dialogical — *if* the instructor's worldview isn't the only mirror.

### hooks's engaged pedagogy

bell hooks extends Freire by centering the embodied experience of the
classroom — race, gender, class, and the lived presence of teacher
and student. The classroom is not abstract. Whose body is at the
front? Whose voice carries weight in critique? Whose aesthetic
preferences are treated as professional and whose as decorative?

For DSGN: think about who's presenting in your case studies, whose
work is held up as exemplary, whose feedback is taken seriously in
crit. These are pedagogical decisions, not neutral defaults.

### The Design Justice principles

The Design Justice Network articulates 10 principles. Selected:

- **Center the voices of those who are directly impacted** by the
  outcomes of the design process.
- **Look for what is already working at the community level.**
  Honor and uplift existing solutions.
- **The role of the designer** is to facilitate, not to be the
  expert.
- **Sustainability and resilience** over speed and scale.

These principles directly shape how a design program might frame
"the user," "the client," "the brief." DSGN's MLO 3 names "client
needs, user behavior, and market data" — design justice expands that
list to "people most affected by design outcomes," which often
overlaps with but isn't identical to the paying client.

### Decolonising design

The decolonising-design movement names three claims:

1. **Design has imperial roots.** The discipline as taught in
   Europe/US emerged from colonial trade, advertising, and modernist
   universalism. These are not neutral inheritances.
2. **The canon is partial.** Bauhaus, Swiss, Modernism are Western
   European; design traditions from other regions are typically
   bracketed as "vernacular" or "ethnic."
3. **Methods matter.** "User research" framed as extraction (a
   designer studying "users") differs from research framed as
   collaboration with people whose worlds are at stake.

For graphic design: this affects what work appears in DSGN 230
(Design History), what methods are taught in DSGN 220 (Design
Thinking), and what counts as "professional" in DSGN 412
(Portfolio).

## How it applies to DSGN course design

### Audit the canon in DSGN 230 (Design History)

Most graphic-design history courses center 19th–20th-century European
and US-American figures. A critical-pedagogy audit asks:

- Are non-Western design traditions present, or only as footnotes?
- Are women, people of color, and queer designers included as
  contributors to "the canon," not just as case studies of difference?
- Is the framing "design history *is* European Modernism + others"
  or "European Modernism is *a* design history among many"?

This isn't about replacing the canon — it's about presenting it
honestly as one tradition among several.

### Re-frame "the user" across the curriculum

DSGN 220 (Design Thinking), DSGN 310 (Brand Communication), DSGN 410
(Business of Design), and DSGN 498 (Capstone) all reference clients,
users, and audiences. Design justice asks:

- When you say "user," whose body and life are you imagining?
- Are people most affected by the design outcomes also voices in the
  design process, or only research subjects?
- Whose problems is this brief solving — and whose problems is it
  ignoring?

These are not abstract questions. They change what the deliverable
should *do*.

### Crit norms

Engaged pedagogy implicates the studio crit format. Whose feedback
is taken seriously? Who interrupts whom? Whose aesthetic preferences
are treated as universal? Hattie & Timperley's framework provides
the structure; critical pedagogy provides the *interrogation* of
that structure.

### Concrete example — DSGN 410 ethics scenario assignment

The DSGN 410 existing-materials analysis flagged "professional
ethics" (CLO 4) as a gap with no dedicated home. A design-justice
informed ethics scenario could ask:

- A client wants you to design a campaign for a payday-loan company.
  How do you respond?
- You're working on a hospital wayfinding system. How do you
  involve people with non-English literacy or cognitive disabilities
  in the design — not as test subjects, but as collaborators?
- Your portfolio's "best work" features mostly white, able-bodied
  people. What does that signal, and is it the signal you mean?

These scenarios pull MLO 8 (Entrepreneurship) and MLO 7
(Collaboration & Comms) into ethical territory the existing
assignments don't reach.

## Questions for assignment authors (Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice checklist)

- [ ] **Whose problem:** Whose problem is this assignment solving?
      Are the people most affected by the design outcomes named, or
      assumed?
- [ ] **Whose voice:** Whose voice carries authority in the
      assignment? Is the brief framed by an "expert" voice (often
      the instructor's worldview), or does it leave room for
      alternative framings?
- [ ] **Canon audit:** When you cite exemplars, case studies, or
      "good design," is the set of references diverse — across race,
      gender, geography, era? Or is it the standard Modernist
      shortlist?
- [ ] **Power in the brief:** Does the brief assume a paying client
      whose interests align with the public good, or does it open
      space for tension between client interests and broader
      impact?
- [ ] **Feedback equity:** In crit, whose feedback is being treated
      as more authoritative? Are quieter voices being asked into
      the conversation?
- [ ] **Banking-model check:** Is the assignment positioning the
      student as receiver of the instructor's expertise, or as a
      co-investigator working alongside the instructor on a real
      question?
- [ ] **Honest scope:** This framework is uncomfortable to apply
      consistently. Don't tokenize. If a course can only honestly
      address part of this lens, name what it does *and* what it
      doesn't.

## A note on this framework

Critical pedagogy and decolonising design are *normative* — they
make claims about what design education *should* do, not just how it
*does* work. That's different from Backward Design or Bloom's, which
are largely descriptive. Use these frameworks as ethical lenses, not
as predictive tools.

The frameworks are also under active development. Costanza-Chock's
Design Justice (2020) and the Decolonising Design roundtable (2018)
are recent; the discourse will continue to evolve. Treat citations
in this folder as a snapshot, not a final answer.

## Sources

- **Freire, P. (1970/2000).** *Pedagogy of the Oppressed* (30th
  anniversary ed., M. B. Ramos, Trans.). Continuum. ISBN
  978-0-8264-1276-8.
  *Annotation:* Foundational text defining the "banking model" of
  education and arguing for dialogical, problem-posing pedagogy. The
  conceptual anchor for any critique of teacher-as-authority in the
  design studio. Open-access PDF circulates widely.

- **hooks, b. (1994).** *Teaching to Transgress: Education as the
  Practice of Freedom.* Routledge. ISBN 978-0-415-90808-5.
  *Annotation:* Extends Freire into the lived experience of the
  classroom, centering race, gender, and embodiment. Useful for
  thinking about how studio norms (whose aesthetic counts, whose
  voice dominates critique) reproduce or challenge structural
  inequalities. Paywalled (book); chapter PDFs circulate widely.

- **Costanza-Chock, S. (2020).** *Design Justice: Community-Led
  Practices to Build the Worlds We Need.* MIT Press.
  <https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001>
  *Annotation:* The contemporary synthesis for design educators.
  Includes a dedicated chapter on design pedagogies, the Design
  Justice Network principles, and community-led participatory
  design. Essential grounding for any graphic-design course
  addressing social responsibility or inclusive design. Open access
  via MIT Press.

- **Schultz, T., Abdulla, D., Ansari, A., Canlı, E., Keshavarz, M.,
  Kiem, M., Prado de O. Martins, L., & Vieira de Oliveira, P. J. S.
  (2018).** *What is at stake with decolonizing design? A
  roundtable.* Design and Culture, 10(1), 81–101.
  <https://doi.org/10.1080/17547075.2018.1434368>
  *Annotation:* The founding manifesto of the Decolonising Design
  collective, addressing whose epistemologies, bodies, and
  aesthetics are centered in design curricula. A direct provocation
  for reviewing what graphic-design courses treat as canonical.
  Open access via UAL Research Online.

- **Ansari, A., Kiem, M., Prado de O. Martins, L., & Vieira de
  Oliveira, P. J. S. (2018).** *Three perspectives on decolonising
  design education.* PARSE, 8.
  <https://doi.org/10.70733/53gdbvmu92sa>
  *Annotation:* Offers three concrete pedagogical positions —
  design as imperial legacy, "impossible methods" that expose
  exploitation, and local cosmologies as alternatives to Western
  problem-solving. Translates the decolonization argument into
  classroom strategies. Open access.
