# Pedagogy — Foundations for Course Authoring

Reference materials for the three pedagogical frameworks that ground the
DSGN course-content authoring system. Read these before authoring
assignments — they shape how the assignment template is structured and
what makes a "good" CLO mapping.

## How they fit together

The frameworks operate at different levels and reinforce each other:

- **Backward Design** is the top-level *planning logic*. It decides
  *what* a course or assignment should accomplish (CLOs) and *what
  evidence* will confirm it (rubrics, deliverables) before any
  instruction is designed. Every assignment in this program starts here.
- **Bloom's Taxonomy** sharpens *the verbs* in those CLOs — it makes
  "understand" become "explain," "apply," or "evaluate" so the
  cognitive level being targeted is unambiguous.
- **Merrill's First Principles** are the *instructional structure*
  inside each assignment. They check that the work is anchored in a
  real problem, activates prior knowledge, demonstrates new content,
  asks students to apply it, and integrates it back into their world.
- **Kolb's Experiential Learning** is the *learner-side cycle* — the
  loop a student travels through during the work. It reminds the
  author that students need both the experience and the time to
  reflect, conceptualize, and re-apply.
- **Schön's Reflective Practitioner** is the *design-specific theory*
  of how expertise develops in the studio — through reflection-in-action,
  tacit knowing made visible, and dialogical critique.
- **Hattie & Timperley's Feedback Model** is the *operational frame
  for critique* — feed-up / feed-back / feed-forward at the process
  level, not the self level.
- **Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice** is the *ethical lens* — who
  the program centers, whose voices count, whose canon is treated as
  default. Used to interrogate everything above.

In practice, a well-designed assignment:
1. Starts from a CLO declaration and rubric (Backward Design — Stages 1–2).
2. Has Bloom-aligned verbs at the cognitive level the work actually exercises.
3. Is anchored in a real-world problem with all five Merrill principles present.
4. Moves a student through all four Kolb stages — not just "doing the deliverable."
5. Surfaces tacit knowing through Schön-style demonstration + reflective practicum.
6. Uses Hattie & Timperley's three feedback questions in any critique structure.
7. Has been audited through the Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice questions
   for whose interests it serves and whose voices it includes.

## How this informs the assignment template

`course-design/templates/assignment-template.md` is structured to make
multiple frameworks visible at once:

- **Header → Learning Outcome Mapping → Rubric** — Backward Design
  Stages 1 and 2 made unskippable. The CLO verbs declared here are
  where Bloom's Taxonomy gets applied.
- **Why this matters → Tools → Instructions → AI use → Real-world
  connection** — Merrill's problem-centered, activation, demonstration,
  application, integration.
- **Reflection** — Kolb's reflective observation and abstract
  conceptualization, plus Schön's reflection-on-action, given a
  dedicated slot rather than left implicit.

When something feels off about an assignment, ask:
- Is the CLO/evidence chain weak? → Backward Design issue.
- Is the verb in the CLO unmeasurable? → Bloom's verb issue.
- Is one of the five Merrill principles missing? → Instruction issue.
- Is a Kolb stage skipped? → Experience-design issue.
- Is the studio crit just a verdict, not a dialogue? → Schön issue.
- Does feedback land warm but teach nothing? → Hattie & Timperley issue.
- Are you sure who the brief is *for* and whose voice is missing? →
  Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice issue.

## A note on critique

None of these frameworks is universally accepted. Each has known
limitations:

- **UbD** has been critiqued as biased toward verbal/literacy disciplines
  and may not map cleanly onto skill-heavy fields like graphic design
  (see Lin et al., 2025 in `backward-design.md`).
- **Merrill's** empirical evidence has historically been generated
  largely by his own research group; independent peer-reviewed
  validation is thinner than the framework's adoption suggests.
- **Kolb's learning styles** specifically have been seriously challenged
  on reliability and predictive validity grounds (Coffield et al.,
  2004); the cycle holds up better than the typology.
- **Bloom's Taxonomy** — Larsen et al. (2022) found the two
  dimensions (Cognitive Process × Knowledge) aren't strictly
  independent and verbs don't reliably signal cognitive level; use
  the taxonomy as scaffolding, not a deterministic classifier.
- **Schön** — empirical base is thin; the framework's power is
  conceptual, not evidentiary (Caceres 2017 critique).
- **Hattie & Timperley** — the *evidence is strong* (d ≈ 0.48
  overall, d ≈ 0.99 for high-information feedback). The challenge is
  practice, not theory: instructors default to task-level feedback
  unless deliberately structured otherwise (Lipsch-Wijnen & Dirkx
  2022).
- **Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice** — *normative* rather than
  descriptive. These are ethical lenses, not predictive tools. Don't
  expect them to tell you what *will* happen — they tell you what
  *should* be considered.

The frameworks are useful tools, not articles of faith. Where the
research is mixed, that's noted in the per-framework files.

## Suggested reading order for new course authors

1. **Backward Design** — start here. The 2012 ASCD white paper (free,
   20 pp.) is the fastest entry point.
2. **Bloom's revised** — Krathwohl 2002 overview (free PDF, ~6 pp.)
   gives the verb framework you'll use immediately.
3. **Merrill's 2002 paper** — 17 pp., articulates the five principles
   with worked examples.
4. **Hattie & Timperley 2007** — read alongside Merrill; they
   complement each other (instruction structure + feedback structure).
5. **Kolb & Kolb 2005** — extends the cycle to course environments;
   directly relevant to studio-format courses.
6. **Schön 1983** (or 1987) — the design-specific theory. Read
   selected chapters; not the whole book.
7. **Costanza-Chock 2020** — the contemporary design-justice
   synthesis. Open access, the Pedagogies chapter is the highest-
   leverage section for course authors.
8. **The studio-design papers** (Küçükersen 2026; Zainuddin 2025;
   Fleischmann 2024) — apply the frameworks specifically to design
   education.

Each per-framework file ends with the full annotated bibliography.
