## Learning Outcome Mapping

This is the summative assessment for DSGN 410 — the single integrated
demonstration that students have met the entire course. Every CLO is
exercised; nothing important from the course is left untested.

**CLOs addressed**

- **CLO 1:** Describe key business models and career pathways in the
  graphic design industry, including freelancing, agency work, and
  entrepreneurship.
- **CLO 2:** Develop project budgets, timelines, and proposals in
  response to client or market needs.
- **CLO 3:** Draft contracts, invoices, and usage rights agreements
  in alignment with industry standards.
- **CLO 4:** Apply ethical, legal, and professional practices to
  client relationships, self-promotion, and content creation.
- **CLO 5:** Communicate business strategy through branded content,
  data visualizations, and presentations.
- **CLO 6:** Use AI tools to support business operations such as
  estimating, content drafting, client comms, or market research.

**MLOs exercised**

MLOs 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8 (all course MLOs — no MLO 4 / UX-UI, which
is intentionally not part of DSGN 410).

**Alignment rationale**

A practicing designer doesn't perform the CLOs in isolation. They
combine them every time they take on a client. This summative
mirrors that integration: the student declares a practice
(CLO 1), runs a project through it end-to-end (CLOs 2, 3, 4, 6), and
pitches the whole thing (CLO 5, plus MLO 7 collaboration during peer
crit). Assessment by integration, not by checklist.

---

## Objective

Synthesize the entire course into one integrated demonstration of
your readiness to launch and operate a freelance design practice.
You will (1) declare your practice, (2) walk one client engagement
end-to-end through that practice, and (3) pitch it all to an
audience of peers and your instructor (and, where possible, a
practicing designer).

## Why this matters

Real designers ship. They don't just *have* a business plan — they
*execute* on it: send the intake email, write the proposal, sign the
contract, deliver the work, send the invoice, debrief the client.
This summative is your rehearsal for actually doing this after
graduation. The artifacts you produce here are usable on Day 1 of
your career — adjust the names, send for real.

## Instructions

This assignment has **two parts**: the Practice Launch Plan
document (Part 1) and the live pitch (Part 2). Both are required.

The 5 components below correspond to formative drafts you produced
in Weeks 2–9 — you are not generating from a blank page. Your job
in Weeks 10–16 is to integrate, refine, and defend through one
full client engagement.

### Part 1 — Practice Launch Plan document (CLOs 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6)

A single integrated document (Notion page / Figma file / PDF) that
makes the case for your design practice through one full client
engagement. ~7–9 pages total. Five components, in this order:

#### A. Practice statement *(CLOs 1, 4)*

One page. State:
- The kind of practice you're launching (independent freelance,
  cooperative, in-house, agency-employed, hybrid)
- Your target client / sector / problem domain
- Your services and approximate rates
- Two trade-offs your model makes explicit
- Whose problems your practice is positioned to solve, and whose
  voice is centered (one short paragraph engaging the Design
  Justice question — see
  `course-design/pedagogy/critical-pedagogy-design-justice.md`)

**Deliverable:** 1 page, ~300 words. ~1.5 hours of work.

#### B. Mock Client Engagement *(CLOs 2, 3, 4, 6)*

The longest section. Walk one client engagement end-to-end through
the practice you described in Part A. **Six lifecycle artifacts**,
in order:

1. **Intake email exchange** (~150–250 words). The first email
   from the client stating their need, plus your reply with
   clarifying questions and confirmation that you'll send a
   proposal.
2. **Proposal + budget + timeline** (1–2 pages). Scope,
   deliverables, milestones, price; include a budget table and a
   timeline visualization.
3. **Contract excerpt** (~1 page). Adapt a template (cite source).
   Include scope, IP/usage rights, kill fee, payment schedule.
4. **Milestone deliverable** — one actual design artifact you would
   deliver mid-engagement (brand mark, homepage wireframe,
   campaign concept board). May be a redux of prior portfolio
   work, scoped to fit *this* engagement.
5. **Invoice** (½ page). Line items, due date, payment method,
   late-payment terms.
6. **Close-out + debrief** (~250 words). A short close-out email
   to the client *plus* your own internal debrief — what went
   well, what you'd change, one specific thing you learned about
   your practice from running this engagement.

> **Internship anchor (required where possible):** If your
> concurrent DSGN 491 internship involved client-style work, use an
> *anonymized version of a real engagement*. Otherwise, use one of
> the fictional briefs from the course Drive folder.

**Deliverable:** 6 lifecycle artifacts integrated with brief
framing prose. ~6–8 hours of work spread across multiple weeks.

#### C. Pitch deck with data visualization *(CLO 5)*

6 slides making the case for your practice. Branded — your visual
identity consistent across slides. **At least one slide must be a
meaningful data visualization** arguing something about your
practice (target market, income projections, time allocation,
competitor positioning, etc.).

> *Why mandatory data viz:* CLO 5 explicitly requires it, and the
> existing materials analysis flagged that no other DSGN 410
> assignment exercised it. This summative closes that gap.

**Deliverable:** PDF, embedded in the document. ~2.5 hours of work.

#### D. AI workflow disclosure *(CLO 6)*

A short structured document (½–1 page) showing how you used AI
across the assignment. Required structure:

- **Tools used** (name + version where relevant)
- **3+ specific moments** AI showed up in your workflow
- **At least one prompt you wrote** (paste it)
- **What you kept, changed, or rejected** from AI output, and why
- **One verification you did** (looked up cited sources, asked a
  human, cross-checked with another tool)

> Disclosure isn't a footnote — it's CLO 6 evidence. The depth and
> specificity are the assessment.

**Deliverable:** ½–1 page, embedded in the document. ~1 hour of work.

#### E. Reflection *(CLO 4)*

250–400 words answering ONE of the course's essential questions:

1. What does it mean to make a living from this work?
2. When should you say no to a brief, and what does saying no
   professionally look like?
3. Who gets to be a working designer, and what would expand that
   pool?
4. How do you know an AI-generated draft is good enough to send to
   a client?

Write specifically — refer to your practice statement and your
client engagement as evidence. This is not a generic essay; it's a
defense of your practice's values.

**Deliverable:** 250–400 words, end of the document. ~1 hour of work.

#### F. Mid-summative revisions *(CLOs 2, 3, 4, 5)*

Real client work doesn't run linearly — circumstances change, and
designers must respond. Two events during the summative require you
to **revise** earlier components, not just polish them. You will
submit a short *change log* (½ page) at the end of each event
showing what you changed, where, and why.

**Event 1 — Week 12 (uniform).** Your client just lost 30% of their
projected budget. Revise your proposal, contract, and timeline to
fit the new constraint. What stays, what goes, what changes
contractually, and how do you communicate the change to the client?

**Event 2 — Week 14 (student-chosen scenario).** Draw or select one
scenario from the deck below (or pitch your own to the instructor in
Week 13 for approval). Revise the relevant artifacts and document
the change. Different students will face different scenarios — peer
crit becomes a comparison of judgment under different constraints.

> Both events test the same higher-order skill: *adapting your
> practice under pressure without losing its values.* This is where
> the course's CLOs 2, 3, 4, 5 get integrated.

**Deliverable:** Two ½-page change logs (one per event) + the
revised artifacts. ~2 hours of work each.

### Part 2 — Live Practice Pitch (CLOs 5, 7)

A **7-minute** live presentation in Week 16 of class. Format:

- **Minutes 0–5:** Present your practice. Walk the audience through
  one moment of your project package that demonstrates judgment
  (a scope decision, a pricing call, a contract clause you fought
  for, the data slide).
- **Minutes 5–6:** Process-level Q&A from peers and instructor
  (Hattie & Timperley — no Self-level praise / verdict).
- **Minutes 6–7:** *You* name your next move — what you'd refine
  before launching for real. (Feed-forward is yours.)

Recorded for assessment and student review. ~1 hour of prep.

## AI integration

This assignment **requires** documented AI use in at least three
distinct moments of the workflow (see Part 1 Section D). Patterns
to draw from:

- **Co-author:** AI helps draft a contract clause, a proposal
  paragraph, or pitch speaker notes. You revise; you keep ownership.
- **Operations:** AI handles repeatable tasks — drafting follow-up
  emails, formatting an invoice, summarizing market research.
- **Critic:** AI reviews your draft and surfaces gaps you address.

You **must not** ship anything an AI wrote that you have not
verified and revised. Disclosure required for everything; the
expectation is that you used AI well, not that you avoided it.

## Appendix — Event 2 Scenario Deck

For Event 2 (Week 14), pick ONE scenario below. Different students
should pick different scenarios where possible to enrich the studio
crit. Subject to instructor approval (Week 13). You may also pitch
your own scenario instead — but it must produce a substantive
revision across at least 2 artifacts.

### Scenario 1 — Scope creep without budget

The client returns to you mid-engagement asking for two additional
deliverables that weren't in the original proposal. They expect them
"because we're already working together" and don't want to increase
the budget.

**Revise:** proposal scope language, the client comm in your
intake/close-out flow, possibly the contract (kill fee or change-
order clause). Explain in the change log how you protected the
relationship and your labor.

### Scenario 2 — Late stakeholder added

A new decision-maker on the client's side appears in Week 3 of the
engagement. They have different priorities and want changes you've
already passed milestones on.

**Revise:** timeline (you've now lost time to re-explanation), the
proposal's stakeholder list, the contract's approval-process
language. Explain how you'd handle this without restarting the
project.

### Scenario 3 — IP terms challenged

The client's legal team comes back on the contract and demands
ownership of *all* design process work — including sketches, working
files, and rejected concepts. Your standard terms only transfer the
final deliverables.

**Revise:** the contract's IP/usage rights section, the proposal's
deliverable list (what's "delivered" vs. retained), and your pricing
(IP transfer typically commands a premium). Explain what you'd
accept and what you'd refuse.

### Scenario 4 — Audience pivot

The client realizes mid-engagement that their target audience is
different from what you scoped for — broader, narrower, or
different demographic. Most of your design work still applies, but
the data viz and the pitch deck don't.

**Revise:** the data visualization (it's now arguing the wrong
thing), the pitch deck framing, the practice statement (does this
client still fit your target?). Explain how you re-aimed without
starting over.

### Scenario 5 — Late payment + renegotiation

The first invoice is 60 days past due. The client claims cash flow
issues and wants to renegotiate — extended payment terms, partial
payment, or trading services. You need cash; they're a referral
source you don't want to alienate.

**Revise:** the invoice (with new terms), a follow-up email, the
contract's late-payment language for future engagements. Explain
your reasoning — and where you drew the line.

### Scenario 6 — Ethical conflict surfaces

You discover, mid-engagement, that the work you're doing for this
client will be used in a way you have ethical concerns about (e.g.,
the campaign targets a vulnerable group manipulatively; the client
plans to misuse the data viz; the brand is in a sector you find
problematic).

**Revise:** the close-out debrief (your decision and reasoning), the
practice statement (does your stated values position align with what
just happened?), and document any contract clause changes you'd
make for future similar work. Explain whether you finish the
engagement, walk, or some middle path — and why.
