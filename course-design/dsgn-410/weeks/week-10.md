## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Articulate** the scope of their Practice Launch Plan — which
   engagement they'll walk through and why it tests the practice
   they've declared.
2. **Identify** the components of their summative most at risk of
   being under-specified, using the rubric criteria as a diagnostic.
3. **Apply** the Hattie & Timperley feed-up structure to name a
   clear goal before receiving feedback, so the 1:1 consultation
   is focused, not open-ended.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 1, CLO 2, CLO 3, CLO 4, CLO 5, CLO 6
(kickoff — all CLOs oriented, none yet deeply advanced)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~30 minutes):**
- *Bring:* Your draft practice statement (Part A) — even if rough.
  If you haven't started, write one paragraph by hand before class.
- *Bring:* A 1-paragraph sketch of the client engagement you plan
  to walk through (which client type, which sector, what the
  central design challenge is). Does not need to be polished.
- *Read:* The summative rubric in
  `course-design/dsgn-410/assignments/01-practice-launch-summative.md`
  — the seven criteria. Underline the two criteria you feel least
  prepared for. Bring that annotation.

**Recommended:**
- Skim the Hattie & Timperley feed-up section in
  `course-design/pedagogy/hattie-timperley-feedback.md` — the
  "Where am I going?" question is exactly what the 1:1 is designed
  to answer.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | Instructor asks: "Write down the one sentence you'd use to pitch your practice to a stranger at a conference. Not your name — your practice." Students write for 90 seconds, no sharing yet. This surfaces whether they have a frame or are still vague. |
| 5–10 | **Frame the 1:1 structure** | Instructor briefly explains the Hattie & Timperley feed-up protocol for the consultation: each student will (1) state the goal of their practice in one sentence, (2) name the engagement they're walking through, (3) name the rubric criterion they're most uncertain about. That's the agenda for the 1:1. Students don't get to open with "so what do you think?" — they open with goal-first. |
| 10–40 | **1:1 consultations + peer-pair work** | See Interactive activity below. |
| 40–47 | **Debrief** | Instructor pulls 3 patterns from the consultations: common scope risks, common under-specified sections, common engagement choices that won't stretch the rubric. No names. *Kolb: abstract conceptualization from the room.* |
| 47–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Formative due before Week 11: polished Part A + 1-paragraph engagement sketch. Preview Week 11: practice statement and intake email studio peer review. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Scope-Check 1:1s with Structured Peer-Pair Hold

**Goal:** Give every student a direct feed-up conversation with the
instructor before the summative picks up momentum — catching scope
problems (too vague, too thin, too ambitious) at Week 10 rather than
Week 14. The peer-pair task keeps waiting students productive and
surfaces clarifying questions they carry into the 1:1.

**Setup:**
- Time: 30 minutes (1:1s run continuously; peer-pair task runs
  simultaneously in the waiting space)
- Group size: individual 1:1s with instructor (5–8 min each);
  peer pairs while waiting
- Materials: students' own draft practice statements and engagement
  sketches; printed copy of the summative rubric (or digital)

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(Before class)* Build a brief rotation list — who goes first,
   second, third. Post it or project it so students know when
   they're up and can plan their peer-pair time.
2. *(10 min, before 1:1s start)* Deliver the framing segment (see
   Lesson plan 5–10 min). Make the feed-up protocol explicit: each
   student's 1:1 starts with the student speaking, not the
   instructor asking "so what have you got?"
3. *(Ongoing, 5–8 min per student)* Run 1:1 consultations at a
   side table or corner of the room. Structure: (a) student states
   their one-sentence practice goal, (b) student names the
   engagement, (c) student names the rubric criterion they're most
   uncertain about. Instructor responds at process level: "Your
   scope is too broad for seven weeks — here's the narrowing move."
   Name one specific risk and one next step. Keep to 5–8 min;
   running long here cascades badly.
4. *(Simultaneously)* Students waiting run the **Peer-Pair task**:
   pair up, silently read each other's practice statement draft for
   2 minutes, then take 3 minutes to ask each other one clarifying
   question: *"After reading your statement I'm not clear on
   [X] — can you explain?"* This is not a critique; it's a
   clarification request. Students note the answer to bring to
   their 1:1.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Many students will arrive with vague practice statements ("I want
  to do branding"). Push toward specificity: *which* clients, *what
  size engagement*, *at what rate*. Vagueness here cascades into
  every section downstream.
- Some will want to spend the full 1:1 getting validation. Redirect
  to the protocol: goal first, then criterion of most risk.
- The peer-pair clarifying questions often surface gaps the student
  didn't realize existed — note those and let them bring them to
  the 1:1 if the question is still open.
- A few students will be genuinely stuck on which engagement to use
  (real internship vs. fictional brief). Help them decide; the
  decision doesn't need to be perfect, it needs to be made so the
  work can start.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class:* extend to 60 min or run 1:1s as a second class
  meeting that week. Don't compress the consultations — 5 min is
  the floor.
- *Smaller class:* combine the peer-pair wait with a brief group
  share-out of clarifying questions before the debrief.
- *Fully remote:* run 1:1s via breakout rooms; whole class stays
  in main room for peer-pair task.

- **Builds on:** Weeks 1–9 (all formative work). This is the first
  week where the summative is the live work — students are no longer
  handing in practice drafts, they are beginning to integrate them.
- **Sets up:** Week 11 (studio peer review on practice statement
  and intake email). The scope decisions made here determine whether
  Week 11 work has enough specificity to crit.
- **Pedagogical lens:** Hattie & Timperley (the 1:1 IS the feed-up
  + feed-back at process level — not a check-in chat) + Schön (the
  consultation is the reflective practicum: instructor makes tacit
  scope-judgment visible in real time).

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The emotional temperature in this class is higher than it looks.
  Students have spent nine weeks building toward this moment, and
  Week 10 is the first time the summative feels real and not
  theoretical. Pre-launch nerves are legitimate. Don't dismiss them
  ("you'll be fine") — acknowledge them briefly and redirect to the
  work: *"That nervousness is information. Let's use it to figure
  out where your scope is under-specified."*
- Some students will try to shrink scope to avoid the anxiety of
  ambition. Watch for the student who says "I'll just do a simple
  freelance thing" as a defensive move. Validate the simplicity but
  push for specificity — a simple engagement that's clearly scoped
  is a better learning instrument than a vague ambitious one.
- The 5-second opening prompt ("write your one-sentence practice
  pitch") will reveal who has a frame and who is still drifting.
  That's diagnostic information for the 1:1 sequence. Hold it — use
  it to triage who needs the longest consultation.
- Hold the 5–8 min per student religiously. The biggest risk of
  this format is the first 1:1 running 15 minutes and half the
  class not getting seen. Set a visible timer.
- Students who came in with no draft will feel exposed during the
  peer-pair task. Redirect them: the peer-pair isn't graded and a
  rough paragraph is enough. The point is to generate *something*
  to receive a question about.
- Finish class by naming 3 patterns from the room without naming
  students. This normalizes the common struggles — "several students
  are grappling with X" lands very differently than silence after
  the 1:1s end.
