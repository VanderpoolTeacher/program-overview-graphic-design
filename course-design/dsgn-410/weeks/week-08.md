## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Compose** a professional response to a client brief that
   conflicts with their values — without burning the relationship
   or compromising their position.
2. **Analyze** an IP dispute scenario to determine what each party
   is claiming and what professional recourse exists.
3. **Evaluate** whether a design engagement involves exploitation
   of users or workers — and articulate what "professional" looks
   like when "professional" and "ethical" are in conflict.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 4 (primary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~45 minutes):**
- *Read:* Mike Monteiro, "Design is a Job" — the chapter on saying
  no to clients (~15 min). Frame: "no" is a professional skill, not
  a failure of client service.
- *Read:* AIGA, "Designer of 2025: Professional Ethics in Design" or
  the AIGA Code of Ethics (~20 min). Available at
  <https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-design-business-and-ethics>.
  Focus on the sections addressing client relationships and work
  that harms communities.
- *Read:* One short case study: Armin Vit, "The Ethics of Logos" on
  Brand New (UnderConsideration) — specifically a case where a
  designer worked with a controversial brand. (~10 min, instructor
  locates a current example from the Brand New archives or a
  comparable practitioner reflection.)

**Recommended (optional):**
- Costanza-Chock, "Design Justice" — Chapter 1 introduction (~20 min).
  MIT Press open access at <https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001>.
  Good primer for students who want the theoretical grounding for
  the exploitation scenario.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "Have you ever been asked to do something — at work, in class, anywhere — that you thought was wrong? What did you do?" — 60 seconds of individual reflection, then two or three students share briefly. No judgment. This activation establishes that ethics is lived, not theoretical, before the content delivery names it. Merrill: activate the prior experience that is the real raw material for this week. |
| 5–20 | **Content delivery** | Three scenarios presented as cases, not rules. Do not front-load with "here is the right answer" — present each scenario and the competing interests, then let the activity surface the reasoning. (a) **Scenario 1 — Saying no to a brief.** Client brief whose values conflict with yours (e.g., a payday-loan company, a campaign to roll back environmental regulations, a brand whose supply chain you've researched and found problematic). What are the actual moves available? Declining, qualifying acceptance, deferring the question. What language is professional? What's the cost of saying yes? (b) **Scenario 2 — IP dispute.** Client wants to own all process work (sketches, explorations, research notes) in addition to the final deliverable. You did not address this in your contract — or you did, and they're contesting it. What are you actually owed? What can you do? (c) **Scenario 3 — Exploitation.** Client is a platform that uses design to dark-pattern or extract from users (a deceptive checkout flow; an addictive feed; a "social good" campaign masking labor exploitation). Where is the designer's responsibility? Is "I was just doing the brief" a viable professional stance? Schön: tacit knowing visible — show the real decisions designers make when values are on the line, not a clean ethics checklist. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | The Ethics Triangle — see below. |
| 40–48 | **Debrief** | "What counts as professional when values conflict?" Ask directly. Listen for: the default toward client deference (name it), the assumption that ethics is individual preference (push back), and moments where students named a concrete move rather than a principle. Surface the Critical Pedagogy question: whose definition of "professional" is operating? (Often: the client's. Often: the dominant-group standard.) Kolb: abstract conceptualization — move from the specific scenarios to the generalizable question. |
| 48–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Hand off the formative deliverable: ethics scenario response due before Week 9 class. Preview Week 9: branded communication and AI as an operations tool — the practical side of the final summative. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** The Ethics Triangle

**Goal:** Students work with a real scenario, write a professional
response, then review another group's response — surfacing that
"professional" is not a neutral standard and that different designers
with different stakes would make different calls. The group structure
means students see three responses to three different scenarios in
the time it would take to work through one together as a class.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (6 min write / 6 min swap + read / 8 min debrief)
- Group size: three groups of equal size (works for 6–18 students;
  for smaller classes, individuals or pairs can represent each
  "side" of the triangle)
- Materials: three scenario cards (one per group) with the
  scenario summary and the prompt: "Write a 4–5 sentence professional
  response to this situation — what you would say or do, and why."
  The response can be a message to the client, a note-to-self
  decision record, or a policy you'd establish for your practice.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(1 min)* Divide into three groups. Each group receives one
   scenario card. Read the scenario as a group; make sure everyone
   understands the situation before writing begins.
2. *(6 min)* **Group write.** Each group drafts a collective
   professional response. One person writes; others contribute. The
   goal is consensus — or, if the group can't reach consensus, a
   response that acknowledges the disagreement.
3. *(1 min, transition)* Groups pass their scenario card and their
   written response to the group on their left (or whichever
   direction you establish — the point is each group now has a
   *different* scenario and a *different* group's response to it).
4. *(5 min)* **Review.** The receiving group reads the response
   they've been handed. Their task is not to grade it — it's to
   answer: "What does this response assume about what 'professional'
   means? Whose interests is it prioritizing? Is there anything it's
   not saying that should be said?"
5. *(8 min)* **Full debrief.** Each group reports out in 90 seconds
   on what they found reviewing someone else's response. Instructor
   listens for: the moments where groups named different ethical
   standards; where "professional" was defined by client interest
   rather than designer values; where groups agreed in principle
   but differed in execution. Name those disagreements — don't
   resolve them. "This is the real conversation the industry has
   been having. You just had it."

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most groups will write Scenario 1 responses that decline politely
  but vaguely — "this isn't the right fit for me." Push during debrief:
  why not name the values conflict directly? What's the cost of
  naming it? This is where the power-asymmetry question lives.
- Scenario 2 (IP dispute) will often produce technically correct
  responses about contracts without engaging the relational
  dimension. Ask: "What does winning this dispute cost you? Is there
  a response that's legally sound and relationship-preserving?"
- Scenario 3 (exploitation) is the hardest. Some students will
  write responses that decline immediately. Others will write
  responses that accept with conditions. Both are defensible;
  neither is automatically "professional." The review step is
  designed to make those differences visible.
- When reviewing another group's response, students often default
  to "this is good" — Hattie & Timperley's self-level praise. Push
  them to the process level: "What specific move in this response
  would you do differently? Why?"

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Smaller class (under 6):* run as a whole-class activity with
  three scenarios on the board. Assign one scenario to each third
  of the class; everyone writes individually, then shares.
- *Larger class (over 18):* keep three groups but designate a
  "dissenter" in each group whose job is to write a minority-
  opinion response. The reviewing group sees both the majority and
  dissenting views.
- *More challenge:* after the debrief, ask each group to revise
  their original response in light of what they learned from the
  review. This adds a Schön reflection-on-action loop.

## Resources

- **Three scenario cards:** instructor-prepared, one per group. Write
  these from real industry situations (not invented abstractions).
  Suggested sources: reported cases in design trade press (Brand New,
  AIGA Eye on Design, Dezeen), the AIGA ethics case studies, or
  your own anonymized professional experience. Refresh annually.
- **AIGA Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct:**
  <https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-design-business-and-ethics>
  — the industry's formal ethics framework. Students should know it
  exists and what it covers, even if in practice designers rely on
  personal judgment more than formal codes.
- **Mike Monteiro, "Design is a Job":** Mule Design, 2012. The
  "saying no" chapter. Practitioner-voice, opinionated, useful
  precisely because Monteiro takes positions.
- **Costanza-Chock, "Design Justice":** MIT Press, 2020.
  <https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001> — open access.
  Chapter 1 is the most relevant for the exploitation scenario.
- **Brand New (UnderConsideration):** <https://www.underconsideration.com/brandnew/>
  — archive of practitioner commentary on logo and brand work,
  including ethical controversies. Useful for locating current
  scenario material.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- This week carries more emotional weight than most. Some students
  will have personal experience with being asked to do something
  wrong and not knowing how to say no. The opening activation is
  designed to make that experience visible before the scenarios
  arrive. Do not skip it or rush it.
- Scenario 3 (exploitation) is the most contested. Some students
  will argue that the designer's responsibility ends at the brief.
  Others will argue the designer is complicit in any harm the
  product causes. Both positions are live in the industry. Your
  job is not to settle the debate — it's to make sure students can
  articulate the stakes on both sides and choose a defensible
  position rather than defaulting to one unreflectively.
- The Critical Pedagogy lens means you need to name explicitly that
  "professional" is not a neutral standard. This is uncomfortable
  in a business-skills course where students expect practical
  answers. Hold the discomfort. The practical skill IS naming the
  values conflict, not resolving it.
- Do not let the debrief become a general ethics lecture. Pull
  it back to the scenarios: "In Scenario 1, what was the specific
  move? What did you actually say or do?" Abstraction is easier
  than commitment; the learning is in the commitment.
- Timing: the group write (6 min) feels short. It is — the brevity
  prevents overthinking and produces more instinctive responses,
  which are more revealing in the review step. Trust the timing.
- If your class is predominantly from one cultural or professional
  background, the three scenarios may land very differently than
  they would with a diverse group. Be prepared for the room to
  converge on one "obvious" answer for some scenarios — that
  convergence is itself a critical pedagogy moment. Name it.
