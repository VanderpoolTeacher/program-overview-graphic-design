## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Distinguish** between an intake email that moves a project
   forward and one that stalls it — naming at least three structural
   differences.
2. **Compose** a response to a fictional client brief that asks the
   right scoping questions without overwhelming or alienating the
   client.
3. **Evaluate** a brief for ethical and professional risk — and
   articulate, in writing, a professional response to a brief that
   crosses a line.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 2 (primary), CLO 4 (secondary — saying
yes/no professionally)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 3, 6, 8

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–6 | **Opening / activation** | Instructor distributes or projects a brief one-sentence client inquiry: "Hi — I'm looking for someone to help rebrand my company. Are you available?" Ask: "What do you do with this?" 60 seconds of private writing. 3–4 quick popcorn responses. Listen for: who wants to say yes immediately; who asks questions; who notices what's missing. Surface the tension: the instinct to say yes quickly is real and understandable; the cost of under-scoping is also real. (Merrill activation.) |
| 6–20 | **Content delivery** | (a) How to read a brief: what a good brief tells you (budget signals, deliverable scope, timeline, decision-maker); what a vague brief hides (or doesn't know). Demonstrate with a real-ish fictional brief on screen — walk through it aloud, naming what you notice and what's missing. (b) Intake email patterns: what questions to ask, in what order, at what level of detail. The goal of the intake email is not to demonstrate that you're thorough — it's to get enough information to scope and price the project. Three most important questions: (1) What does success look like? (2) Who makes the final decision? (3) What's your timeline and budget range? (c) When to say no: briefly, name the three categories of professional no — values conflict (the brief asks you to do something you won't do), capacity conflict (you can't serve this client well right now), and scope/rate mismatch (the budget doesn't match the ask). Saying no is a skill; it protects the client too. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | Live Intake Triage — see below. |
| 40–47 | **Debrief** | Pull patterns from the activity. "Which brief was hardest to respond to? Why?" Expect: the ethically iffy brief generates the most disagreement — that's exactly right. Name it: "There's no single correct professional response to a values conflict. What we practiced today is the *process* of noticing the conflict, naming it to yourself, and responding in a way that's honest and professional." Abstract conceptualization (Kolb). Ask: "What would you do differently if you got that third brief in real life?" Students name the move — feed-forward (Hattie & Timperley). |
| 47–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Hand off the Mock Intake Email Exchange formative: ~150 words across both messages (initial client email + your reply), due before Week 5. Remind: this feeds directly into summative Part B.1. Preview Week 5: you'll reply to the same brief with a full proposal, budget, and timeline. The intake email is the first move; the proposal is the next. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Live Intake Triage

**Goal:** Practice rapid judgment under realistic time pressure.
A real intake email arrives without warning and requires a response.
Students need the skill of reading a brief quickly, identifying what
they need, and drafting a reply that asks the right questions without
sounding suspicious, demanding, or naive. The three-brief structure
means they practice the normal case, the underspecified case, and the
ethically contested case in a single session.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (5 min per brief × 3, plus 5 min summary)
- Group size: pairs
- Materials: three one-paragraph fictional client brief emails,
  printed or projected. Brief 1: realistic (clear scope, vague
  budget but otherwise coherent). Brief 2: vague (could be many
  things, no budget, no timeline, no deliverable clarity). Brief
  3: ethically iffy (the brief is well-specified but the client
  or the work raises a values question — e.g., a brief for a
  payday-lending marketing campaign, or a brief that asks the
  designer to create content that misrepresents a product).
  Instructor authors these before class; they do not need to be
  extreme — the ethical brief should be genuinely ambiguous, not
  cartoonishly bad.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(1 min)* Distribute the three brief sheets or project them.
   Tell pairs: "You'll work through all three briefs. You have
   2 minutes per brief. Your job: write the first 2–3 sentences of
   your intake email reply. You don't have to finish it — just
   start it, and be ready to say out loud what question you'd ask
   first."
2. *(2 min, Brief 1 — realistic)* Pairs draft. Instructor
   circulates. At the signal, call on one pair: "Read me your
   first sentence." Then: "What's the most important question
   you'd ask?" Expect: most pairs ask about budget or timeline.
   Fine. Note any pair that asks about decision-maker — that's
   worth pointing out.
3. *(2 min, Brief 2 — vague)* Same structure. At the signal:
   "This one is vague. What did you do differently?" Expect:
   some pairs ask everything at once (too many questions), some
   freeze. Name the pattern: a vague brief is not an invitation
   to ask 12 questions. Pick the one question that opens the
   most scope if answered.
4. *(2 min, Brief 3 — ethically iffy)* Same structure, but more
   deliberate. Tell pairs before they start: "This one has
   something in it that might give you pause. Notice what it is
   before you reply." At the signal, ask: "Who noticed the
   thing? What was it?" Let students name it themselves before
   you do. Then: "What would you actually write in reply — do you
   say yes with conditions, ask for more info, or decline? What
   does a professional 'no' look like in 2 sentences?"
5. *(5 min)* Whole-class pattern summary. Instructor draws out:
   "Brief 1 — what did a good reply look like? Brief 2 — what
   was the one question that mattered most? Brief 3 — what did
   people decide, and what split the room?" Let the disagreement
   on Brief 3 stay visible. Name it: "That's the right experience
   to have. This is where your practice statement from Week 3
   becomes load-bearing — your values decide how you reply."

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Brief 1: Students mostly do well. The debrief value is showing
  that the question order matters — budget first, or scope first?
  There's a defensible argument either way.
- Brief 2: Students often write too many questions. This is the
  most common intake error in real practice. Naming it here
  prevents it from appearing in the formative.
- Brief 3: The room will split. Some students will want to say
  yes and ask questions; some will decline immediately; some will
  hedge with conditions. Don't resolve the split — sit in it.
  The learning is noticing that a values conflict requires an
  explicit decision, not just a professional-sounding non-answer.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class:* Have groups of 3 instead of pairs; each person
  takes one brief and the others challenge them. Share-out
  representatives instead of random cold calls.
- *Shorter time:* Drop Brief 2 and run only Brief 1 (normal case)
  and Brief 3 (ethical case). These two together carry most of
  the learning.
- *More challenge:* After the activity, give pairs 2 minutes to
  write a brief for the *other side* — what would a client write
  to you that would make you want to say yes? Reversal is hard and
  builds empathy for the client's communication burden.
- *Remote:* Use a shared Google Doc per pair; each brief is a
  different section. Pairs type their reply; instructor reads them
  in the share-out. Works well; resist the urge to have everyone
  read simultaneously — it gets noisy.

## Connections

- **Builds on:** Week 3 (practice statement). The intake email
  is not generic — it reflects the practice model the student
  is building. A freelance intake and an in-house team intake
  look different. The practice statement from Week 3 is load-bearing
  here: "your values decide how you reply" is not a platitude,
  it's a reference to what students wrote last week.
- **Sets up:** Week 5 (proposals, budgets, timelines). The intake
  conversation produces the scope; the proposal is the formal
  response to that scope. Students will use the fictional brief
  from Week 4's formative as the starting point for their Week 5
  proposal — coordinate the brief prompts so they chain.
- **Pedagogical lens:** Merrill (problem-centered: three real
  fictional briefs, not abstract intake theory; students respond
  to actual emails, not descriptions of emails) + Critical
  Pedagogy / Design Justice (the ethically iffy brief is not
  a gotcha — it's a site where the "just be professional" instruction
  fails, and students need to see that professional practice requires
  values, not just technique). See
  `course-design/pedagogy/merrills-first-principles.md` and
  `course-design/pedagogy/critical-pedagogy-design-justice.md`.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- Author Brief 3 (the ethically iffy one) carefully. It should
  be genuinely ambiguous — not a cartoon villain scenario. A brief
  for a payday-lending rebrand, a pharma company's patient
  communication campaign, a political campaign, or a client who
  asks you to use AI-generated images without disclosing it — all
  of these are real scenarios with real professional disagreement.
  The room splitting is the lesson. If everyone agrees, the brief
  is too easy.
- Students who have had internship experience may have a "real"
  bad brief story. Invite it in the debrief, briefly — real
  experience is more credible than your fictional examples. Keep
  it to one anecdote per debrief so it doesn't become a complaint
  session.
- The timing is tight: 2 minutes per brief is short. Hold it.
  Students can feel rushed, but the pressure is productive — intake
  emails in real practice don't come with unlimited reflection time.
  The speed is pedagogically intentional.
- The chain to Week 5 is important: the fictional brief from this
  week's formative should be the same brief (or a continuation of
  the same project) that the Week 5 proposal responds to. Author
  the briefs together before the semester so they form a coherent
  fictional project arc.
- Energy: this is the first week of Phase 2. Students are shifting
  from "who am I as a practitioner?" to "how do I run a project?"
  That's a gear change. Acknowledge it briefly at the start:
  "Last three weeks we were talking about practice models. Now
  we're in the operational details — how do you actually do the
  work? Same values, different vocabulary."
