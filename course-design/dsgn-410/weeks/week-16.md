## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Present** their practice and one client engagement end-to-end
   in 7 minutes — communicating scope, judgment, and values to an
   audience that includes a practitioner from outside the default
   design canon.
2. **Distinguish** process-level feedback from Self-level praise
   in peer evaluation — using the audience voting criteria as a
   structural guide, not a popularity contest.
3. **Name** one specific next move for their practice after
   graduation — the feed-forward belongs to them, not the
   instructor.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 1, CLO 2, CLO 3, CLO 4, CLO 5, CLO 6
(all CLOs; the summative integrates them all)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~60 minutes):**
- *Submit:* Final Practice Launch Plan document (PDF or shareable
  Notion/Figma link) before class — before you walk in the door.
  Late submission affects assessment; late document means pitching
  work the audience cannot reference.
- *Prepare:* Your 7-minute pitch. Practice it at least twice out
  loud. Know your timing: 5 min present + 1 min Q&A + 1 min
  self-named feedforward. Know which moment of professional
  judgment you'll walk through — don't try to show everything.
- *Plan:* If an invited practitioner is attending, your pitch
  should be legible to someone who does not know your engagement.
  Don't assume shared context.

**Recommended:**
- Record yourself running the pitch once and watch it back. Most
  students discover one of three things: they rush past the
  judgment moment, they under-explain the data viz, or they hedge
  the feed-forward. Fix the one that applies to you.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 | **Opening** | Instructor briefly names the day: "This is the last class. Everyone pitches. The document is submitted. Today is the demonstration." Brief introduction of the invited practitioner (if attending) — name their practice model and why they're here. No lengthy bio; the framing matters more than the credentials. |
| 3–43 | **Live pitches** | 7 min per student, run back-to-back with a 1-min transition. See Interactive activity for the full structure. With 5 students: 40 min. Adjust based on class size (see Variations). |
| 43–48 | **Audience voting + reveal** | After all pitches, reveal the anonymous voting results for the three process-level categories. Instructor names patterns in the votes — what the room collectively saw, not who "won." |
| 48–50 | **Instructor-led debrief** | One paragraph per essential question: "What did this course teach you about making a living from this work?" Instructor names what the room demonstrated, not what it should have demonstrated. Close with the practitioner's one-sentence observation if available — that observation closes the canon-loop. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Final Pitches with Audience Voting

**Goal:** Deliver the summative in live performance conditions, with
structured audience feedback that stays at the process and structural
level. The voting categories are designed so the room names what it
*learned*, not who produced the best work. The invited practitioner
from a non-default practice models — through their presence and
their response — that viable design practice looks different from
the curriculum's default freelancer.

**Setup:**
- Time: 40 minutes (7 min per student × class size; adjust)
- Group size: whole class audience; individual pitches
- Materials: each student's pitch deck (projected from their own
  device or instructor's screen); a visible timer; anonymous voting
  mechanism (paper ballots, Google Form, or Mentimeter with 3
  categories); practitioner guest (strongly recommended — recruit
  a practitioner from a non-default practice model)

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(Before class)* Confirm the room setup: projection, timer,
   voting mechanism. If using digital voting, test it. If using
   paper ballots, distribute them before pitches start. Brief the
   invited practitioner on the three voting categories and the
   process-level framing — they should know "most authentic
   practice" means the pitch that showed real values at stake,
   not the slickest slides.
2. *(1 min before each pitch)* Student connects their device or
   pulls up their deck. Timer starts when the student speaks.
3. *(5 min)* Student presents. Structure: walk the audience through
   one moment of the project package that demonstrates judgment
   — a scope decision, a pricing call, a contract clause they
   fought for, the data visualization, how they handled one of the
   two events. Not a tour of every section; a focused argument
   for one moment of professional judgment.
4. *(1 min)* Process-level Q&A from audience — peers and
   practitioner. One question per asker. Instructor enforces the
   process-level norm: "What was the reasoning behind that
   decision?" not "Did you like working on this?"
5. *(1 min)* Student names their own feed-forward — one specific
   thing they'd refine before launching this practice for real.
   This is theirs to name; no instructor prompt.
6. *(1 min transition)* Audience votes on three categories, using
   a ballot or a digital form. Categories:
   - **Most authentic practice** — the pitch that showed real
     values at stake (a moment where something was risked, a
     choice was defended, a constraint was honored).
   - **Best handled adaptive revision** — the pitch that made
     the change-log reasoning most legible and principled.
   - **Most useful Day-1 portfolio** — the practice package most
     ready to take into a real engagement.
   Students vote for each category; they cannot vote for
   themselves. Votes are anonymous. Revealed after all pitches.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most students will be nervous before their pitch, composed
  during it, and relieved immediately after. The affect cycle is
  predictable and normal. Don't narrate it during the pitches;
  name it afterward in the debrief.
- Some students will try to present everything — a tour of the
  whole document. Hold them to "one moment of judgment." The Q&A
  will surface what the audience needs beyond that.
- The Q&A minute is often the most revealing part of the pitch.
  Students who don't yet own their decisions will hedge under
  questioning. Students who have internalized their practice will
  answer directly. Note the difference; name the pattern in
  the debrief without naming students.
- The voting reveal has energy in the room. Let it. The categories
  are structural — they name *what the room learned*, not *who
  won*. Make this explicit before revealing: "These votes name
  what the room collectively saw. They're not rankings."
- If the invited practitioner is from a non-default practice
  (co-op, community-engaged, in-house at a nonprofit), their
  presence alone disrupts the default canon. Their one-sentence
  observation in the debrief lands differently than anything the
  instructor says. Create space for it — and don't rush it.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class (10+ students):* pitches will exceed 50 min.
  Options: (a) extend to 75–80 min with permission, (b) split
  across two class meetings (Week 15 + Week 16), or (c) reduce
  pitch window to 5 min per student (3 present + 1 Q&A + 1
  feed-forward). Do not cut the feed-forward — it's the most
  important minute.
- *No practitioner available:* the voting still works. The
  canon-disruption moment is lost; compensate by having the
  instructor name a practitioner from outside the default in the
  debrief — by name, by practice model, by one observation from
  their public writing or interview.
- *Remote delivery:* pitches via video call. Voting via Google
  Form or Mentimeter. Recording via Zoom or Loom — each student
  uploads their own recording. The format translates well; the
  energy is different, but the structure holds.

## Resources

- **Summative assignment file:**
  `course-design/dsgn-410/assignments/01-practice-launch-summative.md`
  — the full rubric (7 criteria) drives instructor assessment
  after pitches.
- **Audience voting form:** three-category ballot (paper or digital).
  The three categories are listed above. Prepare in advance;
  do not improvise the categories in the room.
- **Invited practitioner brief:** a one-paragraph note sent to the
  practitioner before class: what the course is, what the students
  have built, what the three voting categories are, and the
  request for a one-sentence closing observation. Keep it short;
  respect their time.
- **Recording setup:** Zoom record, Loom, or a simple screen
  recorder. Test before students arrive. Each student's recording
  should capture their voice and their pitch deck. Upload
  instructions should be in the LMS before class day.
- **Timer (visible):** project it or use a physical countdown
  timer the student can see. The 5-min presentation boundary is
  a professional courtesy to the class; enforce it consistently.

## Connections

- **Builds on:** Weeks 10–15 — all summative milestones, both
  revision events, the Dialogic Pass. Week 16 is the demonstration
  of the whole arc.
- **Sets up:** Nothing within the course — this is the end. But
  the practice statement, lifecycle artifacts, and pitch deck the
  student leaves with are Day-1 tools for the career. The
  feed-forward the student named in this class is the first move
  of that career.
- **Pedagogical lens:** Hattie & Timperley (audience voting at
  the structural level — categories name what the room learned,
  not who performed best; Self-level praise is structurally
  excluded by the category design) + Critical Pedagogy / Design
  Justice (the invited practitioner from a non-default practice
  closes the canon-loop the course opened in Week 1 — the "default
  freelancer" the course questioned is answered by a real working
  designer whose practice looks different).

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The pitches will be uneven. Some will land; some will feel thin;
  one or two will surprise you. Hold the same structure for each —
  5 min / 1 min Q&A / 1 min feed-forward. Resist the impulse to
  extend time for strong pitches or cut short weak ones. The
  structure is the respect.
- The invited practitioner is the highest-leverage element of
  this class. If you can recruit someone from a cooperative,
  a community design organization, a nonprofit in-house team,
  or a design practice run by someone from a historically
  excluded community — do it. Their presence and their 30-second
  observation will do more for the Design Justice thread than
  anything else in the course.
- If no practitioner is available this year, name one in the
  debrief — a real designer, by name, from a non-default practice
  — and read one sentence from their public work. The canon
  disruption still happens; it's just smaller.
- Post-pitch affect is real and varied. Some students will feel
  relieved; some will feel their pitch didn't represent the work
  fairly; a few will feel quietly proud. The debrief should honor
  all three without collapsing into a group hug. *"What did
  this course teach you about making a living from this work?"*
  is the question that holds the room together.
- Fear of failing publicly is at its highest right before the
  pitches begin. Before the first student gets up, acknowledge
  it briefly and directly: *"Everyone in this room has done real
  work over sixteen weeks. The pitch is not a judgment of that
  work — it's a demonstration of it. Different thing."* Then
  start the timer.
- Don't close the course with a verdict. Close it with a question —
  one of the four essential questions, restated. "What does it
  mean to make a living from this work?" Leave it open. That's
  the right ending for a course that teaches students to think,
  not just to execute.
