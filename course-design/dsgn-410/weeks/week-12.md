## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Analyze** how a budget constraint propagates across a proposal,
   contract, and timeline — what changes downstream, what stays.
2. **Evaluate** trade-offs among scope-cutting strategies (kill
   deliverables / extend timeline / reduce quality / renegotiate
   role) and choose one principled response.
3. **Document** the revision in a Change Log v1 that explains *what
   changed, where, and why* — making professional judgment legible.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 2 (project economics under constraint),
CLO 3 (contract amendments), CLO 4 (professional response to client
news), CLO 5 (communicating the change)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 3, 5, 6, 7, 8

> This week activates Bloom's Analyze and Evaluate levels —
> deliberately. The course's CLOs are mostly Apply / Create; the
> events (Weeks 12 + 14) close that gap.

## Pre-class

- *Read:* Mike Monteiro, "Design is a Job" — the chapter on scope

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / the curveball arrives** | Instructor pulls up a fake client email on screen: *"Hi [Designer], thanks for the proposal. Unfortunately our Q1 budget came in 30% lower than projected. I still want to work with you — can you revise the scope to fit?"* — read aloud, sit with the silence for 5 seconds. Then: "You have 50 minutes." |
| 5–15 | **Content delivery** | Brief framing on the four scope-response strategies: (1) cut deliverables, (2) extend timeline, (3) reduce fidelity, (4) renegotiate the designer's role/rate. Walk through one anonymized real example where each was used. *Schön: tacit knowing visible — show the moves a senior designer would actually make.* |
| 15–40 | **Interactive activity** | Live Re-scope Studio — see below. |
| 40–47 | **Debrief** | Three quick patterns from the room: which strategy showed up most? where did students struggle? where did values get tested? *Kolb: abstract conceptualization — pull principles out of the lived experience.* |
| 47–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Change Log v1 due before Week 13 class (½ page documenting the revision). Preview Week 13: lifecycle artifacts (milestone deliverable + invoice + close-out) AND choose your Event 2 scenario from the deck. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Live Re-scope Studio

**Goal:** Practice CLO 4 (professional response under pressure) and
CLO 2 (re-scoping) in a tight time window with peer pressure as the
stand-in for client pressure. The constraint *is* the lesson.

**Setup:**
- Time: 25 minutes (5 min individual / 15 min paired / 5 min share)
- Group size: pairs (different from Week 11 pairs to break echo)
- Materials: students' own laptops with their Week 11 proposals
  open; one printed worksheet with the four strategies as a quick
  reference

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(5 min)* **Solo re-scope.** Each student silently revises their
   proposal to fit the 30% cut. They must commit to one strategy
   (or a hybrid) within 5 minutes. No discussion yet.
2. *(15 min)* **Paired pressure-test.** Students partner up. Each
   partner takes 7 minutes: present your revision, *defend why you
   cut what you cut*, your partner pushes back like a difficult
   client. Then swap. Instructor circulates to surface principled
   responses and gently call out reflexive ones.
3. *(5 min)* **Whip-around.** Each student names in 20 seconds:
   the strategy they chose and the one moment in the pressure-test
   where their partner pushed them somewhere they didn't expect.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most will default to "cut deliverables" because it's the easiest
  to talk about. Push them toward strategies 2–4.
- Some will refuse to compromise quality — that's a legitimate
  values stance; surface it and validate it as a *response style*.
- A few will try to "just say no to the budget cut." Frame this
  honestly: it's an option, but rare. Most working designers face
  this and decide.
- Listen for "I don't know what to ask the client" — this is the
  real gap. Coach them through one example.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *More challenge:* introduce a second constraint mid-pressure-test
  ("the client also added a stakeholder who wants their input") —
  models the cumulative-pressure reality of real client work.
- *Less time:* drop the whip-around to "name your strategy in
  three words" — still surfaces patterns.
- *Larger class:* use triads instead of pairs; the third role is
  "observer" who gives 30-sec feedback at the end of each pair.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The 5-second silence after reading the curveball email is not a
  mistake. It's the most pedagogically loaded moment of the
  semester — students realize this is real (or feels real) and
  shift posture. Don't fill the silence.
- Some students will feel the curveball as failure ("I should have
  scoped lower"). It isn't. Reframe firmly: *every* working
  designer faces this. The course is teaching them to land it.
- Pair carefully. Pair students whose drafts are at different
  scope levels so the conversation has range. Avoid pairing two
  students whose proposals are both at the high or low extreme.
- Watch for the "I'd just refuse the budget cut" stance. Validate
  it briefly, then name what's at stake: *most* working designers
  facing this can't refuse without losing the client and the
  income. Naming the realism is part of the lesson.
- The emotional load on this week is real. Build in 60 seconds at
  the end of class for "name one thing you're proud of in your
  response" — small dose of self-regulation feedback (Hattie &
  Timperley level 3) before they leave.
