## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Evaluate** a peer's intake email for scope clarity — identifying
   whether it asks the right questions and whether the email
   signals professional confidence without overclaiming.
2. **Revise** their own practice statement based on written
   process-level feedback from peers, naming what changed and why.
3. **Apply** the discipline of silent reading + written response
   to give feedback that stays at the process level, not the
   self/praise level.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 1 (practice statement), CLO 2 (intake
email anchors the proposal work beginning)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 3, 6, 8

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** The Intake Read

**Goal:** Practice giving written process-level feedback on a
professional communication artifact (the intake email) — and
receive that feedback on your own. The silent-reading discipline
keeps feedback at the process level by removing the social
softening that happens in spoken crit ("I really liked how you...").

**Setup:**
- Time: 30 minutes (small group circulation + return + brief
  share-out)
- Group size: groups of 3–4 students
- Materials: each student brings their B.1 intake email printed or
  on a shared screen. Three process-level sticky notes or a notes
  template per student (can be digital).

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(2 min)* Arrange students into groups of 3–4. Each student
   places their intake email face-up or opens it on screen.
2. *(3 rotations × 6 min = 18 min)* **Silent circulation.** Each
   student reads a peer's intake email for 90 seconds — silently,
   no talking. Then writes one process-level note on a sticky
   (physical or digital): *"Did this email scope clearly? Did it
   ask the right questions? If not, what's the missing question?"*
   Notes go on or attached to the email. After each rotation,
   students pass to the next peer. Three full rotations so each
   email gets 3 readers.
3. *(5 min)* **Return and read.** Students get back their own email
   with 3 written notes. Give 5 minutes of silent reading — no
   discussion yet. Students annotate their own email with one
   revision move they'll make based on the notes.
4. *(5 min)* **Brief share-out.** One student per group names
   the most useful note they received (process level, not "best
   email in the room"). Instructor listens for patterns across
   groups.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most students default to positive notes first ("this sounds
  great!"). Redirect before the activity starts: *"The note should
  name a specific process thing — did the email scope clearly? Did
  it ask the right questions? That's what we're here for."*
- Some groups will start talking during the silent read. Hold the
  silence. The written note quality goes up significantly when
  students can't verbally soften the feedback first.
- The most common gap in intake emails: the clarifying questions
  are too vague ("what's your timeline?") rather than scoped
  ("will this engagement require print and digital deliverables,
  or digital only?"). Name this in the debrief.
- A few students will be unsettled by 3 notes that all point to
  the same problem. That's the most useful outcome of the activity.
  Normalize it: *"Three people noticing the same gap means the
  gap is real and fixable — not that the email was bad."*

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class:* groups of 4–5; extend the activity to 35 min.
- *Less time:* reduce to 2 rotations (2 readers per email). Still
  effective; loses some triangulation.
- *Remote:* use a shared Google Doc or Figma file; each student
  leaves a comment thread on a peer's email. Same 90-second
  discipline per comment.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- Submission before class is non-negotiable for this week. No draft
  to read = no useful feedback in the rotation. If a student arrives
  without submitting, direct them to sit out the first rotation and
  write their email in class — rough is fine, nothing is not.
- The transition from Week 10's individual 1:1 energy to Week 11's
  group peer-crit energy is real. Some students will still be in
  "consultation mode" — wanting to talk to you. Hold them to the
  peer-pair format. The point of this week is that *peers* surface
  the gaps, not just the instructor.
- The silent read is often the most unsettling moment of the week
  for students. They're used to reading work in conversation, not
  in silence with a judgment to write down. Honor the discomfort —
  it's the productive kind.
- Watch for students who annotate their own email defensively
  ("the reader missed my point"). The purpose of the self-
  annotation step is revision planning, not rebuttal. Redirect
  gently: "If three readers missed the same point, the point needs
  to be on the page, not in your head."
- Brief preview of Week 12 belongs at the end. "The proposal you
  submitted today is the one that gets budget-cut next week" is
  a sentence that changes how students leave the room. Don't
  say it casually — it earns a beat of silence.
