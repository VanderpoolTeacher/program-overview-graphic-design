## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Produce** a professional invoice for a design project that
   includes all standard line items, payment terms, and late-payment
   language.
2. **Compose** a professional follow-up message for a payment that
   is 60 days past due — firm, non-punitive, and relationship-
   preserving.
3. **Describe** the foundational financial hygiene practices for
   self-employed designers: separating business and personal accounts,
   setting aside quarterly estimated taxes, and tracking billable time.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 3 (primary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

- *Read:* Wave "Invoice 101" documentation — the "what's on an
  invoice" overview section (~15 min). Available at
  <https://www.waveapps.com/freelancers/invoice>. (You don't need
  to create an account — just read the feature walkthrough and the
  "getting paid" guidance.)
- *Read:* AIGA, "Running Your Design Business: The Finance Basics"
  — or equivalent practitioner piece on self-employment taxes for
  creatives (~20 min). (A good alternative if AIGA doesn't have a
  current piece: the IRS "Self-Employed Individuals Tax Center"
  intro page is dry but accurate.) The goal: students arrive
  knowing what quarterly estimated taxes are before the content
  delivery.

**Recommended (optional):**
- The Futur, "How to Deal With Late-Paying Clients" — 8-min
  YouTube. Good voice-model for professional tone in late-payment
  conversations.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "Has anyone here been paid late for work they did — design or anything else? What did you do about it?" — quick show of hands and one or two volunteers share briefly. Surfaces the real emotional experience and practical instincts students already carry. Merrill activation: start from lived experience, not theory. |
| 5–20 | **Content delivery** | Two threads, timed equally: (a) **The invoice, line by line** (8 min). Pull up a real (anonymized) invoice in Wave or a PDF. Walk through each element: project name, line items, rates, subtotal, applicable tax, payment terms, late-payment clause, payment methods accepted. Show why "net 30" matters and where it lives in relation to last week's contract. Merrill demonstration: show the real artifact, not a textbook diagram. (b) **The self-employment finance floor** (7 min). Three practices every designer running any kind of independent work needs: (1) a separate business checking account — even a free one — to separate business income from personal spending; (2) quarterly estimated taxes — what they are, roughly what percentage to set aside (25–30% is the common practitioner rule of thumb; instructor should caveat that students consult a tax professional for their specific situation); (3) tracking billable hours — why it matters for both invoicing and rate-setting. Keep this concrete and brief; this is orientation, not accounting class. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | The Late-Payment Conversation — see below. |

Key synthesis: "The goal is to get paid AND keep the client. Those are not in conflict unless you let the relationship deteriorate first." ## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** The Late-Payment Conversation

**Goal:** Students rehearse the professionally awkward skill of
following up on a past-due invoice — in a context where both the
stakes and the relationship are real. Lecture can explain what to
say; a role-play makes you feel what it costs to say it, and what
it costs to wait.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (4 min write / 8 min role-play / 8 min debrief)
- Group size: pairs
- Materials: one index card per student (or a blank slip of paper);
  a printed scenario card (one per pair) with the client context —
  e.g., "You completed a 3-week brand identity project for a small
  restaurant three months ago. Your invoice was $2,400. They paid
  the 50% deposit on time. The remaining $1,200 is 60 days past
  due. You've sent one polite reminder. You need the money."

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(1 min)* Distribute the scenario card to each pair. Read it
   aloud together. "You're the designer. Your partner is the client.
   Designer: you're going to write a two- to three-sentence
   follow-up email or opening line for a phone call. Client: read the
   context card and decide privately why you haven't paid — did you
   forget? are you cash-strapped? do you have a complaint about the
   work? Pick one and don't tell the designer."
2. *(3 min)* **Solo write.** Each designer-role student writes their
   follow-up message on their index card. Instructor circulates
   quietly — don't coach yet.
3. *(8 min)* **Role-play.** Designer delivers their message (reads
   from card or improvises). Client responds in character — they may
   be apologetic, defensive, evasive, or honest about cash flow.
   Designer must respond in real time. After 4 minutes, swap roles:
   the client is now the designer (with a different invoice scenario
   on the back of the card), the original designer becomes the client.
4. *(5 min, debrief part 1)* Pairs stay together for 60 seconds:
   "What did the designer do well? What made you, as the client,
   more or less likely to pay?" Then open to room: quick popcorn
   on what worked and what didn't.
5. *(3 min, debrief part 2)* Instructor names 2–3 patterns heard
   across the room. Usual patterns: messages that name the specific
   invoice number and amount are more professional than vague ones;
   a clear deadline ("by end of Friday") performs better than "when
   you get a chance"; offering a payment plan is almost always better
   received than threatening legal action.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most designers will write messages that are too apologetic — hedged
  to the point of not actually asking for payment. This is the most
  common pattern and worth naming directly: politeness that avoids
  the ask doesn't get you paid.
- Some will write messages that are too aggressive or invoke lawyers
  immediately. Gently reframe: legal escalation is a last resort and
  burns the relationship; the professional goal is to keep both the
  payment *and* the client.
- Clients playing the "I have a complaint" role will surface a real
  dynamic: the client may withhold payment not for financial reasons
  but because they're unhappy with the work. This is actually useful
  — students need to know what to do when a dispute is mixed up with
  a payment. Don't cut this short; let the complexity land.
- Some students will freeze in the improvisational response phase.
  That's the lesson: you can't script your way through a real
  conversation. Coach them with a prompt: "What's the next question
  you'd want answered?"

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Smaller class (under 8):* do this as a fishbowl — one pair role-
  plays in the center while everyone else observes and notes what
  they'd do differently. Debrief the whole class together.
- *Larger class (over 16):* add a third role — an "observer" who
  gives 30 seconds of process-level feedback after the exchange
  before roles rotate.
- *More challenge:* mid-way through the role-play, instructor slides
  in a complication (whispers to the client: "you also realized this
  week that you might want to hire this designer again"). How does
  that change both parties' behavior?

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The emotional charge of the role-play catches students off guard
  in a useful way. Even a fictional late-payment conversation
  surfaces real discomfort about money, power, and professional
  identity. Don't talk students out of that discomfort — name it
  and use it.
- Some students will have been in the client role in real life (as
  freelancers who've had to chase payment OR as clients who've
  delayed payment themselves). Both experiences are pedagogically
  valid; make space for both without turning this into a grievance
  session.
- The finance basics segment (quarterly taxes, separate accounts)
  will be entirely new to many students. Do not overwhelm them with
  numbers. The goal is orientation: "these things exist, they
  matter, here's what to Google when you need the details." A
  specific accountant or financial advisor referral in the syllabus
  resource list is worth having.
- Timing: 8 minutes for the role-play feels short. It is. The
  brevity creates productive pressure. Resist the impulse to extend
  it — the debrief is where the learning consolidates, and it needs
  the full 8 minutes.
- Pair variety matters: if you know which students have done any
  independent design work, try to spread them across pairs so that
  each pair has at least one person with some real-world reference
  for the money conversation.
