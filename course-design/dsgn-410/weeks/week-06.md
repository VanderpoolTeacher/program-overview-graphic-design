## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Identify** what a freelance contract clause is protecting, whose
   interest it serves, and what the risk would be without it.
2. **Draft** a contract excerpt for their fictional client project
   that covers scope, IP ownership, kill fee, and payment schedule.
3. **Evaluate** the power dynamics embedded in a standard freelance
   contract — who can afford to negotiate it, and who cannot.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 3 (primary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~45 minutes):**
- *Read:* AIGA Standard Form of Agreement for Design Services —
  skim the full document (20 min), then read sections 2 (scope), 5
  (payment), 9 (IP/work for hire), and 14 (termination / kill fee)
  closely. Available at
  <https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-standard-form-of-agreement-for-design-services>.
- *Read:* Mike Monteiro, "Design is a Job" — the chapter on
  contracts (roughly 15 min). Frame: contracts aren't about distrust;
  they're about shared expectations.

**Recommended (optional):**
- The Futur, "Understanding Design Contracts" — short YouTube segment
  (~10 min). Useful if the AIGA language feels dense.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "Before you read the AIGA contract: did you know that freelance contracts existed? Did you ever read one?" — quick show of hands + 30-second pair-share. Surfaces what students actually know (and fear). Merrill activation: prior experience is the raw material. |
| 5–20 | **Content delivery** | Walk through the four contract "fights" using the anonymized real contract on screen (not a template — a real one). (a) **Scope fight:** the clause that defines what you're delivering and what "done" means — protects against scope creep. (b) **IP fight:** work-for-hire vs. license vs. retained rights to process work — who owns what, and from when. (c) **Kill fee fight:** what you get paid if the client cancels — and why most clients hate this clause. (d) **Payment fight:** milestone-based vs. net-30 vs. on-delivery — and why late payment starts here, not in Week 7. For each clause: read the actual language aloud, name the fight it's having, say what would happen without it. Schön: tacit knowing visible — show the fights behind the clauses. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | Contract Clause Annotation — see below. |
| 40–48 | **Debrief** | Pull patterns from the annotations: Which clauses generated the most disagreement about whose interest they serve? Which surprised students most? Surface the power asymmetry question directly: "If you couldn't afford a lawyer to negotiate this, what would you do? What would the client do?" — Critical Pedagogy lens, named explicitly. Kolb: abstract conceptualization from the lived annotation experience. |
| 48–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Hand off the formative deliverable: contract excerpt draft due before Week 7 class. Preview Week 7: invoicing, late payment, and finance basics. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Contract Clause Annotation

**Goal:** Students experience the interpretive work a real contract
requires — not reading rules, but reading *fights* — and surface the
power dynamics that standard contract language encodes. Lecture can
name those fights; pairs have to find them themselves.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (8 min annotation / 10 min share-out / 2 min
  instructor synthesis)
- Group size: pairs
- Materials: one printed page (or shared PDF) of 4–6 clauses from
  the anonymized real freelance contract — not the AIGA template, a
  real one. Each clause gets a blank annotation box with three prompts
  printed below it: (1) "What is this clause protecting?" (2) "Whose
  interest does it serve?" (3) "What would happen without it?" The
  page is the same for every pair; different pairs are assigned
  different clauses (two pairs per clause in a 12-person class, one
  pair per clause in a smaller class).

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(1 min)* Distribute the annotation sheet. Assign each pair a
   specific clause: "Pair 1, you have the scope definition. Pair 2,
   you have the IP clause. Pair 3, kill fee. Pair 4, payment
   schedule. Pair 5, dispute resolution." (Adjust for class size —
   some pairs share a clause; that's fine.)
2. *(8 min)* Pairs read their assigned clause and write responses to
   the three prompts. Instructor circulates. Key coaching moves: if a
   pair says "this protects the designer," push them: "Does it protect
   both? Who had the leverage to put this language in?" If a pair is
   stuck, read the clause aloud with them and ask: "What's the scenario
   this clause was written to prevent?"
3. *(2 min, transition)* "We'll go clause by clause. Each pair gets
   90 seconds. Tell us what you found — and flag if you disagreed
   with each other."
4. *(8 min)* Share-out by clause. Instructor listens for: correct
   identification of the protected interest, missed asymmetries,
   moments where pairs disagreed. Name disagreements — don't resolve
   all of them. "Pair 3 and Pair 4 had different readings of whose
   interest the kill fee serves. Hold that — it's real. Both readings
   exist in the industry."
5. *(2 min)* Instructor synthesis: "What you just did is what a
   lawyer does for $300/hour. Knowing what fight each clause is having
   is the skill. You can't negotiate what you can't name."

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most pairs will correctly identify the "protected party" but miss
  the asymmetry — e.g., "the scope clause protects both parties"
  without noting that the *client* typically has more leverage to
  define scope in the first place. Surface this.
- The IP clause typically generates the most productive confusion.
  Students often don't know what "work for hire" means or that
  process sketches might not be covered. Spend time here.
- Some students will be alarmed by the kill fee clause ("clients
  have to pay even if they cancel?"). This is the lesson: yes, and
  most clients push back on it. That pushback is itself a fight.
- A few will want to dive into legalese — keep pulling them back
  to the three prompts. The language is less important than the
  interest it encodes.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Smaller class (under 8):* each pair gets two clauses; annotation
  time stays at 8 minutes.
- *Larger class (over 16):* form groups of 3; add a fourth prompt:
  "What would a designer with less leverage have to give up here?"
  This sharpens the power-asymmetry discussion.
- *More challenge:* add one clause that is genuinely ambiguous or
  poorly written (real contracts have these). Ask: "Is this clause
  enforceable? What's missing?" Raises Analyze-level thinking.

## Resources

- **Anonymized real freelance contract (Resource 1):** instructor-
  provided in LMS. Must be a real contract — not the AIGA template —
  with client-identifying information removed. 4–6 pages minimum.
  Refresh every 3 years. Ideally, have two versions: one from a
  solo freelancer negotiating with a small business, one from a
  studio negotiating with a mid-size client.
- **AIGA Standard Form of Agreement for Design Services:**
  <https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-standard-form-of-agreement-for-design-services>
  — the industry baseline. Students should know this exists and what
  it covers, even if they draft their own.
- **Mike Monteiro, "Design is a Job":** Mule Design, 2012. Available
  from A Book Apart. The contracts chapter is the canonical
  practitioner-voice resource on why contracts exist.
- **Annotation worksheet:** one-pager with the 4–6 clause excerpts
  and the three annotation prompts. Maintained as a course-LMS file.
  Instructor prepares this by selecting representative clauses from
  the anonymized real contract.

## Connections

- **Builds on:** Week 5 (proposal + budget + timeline) — the contract
  codifies what the proposal established. Students are drafting a
  contract for the same fictional client engagement.
- **Sets up:** Week 7 (invoicing + finance) — the payment schedule
  clause in this week's contract is the precursor to the invoice
  exercise. Week 12 (Event 1: budget cut) — students will need to
  know which contract clauses address scope changes and kill fees
  when the budget cut arrives.
- **Pedagogical lens:** Schön (tacit knowing visible — the "fights"
  behind each clause are the tacit professional knowledge that contract
  language obscures; making them visible is the instructional move) +
  Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice (whose interests does the standard
  contract serve? who has the leverage to negotiate, and who is taking
  the standard terms because they have no choice? — surfaces power
  asymmetry that pure contract-law instruction ignores).

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The anonymized real contract is the most important resource you
  bring to this week. The AIGA template is clean and educational,
  but it doesn't show the real fights. A real contract has weird
  carve-outs, hedged language, and at least one clause someone's
  lawyer put in after a bad experience. That's where the learning is.
- Do not frame the contract as "protecting yourself from bad clients."
  That framing turns contracts into adversarial documents and misses
  the point. The better frame: a contract is a shared expectations
  document that both parties can return to when something unexpected
  happens. The adversarial framing is real in some markets, but it's
  not the universal starting point.
- Budget extra time for the IP clause. Students who have done any
  freelance work before often have strong (and sometimes wrong)
  opinions about work-for-hire. Surface those opinions as part of
  the annotation; don't pre-empt them.
- The power-asymmetry question at debrief ("who can afford a
  lawyer?") will land differently depending on the room. Some
  students will have family members who've been on the losing end of
  a contract they didn't understand. Make space for that. Don't rush
  past the moment.
- Timing: the share-out in the activity tends to run over. Hold pairs
  to 90 seconds each; the lesson is the patterns across clauses, not
  the depth of any one annotation. If you run short, the IP and kill
  fee clauses are higher priority than dispute resolution.
