## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Analyze** three case-study designers to extract business model,
   client type, value proposition, and ethical posture from each.
2. **Compare** at least two non-default practice models (cooperative,
   in-house at nonprofit, BIPOC-owned studio, queer collective, etc.)
   against the conventional freelance model on concrete criteria:
   income source, client relationship, and definition of
   "professional."
3. **Identify** one aspect of a non-default designer's career pathway
   that shifts their own thinking about what's possible.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 1 (primary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 6, 8

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "Last week you placed yourself on the practice model map. Did anything from the pre-class readings shift that placement, even slightly?" 2–3 brief popcorn responses. No pressure; this surfaces whether the readings landed (Merrill activation, Kolb reflective observation). |
| 5–20 | **Content delivery** | Walk through three case-study designers — one per model type. Present each as a mini-dossier: who are they, how do they make money, who are their clients, what differentiates them, what does "professional" mean in their context. At least one must be a non-default practice. Suggested framing for each case: (a) Freelance designer with a niche vertical — how do they find clients, set rates, sustain themselves; (b) In-house designer at a nonprofit or community-based org — salary structure, internal client vs. external, what "brief" looks like when the brief is a mission; (c) Designer-owner in a co-op or BIPOC-led collective — cooperative ownership model, how decisions are made, how rates and credit are distributed. Schön: make tacit knowing visible — don't just show the designer's work, name the *business decisions* behind it. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | Case-Study Annotation — see below. |

Ask: "What did you see in the business model column that surprised you?" and "What did 'ethical posture' mean in each case — did that category feel easy or hard to fill in?" Pull out the pattern: designers with non-default models often have more explicitly articulated values, because the market doesn't hand them their clients. Abstract conceptualization (Kolb). |

Remind: at least one of the three designers they profile must operate outside the default freelance model. ## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Case-Study Annotation

**Goal:** Force students to read a designer's public-facing profile
analytically, not just admiringly. Lecture can name the categories;
annotation makes students apply them to real (or realistic) text.
The pair structure ensures two sets of eyes on contested calls like
"ethical posture" — which students often skip unless prompted.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (8 min annotation, 12 min share-out)
- Group size: pairs
- Materials: one one-page designer profile per pair. Use the same
  profile for the whole class, or distribute 2–3 different profiles
  and debrief across them. The profile should be a real
  (or realistically constructed) public bio + one-paragraph
  practice description. Instructor prepares these in advance;
  one of them must represent a non-default practice model.
  Annotate-able fields to highlight on the sheet:
  (1) How money enters the practice; (2) Who the clients are;
  (3) What the value proposition is (why would a client pick
  this designer); (4) What ethical commitments are stated or implied.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(2 min)* Distribute the profile. Tell pairs: "You have 8 minutes
   to annotate this. I've put four boxes in the margin. Fill them in.
   If you don't know — if the profile doesn't say — write 'not
   stated' and note what that absence tells you."
2. *(8 min)* Pairs work. Instructor circulates. Watch for:
   - Pairs who fill in every box confidently — probe them: "How do
     you know that? Is that inference or stated?"
   - Pairs stuck on "ethical posture" — coach: "Does this designer
     talk about who they won't work with? What causes they support?
     Even a silence is data."
3. *(6 min)* Whole-class share-out. Instructor writes the four
   category labels on the board. Call on different pairs for each.
   Where annotations diverge, linger: "One pair says 'nonprofit
   clients,' another says 'community organizations' — is that the
   same thing? Does it matter?"
4. *(4 min, with non-default profile)* If using multiple profiles,
   compare the default and non-default profiles on the same four
   categories. Ask: "Which boxes were harder to fill in for the
   non-default practice? What does that tell us about whose
   practice assumptions our categories are built for?"

**Anticipated student responses:**
- "Business model" is the easiest box for most students; "ethical
  posture" is hardest. That's the lesson. Spend the extra time there.
- Students often read "ethical posture" as charity work or activism.
  Redirect: professional ethics includes pricing fairly, giving
  credit, not taking work that conflicts with your values — all
  things any designer navigates.
- For the non-default profile: students may not recognize the
  cooperative model's ownership structure. Brief explanation before
  the annotation helps — or let confusion surface in the annotation
  and address it in the debrief.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class:* Assign different profiles to different pairs;
  each pair annotates a different designer. Share-out becomes a
  "gallery of practice models" — richer variety, but takes slightly
  longer (build in 2 extra minutes).
- *Shorter time:* Drop to 2 boxes (business model + value
  proposition) and give each pair 5 minutes. Full version is better
  though; don't sacrifice "ethical posture" — it's the seed of
  CLO 4 later in the course.
- *Remote:* Use a shared Miro board with the profile image in
  the center and four sticky-note zones in the corners. Pairs
  collaborate on the same board region.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- Finding good non-default designer profiles takes advance work.
  The AIGA spotlight, Dribbble, and design-justice adjacent
  newsletters (e.g., Eye on Design) are useful. Don't use a
  tokenized "diversity example" — find a designer whose practice
  is intellectually interesting on its own terms and whose
  non-default structure becomes visible on inspection.
- The annotation activity works best when the profile you choose
  *doesn't* announce its ethical commitments loudly. A designer
  who just says "I work with nonprofits" is less useful than one
  whose work history implies a set of choices the students have to
  infer. The inferential work is the skill.
- Hattie & Timperley note (see `course-design/pedagogy/hattie-timperley-feedback.md`):
  the Career Profile formative is a good opportunity for process-level
  peer feedback. Consider building a brief peer-response round into
  the Week 3 opening rather than grading it cold — students benefit
  from seeing how others profiled the same non-default model question.
- Don't try to adjudicate "which practice model is best." Your job
  is to make the trade-offs legible, not to rank them. Students who
  hear you editorialize will shape their practice statement to match
  your preference rather than their own reasoning.
