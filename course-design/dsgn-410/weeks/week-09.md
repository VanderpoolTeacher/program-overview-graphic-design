## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Construct** a 3-slide pitch sketch that communicates the core
   business case for their imagined practice — including at least
   one data visualization.
2. **Apply** AI tools to draft business communication (a client
   follow-up email) and identify at least one factual or contextual
   error in the AI output before sending.
3. **Describe** the disclosure habit required for using AI in client-
   facing professional communication — what to check, what to
   correct, and what to log.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 5 (primary), CLO 6 (primary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 2, 5, 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~50 minutes):**
- *Read:* Edward Tufte, "The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint" — or,
  if access is an issue, Alberto Cairo's "The Truthful Art" Chapter 1
  (available through most library systems) (~20 min). Frame: data
  visualization is a design problem, not a numbers problem.
- *Watch:* The Futur (Chris Do), "How to Pitch Your Design Studio" —
  10-min YouTube clip. (~10 min.) Not a template to copy — a model
  to react to.
- *Try:* Use any AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, or similar) to
  draft a follow-up email to an imagined client. Any scenario. Just
  get the output on screen. Notice what the AI does well and what
  feels off. Come ready to talk about it. (~20 min.)

**Recommended (optional):**
- Skim the AIGA "How to Write a Design Proposal" guide or any short
  primer on business decks in the creative industries — reinforces
  the connection between pitch and proposal.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "You tried the AI email draft before class. What was one thing it got wrong — even a small thing?" Quick popcorn: 4–5 students share. No wrong answers. This activates the skeptical habit before the content delivery names it, and surfaces real examples to work with in Part B of the activity. Merrill: activation from the students' own experience. |
| 5–20 | **Content delivery** | Two mini-lectures, 7–8 minutes each. (a) **Data viz primer.** Not a software tutorial — a principles primer. Show two versions of the same business fact: one as a bullet point, one as a simple chart or graphic. Ask: "Which one moves a stakeholder?" Four principles in 5 minutes: show the comparison, show the proportion, show the change, label for the person who won't ask questions. Then: what makes data visualization a graphic-design problem (not just Excel): hierarchy, emphasis, color meaning, spatial logic. Demo one real example from a design studio's pitch deck or annual report. Merrill demonstration: show the real thing. (b) **AI as ops tool — different framing.** In other DSGN courses, AI may appear as a creative generative tool. Here, the frame is different: AI is operational infrastructure — for drafting emails, estimating, researching, templating. That difference matters for disclosure. Show a real AI-generated business email alongside a human-written one. What does the AI email get right (structure, tone, length)? What does it get wrong (specifics, relationship context, factual claims)? Surface the disclosure habit: every AI-generated draft that goes to a client should be verified for factual accuracy and contextual fit before sending, and the AI use should be documented in the designer's workflow. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | Two-Part Studio — see below. |
| 40–48 | **Debrief** | Pull from both parts. Part A: "What did you learn about data visualization from having to produce one in 5 minutes?" Usual lesson: constraints reveal what you actually know how to show. Name 2–3 strong examples from the room. Part B: "What did the AI get wrong?" Compile a short list on the board — this is the verification list students will need for the summative. Close with the disclosure question: "What's the minimum you'd log to document your AI use responsibly?" ## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Two-Part Studio

**Goal:** Part A builds the data visualization and pitch instinct
through constraint; Part B builds the AI verification habit through
a live exercise that generates real examples of AI error. Together
they establish the practical skills that CLOs 5 and 6 require in
the summative.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (5 min Part A / 3 min share / 8 min Part B /
  4 min pairs debrief)
- Group size: individual for Part A; pairs for Part B
- Materials: Part A — any tool available (whiteboard section,
  paper, a slide app — student's choice); one "data prompt" slip
  (one per student). Part B — laptops with access to any AI tool;
  one "client scenario" card per pair.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(1 min setup)* Distribute the data-prompt slips. Each slip has
   one fact about a fictional design practice: "Your studio completed
   14 projects last year. 8 were brand identity. 4 were print. 2 were
   environmental." Or: "Your freelance rate is $95/hr. The market
   average in your region is $75/hr. The high end is $130/hr." (Vary
   the prompts so students are working with different facts.) "You
   have 5 minutes. Visualize this one fact in whatever tool you have
   in front of you. Paper counts. Your goal: a stakeholder who doesn't
   read the caption should understand the point."
2. *(5 min)* **Part A — Data-viz challenge.** Students work
   individually. Instructor circulates quietly — note 2–3 strong
   examples and 1–2 common failure modes (usually: trying to show too
   much, or visualizing the numbers without showing a comparison or
   proportion that makes the numbers meaningful).
3. *(3 min)* **Quick share.** 3–4 students hold up or screen-share
   their visualization. Instructor narrates what works at the
   process level: "This one shows proportion clearly because it
   uses the part-whole relationship. This one has a comparison built
   in. This one has too many variables — pick one." No grades, no
   "this is bad" — process-level only.
4. *(1 min transition)* Form pairs. Distribute the client scenario
   cards. Each card reads: "You completed a brand refresh project for
   a local coffee shop six months ago. The project went well. You
   haven't been in touch since. Draft a professional follow-up email
   checking in and opening the door to future work. Use AI to draft
   it."
5. *(8 min)* **Part B — AI Verification Round.** Each pair uses one
   AI tool to generate a follow-up email draft. Then they must find
   ONE thing in that draft that is wrong, vague, or would embarrass
   them if sent to a real client without editing. Wrong: a fabricated
   detail, an incorrect assumption about the relationship, a misused
   industry term. Vague: a line so generic it could be from anyone to
   anyone. Embarrassing: tone that doesn't match the relationship,
   overpromising, or language that reads as AI-generated to a
   discerning reader. They mark it on the printout or screen.
6. *(4 min)* **Pairs debrief.** Each pair reports in 30 seconds:
   what AI did well, what they found wrong, and what they'd have
   had to fix before sending. Instructor captures examples on the
   board in two columns: "AI does well" and "always verify." This
   is the beginning of the disclosure log concept students will
   formalize in the summative.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Part A: most students will default to bar charts or bullet lists
  with numbers. Both are fine starting points. The coaching move is
  to ask: "What's the one thing you want a stakeholder to take away?
  Is that visible in 3 seconds?" That question drives revision.
- Some students will be paralyzed by tool choice. Reassure: paper
  is fine. The point is the conceptual move, not the production
  value.
- Part B: most AI tools will generate structurally solid emails —
  correct greeting, logical structure, professional tone. The errors
  will usually be in specificity: the AI won't know the client's
  name, the actual project details, or the real relationship context.
  Students sometimes struggle to find an error because the email
  "sounds right." Coach them: "Would you actually send this exactly
  as written to a real client you worked with for three weeks? What's
  missing?"
- A few students will argue that the AI email is fine and just needs
  personalization. That is the lesson: personalization IS verification
  for relationship-based communication. Name it.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Smaller class (under 8):* run Part A as a whole-class exercise
  with the same data prompt on a shared whiteboard. Each person
  adds their approach; debrief the comparison.
- *Larger class (over 16):* for Part B, have pairs swap their AI
  drafts with another pair. Each pair verifies the *other pair's*
  draft — adds a layer of external perspective and reduces the
  tendency to rationalize your own AI output.
- *More challenge:* after Part B, ask pairs to make the correction
  and write a one-line "verification note" as if logging it in a
  real AI workflow document. This scaffolds the summative Part D
  directly.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- Part A and Part B feel like two different classes jammed together.
  They are — deliberately. The theme connecting them is "business
  communication that works": data visualization is communication at
  the information level; AI verification is communication at the
  accuracy level. Name the connection in the debrief.
- The 5-minute constraint in Part A is tight enough to prevent
  overthinking. Students who try to build a polished chart in 5
  minutes will run out of time; students who sketch quickly will
  get more feedback. You can briefly coach the first group toward
  the second by watching who's still on fonts at minute 2 and
  saying "the concept, not the production — move."
- For Part B, the most common mistake is letting pairs spend too
  long arguing about whether the AI email is "good enough." It
  doesn't matter for the exercise. The task is to find one thing
  wrong, not to determine whether the email is sendable. Hold them
  to the task.
- The AI framing in this course ("ops tool, not creative tool")
  may conflict with what students have heard in other DSGN courses
  where AI appears as a generative creative aid. Acknowledge the
  difference explicitly: "In DSGN 310 or 320, you may have explored
  AI as a creative partner. Here, we're looking at it as an
  operations tool — for tasks like drafting emails, templating,
  researching rates. The disclosure norms and the verification
  habits are different in each context."
- The instructor-provided exemplar pitch deck is as important here
  as the anonymized contract was in Week 6. A real deck — with real
  decisions, imperfect slides, and embedded strategy — teaches more
  than any template. If possible, provide two: one from a high-
  volume commercial freelancer and one from a studio doing community
  or nonprofit work. The comparison surfaces the question of whose
  pitch language gets treated as default.
- The debrief's "AI does well / always verify" list on the board
  is worth photographing and sharing in the LMS. Students will
  reference it when writing their summative Part D workflow document.
