## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Articulate** a specific practice model (structure, target clients,
   core services, and rate logic) in 2–3 sentences under pressure
   from a skeptical peer.
2. **Revise** at least one claim in their practice pitch after
   receiving a "who's your client?" challenge — demonstrating
   real-time reframing.
3. **Draft** a one-page practice statement that names trade-offs
   explicitly, not just aspirations.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 1 (primary), CLO 4 (secondary — values
articulation and trade-off thinking)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~50 minutes):**
- *Re-read:* Your own Career Profile formative (Week 2 deliverable).
  Spend 10 minutes re-reading it and asking: "What do these three
  designers have that I want? What do they have that I don't want?"
  Write 2–3 sentences in response before class.
- *Read:* AIGA, "How to Set Your Design Rates" (available in the
  AIGA Resources section at aiga.org — look for the current
  freelance rate guidance or pricing primer, ~20 min). Focus on
  the logic of rate-setting, not the specific numbers.
- *Watch:* The Futur (Chris Do), "How to Find Your Niche as a
  Designer" — 10-min YouTube clip. Available on The Futur channel;
  verify URL before semester. The question is: what's the principle,
  not the tactical advice?

**Recommended (optional):**
- Write a first-draft sentence: "I want to practice as a [model
  type] who works with [client type] to deliver [core services] at
  [rough rate logic]." Bring it — even as a mess. The activity
  works better if you have something to defend.

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–7 | **Opening / activation** | Pairs share their 2–3 sentence Career Profile reflection (written pre-class). Partner names one thing they heard: "You said you want X from that designer's model." 3 minutes pair, 4 minutes brief whole-class report. Goal: get the Week 2 thinking back in the room before we build on it. (Merrill activation; Kolb reflective observation.) |
| 7–20 | **Content delivery** | Brief lecture: the three decisions every practice model must make. (1) Positioning — who are your clients, and what problem do you solve for them that someone else doesn't? (2) Services + rates — what do you sell, and how do you price it (hourly vs. project vs. retainer)? (3) Trade-offs — every model choice forecloses something. Freelance: freedom, but you carry all the client-acquisition weight. Agency: collaboration and scale, but less creative control. In-house: stability, but one client, one aesthetic direction. Co-op: shared ownership, but governance overhead. Name your trade-off and own it — that's the difference between a practice statement and a wish list. Demonstrate by showing an instructor-authored example practice statement (a sample, not the instructor's real one) and pointing to: where is the positioning? where is the rate logic? where is the trade-off named? This is Schön's "tacit knowing made visible" — students see how these moves sit inside a written statement before writing their own. |
| 20–40 | **Interactive activity** | Practice Model Speed-Dating — see below. |
| 40–47 | **Debrief** | Whole class. "Who revised their pitch between round 1 and round 3? What changed?" Listen for: students who sharpened their client type (from "small businesses" to "small food businesses in the Pacific Northwest" — real positioning shift), students who named a trade-off for the first time, students who realized their model is actually hybrid. Name the pattern: "Each time you defended it to a new person, you found the soft spot. That's reflection-in-action — you reframed while speaking." Abstract conceptualization (Kolb). |
| 47–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Hand off the Practice Statement formative: one-page draft due before Week 4. Remind them: this feeds directly into summative Part A. Preview Week 4: we move into operations — how to read a client brief and run an intake conversation. Bring a real brief if you have one from your internship. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Practice Model Speed-Dating

**Goal:** Force multiple rapid reframings. Three rounds of 2-minute
pitches with different partners means students hear the same
challenge ("who's your client?") from three different people and
must adjust. The iteration is the learning — not the pitch itself.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (3 rounds × 5 min each, plus setup and
  transition)
- Group size: pairs, rotating (3 rounds with 3 different partners)
- Materials: optional printed cue card with 3 challenge questions
  per listener: (1) "Who specifically is your client?" (2) "Why
  would they pick you over someone else?" (3) "What are you giving
  up with this model?" Instructor hands these out or projects them.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(2 min setup)* Explain the structure: "You'll pitch your
   practice model to 3 different partners in 3 rounds. Each round
   is 5 minutes: 2 minutes to pitch, 2 minutes of pushback from
   the listener, 1 minute for you to revise out loud — say 'if I
   revised my pitch based on that, it would now say...' Then we
   rotate." Put the three challenge questions on screen.
2. *(5 min, Round 1)* Pairs form. Pitches begin. Instructor
   circulates and listens for: pitches that are all aspiration with
   no client specificity; pitches that have a real trade-off named
   vs. ones that avoid it. Jot 1–2 examples of each to use in
   the debrief — don't name names, just patterns.
3. *(5 min, Round 2)* Rotate. One row of students moves one seat
   left (or equivalent in a non-row setup). Repeat. Remind
   students: you're allowed to use what you learned in Round 1.
   In fact, please do.
4. *(5 min, Round 3)* Rotate again. Third partner. Tell students:
   "This time, in your final revision minute, say what would be in
   a written version of your practice statement — not just the
   pitch."
5. *(3 min, whole-class)* "Who revised their client definition
   between round 1 and 3? Raise your hand." Follow with a
   volunteer explaining what changed. Keep this brief — the debrief
   segment (outside the activity) is where the abstraction happens.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most students start with an overly broad client ("people who need
  design work") and get pushed toward specificity by the listener's
  challenge. This is the most common and most productive trajectory.
- Some students have a strongly formed idea from a previous
  internship or client experience and resist revision. That's fine;
  the challenge is still useful — even "I tested this and it held
  up" is a reflection-in-action result.
- Students who feel uncertain about their practice direction may
  experience the rounds as stressful. Acknowledge at the start:
  "You are not being evaluated on having a finished answer. You are
  being evaluated on whether your answer got sharper across three
  rounds. That's the entire skill."

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Smaller class (under 8):* Do two rounds instead of three; whole
  class shares during the second-round debrief rather than rotating
  a third time.
- *Larger class (20+):* Rotate in groups (two concentric circles,
  inner circle pitches, outer circle challenges, inner circle
  rotates). Works well, slightly more logistics.
- *Less challenge needed:* Drop the "what are you giving up?"
  question and focus only on client specificity and value
  proposition. Reduces stress; slightly weaker trade-off thinking.
- *More challenge:* Add a fourth round where the partner takes
  the opposing position: "I'm a client who has heard your pitch
  and I'm NOT convinced. Convince me." Generates much sharper
  iterations.

## Connections

- **Builds on:** Week 1 (practice model positioning) and Week 2
  (career profiles). Students are now applying the vocabulary from
  Week 1 and the research from Week 2 to their own situation. The
  three practice-statement moves (positioning / rates / trade-offs)
  are a direct synthesis of what the case studies showed.
- **Sets up:** Summative Part A — the practice statement formative
  is the first draft. Week 10 (summative kickoff) is where Part A
  gets polished; this draft is the raw material. Week 4 also builds
  directly on this: you can't write an intake email without knowing
  what practice you're running.
- **Pedagogical lens:** Schön (reflection-in-action — students
  reframe their practice in real time as they defend it across three
  speed-dating rounds; tacit knowing visible in the sample practice
  statement demonstration) + Hattie & Timperley (peer feedback at
  process level — the listener's challenge questions are structured
  at process, not task or self, targeting the *method* of
  positioning, not "I like your idea"). See
  `course-design/pedagogy/schon-reflective-practitioner.md` and
  `course-design/pedagogy/hattie-timperley-feedback.md`.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The sample practice statement you demo in content delivery should
  be authored by you, not pulled from the internet. Generic examples
  land weakly. If you can share a lightly anonymized version of your
  own practice statement — or one from a recent alum who has
  consented — students engage much more deeply. The messier and more
  honestly trade-off-aware it is, the better.
- Speed-dating logistics: if the room has fixed seating, use the
  row rotation (inner/outer ring or odd/even rows). If seats move,
  "stand up and find someone you haven't talked to" works. Don't
  let students pick their own partners — the random partner is part
  of the design.
- The debrief question "who revised their pitch between round 1 and
  3?" almost always generates hands. If it doesn't, ask instead:
  "Who got a pushback you didn't expect?" That usually opens the
  same conversation. The goal is naming what changed and why, not
  counting revisions.
- Time pressure is real here. The content delivery is 13 minutes —
  resist the urge to expand it. Students need the activity time more
  than they need more lecture. If you have to cut something, cut a
  minute from the opening activation, not from the activity.
- By end of Week 3, students have now named, researched, and
  defended their practice model. Some will feel energized; some
  will feel exposed. Both are appropriate. The practice statement
  formative is their first chance to write it down quietly and
  consolidate.
