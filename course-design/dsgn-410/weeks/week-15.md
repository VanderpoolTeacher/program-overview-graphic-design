## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Communicate** the goal of their practice in 30 seconds with
   clarity and without qualifier hedging — a prerequisite for a
   compelling live pitch.
2. **Identify** the one section of their near-final document they
   are most uncertain about and articulate *why* — naming the gap
   enables it to be addressed before Week 16.
3. **Apply** the Hattie & Timperley feed-up / feed-back /
   feed-forward structure from the receiving end — listening for
   process-level feedback and naming their own next move.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 5 (pitch communication), CLO 6 (AI
workflow disclosure), CLO 4 (reflection and professional judgment)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 2, 5, 6, 7, 8

- *Finalize:* Change Log v1 (Event 1) and Change Log v2 (Event 2)

  event, complete them now.

- Practice your 30-second practice-goal restatement out loud before

  the discomfort is information about where your framing is still
  unclear.

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** The Dialogic Pass

**Goal:** Deliver the canonical Hattie & Timperley feed-up /
feed-back / feed-forward structure in time-boxed form — so students
experience what a *well-structured* near-final crit actually feels
like, before the final pitch. The 4-minute format is tight enough
to force clarity and loose enough to surface real gaps. This is the
framework in action, not in theory.

**Setup:**
- Time: 33 minutes total — cycles run back-to-back with a 1-min
  transition between them. Class size of 8–10 fits with 4 min per
  student. Adjust for larger classes (see Variations).
- Group size: small groups of 4–5 students; instructor circulates
- Materials: each student's near-final integrated document (on
  screen or printed); a visible timer; no slides required — the
  document is the reference

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(2 min)* Divide into groups of 4–5. Each group designates a
   timekeeper who holds to the 4-minute window per student.
   Instructor circulates across groups.
2. *(4 min per student, repeated for all students in the group)*
   Each student's 4-minute window runs in this exact sequence:
   - **30 sec — Feed-up:** Student restates the goal of their
     practice in one or two sentences. Not the whole document —
     just the goal. Peers listen; do not interrupt.
   - **90 sec — Walk the uncertain section:** Student opens their
     document to the one section they're most uncertain about and
     explains what they're unsure of. "Here's my AI workflow
     disclosure — I'm not sure if the depth is enough for the
     rubric criterion." Peers can ask one clarifying question.
   - **90 sec — Process-level peer response:** Peers (not the
     instructor) respond at the process level. No "it looks great."
     Something that answers: *"What does this section do well?
     What does it not yet do that the rubric asks for?"* Instructor
     models the first time through; students take over.
   - **30 sec — Feed-forward:** Student names their next move.
     Not "I'll polish it" — a specific revision: "I'm going to
     add the specific prompt I wrote in Week 5 to the AI
     disclosure and name what I changed from the AI output."
3. *(1 min transition between students)* Timekeeper signals; next
   student starts immediately. No extended sidebar discussions.
4. After all students in the group have gone, the group has 2–3
   minutes of informal conversation — this is unstructured, a
   natural decompression. Let it happen.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- The 30-second restatement is almost always too long the first
  time. Students will try to narrate their whole document. Hold
  the time limit. The overcrowding of the 30-second window is
  itself feedback: if you can't state the goal in 30 seconds, the
  goal is not yet clear.
- The "section I'm uncertain about" naming is emotionally
  difficult — it requires students to publicly admit a gap in
  near-final work. Model vulnerability once: the instructor names
  a specific type of document gap they see commonly ("the AI
  workflow disclosure that lists three tools but doesn't say what
  changed from the AI output — that's the pattern I see most").
- Peer responses will drift to task level ("your data viz looks
  clean") unless the instructor circulates and redirects early.
  The redirect is simple: "What does the rubric criterion for CLO
  5 say? Does this data viz do that?"
- The feed-forward ("name your next move") is where the real
  learning lives. Students who name a specific revision leave
  with a plan. Students who say "I'll just review it" leave with
  anxiety. Hold people to specificity in the feed-forward.
- Pre-pitch nerves will be palpable in this class — more than any
  other week except Week 16 itself. The Dialogic Pass often
  surfaces these. Acknowledge the affect briefly and redirect to
  the work: nervousness is energy; the question is where to
  point it.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class (12+ students):* extend to 60 min, or run
  simultaneous groups and trust timekeeper discipline. Do not
  compress the 4-minute window per student — 4 minutes is
  already tight.
- *Remote:* use breakout rooms; instructor rotates through rooms.
  The timer discipline is harder remotely — assign a visible
  digital timer (e.g., shared browser tab) per group.
- *More challenge:* after the feed-forward, the *peers* add one
  "plus-one" next move the student didn't name. This extends
  the self-regulation step and surfaces blind spots.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- Week 15 carries the highest emotional load of any non-pitch week.
  Students are simultaneously finishing the document, absorbing
  two rounds of event feedback, and preparing a live pitch. Pre-
  launch anxiety is a real and legitimate affect, not a distraction.
  Name it at the start of class: *"Some of you are feeling the
  weight of this. That's not a sign something's wrong — it's a
  sign the stakes feel real, which is the point."*
- Do not expand the class into an extended revision session. The
  Dialogic Pass is structured for a reason: each student gets 4
  minutes, then the next person goes. If you break the structure
  to let one student's conversation run long, you lose the feed-
  forward step for the last two or three students. Hold the clock.
- The AI workflow disclosure is the most frequently under-done
  section at this stage. Students list tools but don't answer
  the rubric's actual criteria: *what specific moments, what
  prompts, what you kept vs. rejected, what you verified.* Be
  ready to surface this pattern in the debrief without shame —
  it's a structural gap in how students think about disclosure,
  not a character failure.
- Some students will feel their document is done and resist the
  Dialogic Pass as "just going through the motions." Hold them to
  the structure anyway. The 30-second goal restatement will almost
  always reveal that the document's stated goal and the oral goal
  diverge — that's information they need before Week 16.
- End class by naming the feed-forward moves you heard across
  the groups. Two or three specific ones ("three students named
  their data viz as the uncertain section; the move is X")
  normalize the pre-final revision and make the remaining work
  feel concrete, not sprawling.
