## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Evaluate** a peer's revision strategy for their Event 2
   scenario — identifying the professional principle the revision
   exposed, not just whether artifacts were updated.
2. **Articulate** the values stakes in their own scenario: what
   was being protected (relationship, labor, ethics), what was
   conceded, and why.
3. **Analyze** how diverse constraints (across different scenarios)
   surface different power dynamics in designer-client relationships.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 4 (professional judgment under constraint);
CLO 2, CLO 3, CLO 5 additionally advanced depending on chosen scenario
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 5, 6, 7, 8

## Pre-class

**Required (~45 minutes):**
- *Submit:* Revised artifacts for your Event 2 scenario + Change
  Log v2 (½ page documenting what changed, where, and why) before
  class. The studio crit requires a finished revision to discuss —
  not a work-in-progress.
- *Prepare:* Know your scenario's title, the key revision you made,
  and the one principle that revision exposed. You'll present this
  in 5 minutes.

**Recommended:**
- Re-read your Change Log v1 (from Week 12). Compare it to your
  Change Log v2. What pattern do you see across your two responses
  to constraints? That's a thing to say in Week 15's reflection.

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** The Diversity Crit

**Goal:** Practice identifying the professional principle behind a
revision — not just evaluating the quality of the revision. Because
each student faced a different scenario, there is no single "right
answer" to benchmark against. Students must reason about the
*values logic* of their partner's choices. This also surfaces
Design Justice questions: different scenarios test different power
dynamics, and the debrief makes those dynamics visible for the
whole class.

**Setup:**
- Time: 30 minutes (pairing + presentations + principle
  identification + whip-around set-up)
- Group size: pairs — pairs must have chosen *different* Event 2
  scenarios. Instructor assigns pairs in advance if possible.
- Materials: each student's revised artifacts + Change Log v2;
  the Event 2 scenario deck for reference (in summative assignment)

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(Before class)* Assign pairs. Cross-scenario pairing is
   required — two students who chose the same scenario can
   observe each other's revisions but lose the cross-scenario
   learning. If the class has an odd number of students, one
   triad with three different scenarios is fine.
2. *(2 min)* Pairs confirm they have each other's revised artifacts
   accessible. Each partner reads the other's scenario title and
   Change Log v2 briefly — just enough to understand the
   constraint.
3. *(5 min per student, 10 min total)* **Paired presentation.**
   Each student presents their scenario + revision in 5 minutes:
   (a) name the scenario and the core constraint, (b) name the
   primary artifact you revised and the key change you made,
   (c) defend why you made that choice — what were you protecting?
   Partner listens without interrupting.
4. *(4 min per pair)* **Partner identifies the principle.**
   After both presentations, each partner takes 2 minutes to name
   *the principle the other person's revision exposed*. Not "you
   did a good job." Something like: "Your revision exposed the
   principle that scope protection requires explicit contract
   language — you can't rely on a client's goodwill." Partners
   write this down — they'll need it for the whip-around.
5. *(Whip-around — runs during debrief)* Each student names the
   principle their partner identified. Instructor captures on
   board. Look for patterns across scenarios. Surface any Design
   Justice thread: when the scenario involved a client with more
   power (Scenario 3 — IP challenge, Scenario 5 — late payment,
   Scenario 6 — ethical conflict), whose interests did the
   designer's revision prioritize? Make this explicit — not as
   accusation, but as pattern-naming.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Some students will present their revision as a list of document
  changes ("I updated clause 3 and rewrote the invoice terms")
  rather than a principled argument. Push: *"Why those changes?
  What were you protecting?"* The principle is what the crit is
  after, not the change list.
- Students who chose simpler scenarios (Scenario 1 — scope creep,
  Scenario 4 — audience pivot) will often feel their revision is
  "less interesting" than peers who chose Scenarios 3 or 6. Reframe:
  *every* scenario exposes a real principle. The complexity is in
  the articulation, not the scenario number.
- The Design Justice thread in the whip-around sometimes catches
  students off guard — they didn't realize their revision implicitly
  prioritized the client relationship over their own labor, or vice
  versa. This is the productive discomfort. Don't resolve it;
  name it and let it sit.
- Students facing Scenario 6 (ethical conflict) may have made
  genuinely hard choices. Honor the weight of those choices. This
  is one of the few moments in the course where there is no
  "correct" answer — the debrief should say so.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *More challenge:* after the whip-around, ask: "If you faced your
  partner's scenario, would you have made the same choice? Why or
  why not?" Extends the principle comparison across scenarios.
- *Less time:* drop the written principle step; do the identification
  verbally. Loses some precision but still works.
- *Larger class:* run the paired presentations simultaneously;
  bring the whole class together only for the whip-around.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The pairing step is the highest-leverage preparation task for
  this week. Cross-scenario pairs are required. If you wait until
  class to assign pairs, you lose 5 minutes and risk same-scenario
  pairings. Do this the night before.
- Students coming in with underdone revisions will feel this in the
  paired presentation. That's real — don't soften it. The partner
  will naturally ask "why did you make that change?" and if the
  change isn't made, the question lands hard. The stakes are not
  punitive; they're just pedagogically honest.
- The Design Justice moment in the debrief is optional to push, but
  don't skip it entirely. It's the thread that connects this week
  to the course's larger claim (CLO 4, the course design document's
  "whose problems you solve" enduring understanding). Name it once,
  clearly, and let students sit with it.
- Watch for performance anxiety about the live pitch two weeks out.
  It will start showing up now as displacement activity ("I want to
  talk about my slides" rather than "I want to talk about my
  Change Log"). The pitch is Week 16; the work right now is
  the revision. Keep the class in the work.
- End class with a specific, confident preview of Week 15. Students
  need to hear that the document is almost done — what's left is
  assembly and finishing, not new artifacts from scratch. That
  reframe reduces the anxiety spiral that can take hold between
  Weeks 14 and 15.
