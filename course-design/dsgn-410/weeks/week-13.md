## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Evaluate** the coherence of their lifecycle artifacts (milestone
   deliverable + invoice + close-out) as a set — checking whether
   the invoice line items match the original proposal and whether
   the close-out debrief reflects genuine professional judgment.
2. **Identify** gaps in their B.4–B.6 artifacts through peer gallery
   feedback at the process level.
3. **Select** an Event 2 scenario from the scenario deck and articulate
   *why* that scenario tests something they are not yet sure of in
   their practice.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 3 (invoice accuracy and legal artifact),
CLO 4 (close-out professionalism and ethical judgment)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 1, 6, 7, 8

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "What is the close-out debrief for — the client, or you?" Quick popcorn. Surface both answers: the close-out email is for the *client* (relationship maintenance, professional finish); the internal debrief is for *you* (your own practice, your own learning). They're different documents with different audiences. Students will often conflate them. |
| 5–10 | **Content delivery** | Brief frame on what makes lifecycle artifacts cohere. The proposal promised X. The invoice should bill for X. The milestone deliverable should demonstrate X in progress. The close-out should confirm X was delivered — or document what changed and why. Walk one example of artifacts that *don't* cohere (the invoice bills for something not in the proposal; the close-out is generic and doesn't reference the specific engagement). *Schön: making tacit professional judgment visible.* |
| 10–38 | **Interactive activity** | Three-Stack Crit — see below. |
| 38–45 | **Debrief + Event 2 selection** | Instructor pulls patterns from the gallery stickies: What coherence gaps came up most? What close-out language read as generic rather than specific? Then: Event 2 scenario selection. Students name their scenario choice and the one-sentence reason. Instructor approves on the spot (or requests a pivot). |
| 45–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Event 2 scenario selections locked in. Preview Week 14: revised artifacts + Change Log v2 due; the class compares diverse responses to diverse constraints. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Three-Stack Crit

**Goal:** Give each student process-level gallery feedback on
their B.4, B.5, and B.6 artifacts as a coherent set — not on any
one artifact in isolation. The "stack" framing forces reviewers to
ask whether the three artifacts *tell the same story*. Ethical
professionalism is most concretely tested in the close-out: does
the debrief surface real judgment, or is it a polite formality?

**Setup:**
- Time: 28 minutes (gallery walk 18 min + return and read 5 min +
  Event 2 selection 5 min — selection runs during debrief)
- Group size: whole class gallery walk; individual stacks on display
- Materials: each student's B.4 + B.5 + B.6 artifacts arranged
  in a row — on the wall (printed), on a shared digital board
  (Figma or Miro), or on individual screens. One process-level
  sticky note (physical or digital) per student per stack visited.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(2 min)* Set up the gallery. Each student displays their
   three artifacts in order (B.4, then B.5, then B.6). Instructor
   explains the framing: *"You're reading this as a coherence check.
   Does the invoice match the proposal? Does the close-out reference
   the actual engagement — not a generic 'thanks, great working with
   you'?"*
2. *(18 min)* **Gallery walk.** Students circulate freely. At each
   peer's stack, they leave one process-level sticky: something
   they noticed about coherence, a gap between artifacts, or
   something specific about the close-out that reads as genuinely
   reflective or doesn't. Not "great work" — a specific observation.
   Students aim to leave a note on every stack; adjust pacing for
   class size. Instructor circulates and models: leave your own
   sticky on one or two stacks publicly, saying it aloud, so
   students hear what process-level feedback sounds like.
3. *(5 min)* **Return.** Students return to their own stack and
   read their notes silently. They annotate one revision move
   they'll make before final submission.
4. *(5 min — transitions into debrief)* **Event 2 selection.**
   Each student writes down their scenario choice and one sentence:
   *"I'm choosing Scenario [N] because this tests [something I'm
   not yet sure of in my practice]."* Pass to instructor or submit
   to a shared document. Instructor reviews and confirms or flags
   duplicates — ideally different students choose different
   scenarios.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Invoice coherence is the most common gap: students bill for
  deliverables not in the original proposal, or under-bill for
  scope they actually did. Name this as a real-world risk, not
  just an assignment error.
- Close-out debriefs are frequently generic ("I really enjoyed this
  project"). Push during the gallery: stickies should note when
  the close-out reads as a template rather than an account of
  *this* engagement. Students are often surprised how thin their
  own debrief reads until they see three peers point to the same
  section.
- Some students will choose Event 2 scenarios based on what seems
  easiest, not what tests them. Push back gently on the selection
  prompt: *"Which scenario scares you a little? That's usually the
  right one."*
- A few students will be processing the Event 1 revision (Week 12)
  and feeling behind on B.4–B.6. Acknowledge the cumulative
  pressure without letting it excuse thin work. The gallery walk
  is useful precisely because it makes visible what's missing.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Digital-only:* Use a Miro or Figma board with sticky-note
  functionality. Same structure; enforce the silent-reading
  discipline by keeping the Zoom chat closed during the walk.
- *Larger class:* assign half the class to walk during the first
  9 min, swap. Each student still leaves a note on every stack
  in their half.
- *Less time:* reduce to 2 stickies per student (1 on invoice
  coherence, 1 on the close-out). Still surfaces the most
  important gaps.

- **Builds on:** Week 12 (Event 1: budget cut) — students' revised
  proposal affects what goes in the invoice. Week 7 (invoicing
  formative) — the invoice here is the summative version of that
  practice work.
- **Sets up:** Week 14 (Event 2: student-chosen scenario). The
  scenario selected today determines what artifacts get revised
  next week. Students who choose thoughtfully here have a richer
  Week 14.
- **Pedagogical lens:** Hattie & Timperley (gallery walk delivers
  process-level feedback at scale — every student gets multiple
  written responses without instructor bottleneck) + Critical
  Pedagogy / Design Justice (the close-out is where ethical
  professionalism is most concretely tested: did the designer
  protect their labor and their client relationship honestly, or
  just perform professionalism?).

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- This is the week where the summative starts to feel heavy. Students
  are managing Event 1 revisions, building B.4–B.6, and now choosing
  their Event 2 scenario. The cumulative weight is real. Acknowledge
  it without diffusing it: *"This is the dense part. It's supposed
  to feel like a lot — real engagements feel like this."*
- The gallery walk is one of the few moments in the course where
  students see each other's full lifecycle artifacts. Many will
  realize mid-walk that their own invoice is thinner than three of
  their peers'. That's the productive moment — hold it, don't fill
  it with reassurance.
- The Event 2 selection is emotionally loaded: students who choose
  the scenario that genuinely challenges them show up with better
  work in Week 14. Normalize choosing hard scenarios. *"Easy Event
  2 = easy crit = less practice before you're doing this for real."*
- Close-out debriefs almost universally need a revision pass after
  the gallery walk. That's expected. Frame it in the debrief:
  "Everyone will revise their B.6 after today. That's the purpose
  of the activity — not to deliver verdict, but to generate the
  revision."
- End class by confirming Event 2 selections. Don't let students
  leave without a committed scenario. Unconfirmed selections become
  Week 14 last-minute pivots, which are worse for everyone.
