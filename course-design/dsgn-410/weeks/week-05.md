## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Develop** a project proposal for a fictional client brief that
   includes scope, deliverables, milestones, and price.
2. **Construct** a defensible budget table that translates scope
   into dollars, with realistic line items.
3. **Use** an AI tool to research market-rate ranges in a specific
   sector, then verify the AI output against at least one other
   source.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 2 (primary), CLO 6 (secondary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 2, 3, 6, 8

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | "Last week you wrote an intake email. Today you reply with a proposal. What's the one thing the client needs to feel after reading it?" Quick popcorn round — no wrong answers. Surfaces priors (Merrill activation). |
| 5–20 | **Content delivery** | (a) Walk through the anatomy of a proposal: scope statement, deliverables list, milestones, budget table, timeline graphic, terms. (b) Demo: pull up the anonymized real proposal from the LMS, narrate the *why* behind each section — what fights it's having on the page. (Schön: tacit knowing visible.) (c) Brief: budget math = (estimated hours × rate) + materials + contingency. Emphasize contingency as a *signal* of professional judgment, not padding. |

| 40–48 | **Debrief** | Pull out principles from the activity: "What did the AI get right? What did you have to verify? What's the move when you don't know the answer?" — abstract conceptualization (Kolb). |
| 48–50 | **Wrap + preview** | Hand off the formative deliverable: full proposal + budget + timeline for the fictional brief, due before Week 6. Preview Week 6: contracts and IP. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** The Rate-Research Race

**Goal:** Practice using AI as an *operational* tool (CLO 6) on a
real practice question (rate research), and surface the
verification habit students will need throughout the course and
career.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (8 minutes of work + 12 minutes of share + crit)
- Group size: pairs (2 students per pair)
- Materials: laptops; access to ChatGPT or Claude or Copilot;
  one-page handout with 3 sector prompts (e.g., "freelance brand
  designer working with small wellness brands in the US Midwest";
  "freelance UX designer working with B2B SaaS startups";
  "freelance illustrator working with children's book publishers")

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(2 min)* Distribute the handout. Each pair picks ONE sector
   (or rolls a die). Assign different pairs to different sectors so
   the share-out has variety.
2. *(8 min)* Pairs use AI to answer: "What is the typical hourly
   rate range for this kind of designer? What's the typical
   project-rate range for a brand identity engagement? Cite at
   least one source."
3. *(2 min, on instructor's signal)* Pairs verify ONE thing the AI
   said by checking another source — Glassdoor, AIGA's salary
   survey, a published rate guide, a Reddit thread, anything.
4. *(8 min)* Whip-around: each pair reports (30 sec) what AI
   said, what they verified, and where the AI was wrong or vague.
5. *(2 min)* Instructor closes: name the pattern that emerged.
   Usually: AI is good for ranges, terrible for current data,
   sometimes hallucinates sources.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most pairs will trust the first AI answer and skip verification
  until the instructor's signal — *this is the lesson.*
- Some will report AI fabricated a citation — name this honestly.
- Some pairs will not know how to verify — coach them through one
  example.

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class:* groups of 3; have each group present from a
  different sector.
- *Shorter time:* drop the share-out to 30 seconds per pair.
- *More challenge:* add the constraint that pairs must include a
  *non-default* practice (e.g., a designer co-op, an in-house
  designer at a community org) and find rates for that — much
  harder, exposes the "default" bias of public salary data
  (Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice).

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The Rate-Research Race tends to run long if you let pairs share
  for too long. Hold to 30 sec. The lesson is the pattern across
  pairs, not the depth of any one report.
- Some students will be uncomfortable with "real" rate numbers —
  it makes the freelance career feel concrete and intimidating.
  Acknowledge this; don't push past it. The discomfort is the
  point.
- The anonymized real proposal is the most-cited resource of the
  week. Refresh it every 2–3 years. Keep one for an in-house
  designer too, not just freelance — exposes the "default
  freelancer" bias in this course's instinct.
- Do NOT use a perfect proposal as the demo. A *real* proposal has
  awkward sections, hedged numbers, places where the designer
  didn't know what to ask. That's where the learning is.
