## Learning goals for this week

By the end of this week, students can:

1. **Identify** at least four distinct practice models (freelance,
   agency, in-house, cooperative/hybrid) and articulate one
   trade-off each carries.
2. **Explain** why the "default freelancer" framing is one model
   among several, not a neutral professional baseline.
3. **Place** themselves on a practice-model map and defend that
   placement with at least one concrete reason.

**CLO(s) advanced:** CLO 1 (primary)
**MLO(s) exercised:** MLOs 6, 8

## Lesson plan (~50 minutes)

| Time | Segment | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 | **Opening / activation** | Instructor asks: "Before today's class, how did you think a working graphic designer makes money? Don't answer yet — write it down." 90 seconds of private writing. Then: "Hold onto that. We'll come back." Surfaces priors without shaming them (Merrill activation). |
| 5–18 | **Content delivery** | Walk through the four model families: (a) freelance / independent contractor — you are the business; (b) agency — you're on a team, billing clients is someone else's job; (c) in-house — salary, single client (the org), stability vs. variety; (d) cooperative / hybrid — worker-owned studio, community-engaged practice, or salaried role at a nonprofit. For each, surface the business structure (who finds clients, how money flows, what "professional" looks like). Pull the Freelance vs. In-House reading as in-class material: put one column from it on screen. Then briefly name the Design Justice primer: the default in most "business of design" curricula is the freelancer. That's worth naming openly. |

## Interactive activity

**Activity name:** Practice Model Positioning

**Goal:** Force students to commit to a self-placement before they've
rationalized it heavily. The act of putting yourself on a map — and
defending that placement to a peer — surfaces assumptions that a
lecture cannot.

**Setup:**
- Time: 20 minutes (5 min individual, 10 min pair discussion, 5 min
  share-out)
- Group size: individual then pairs
- Materials: one printed or projected 2×2 grid with axes labeled
  "Solo ↔ Team" (horizontal) and "Stable / Salaried ↔ Variable /
  Client-Facing" (vertical). Post-it notes (physical class) or a
  shared virtual whiteboard (Miro/FigJam) with the same grid. One
  Post-it per student.

**Instructions (for instructor):**

1. *(1 min)* Project the grid. Read the axes aloud. Point out the
   four quadrants: top-left = freelance; top-right = agency;
   bottom-left = in-house (salaried, team); bottom-right = co-op or
   hybrid. Tell students: "Place yourself where you think you want
   to practice. Not where you think you should — where you actually
   want to be right now."
2. *(4 min)* Students write their name on a Post-it and place it on
   the grid. No discussion yet. Instructor circulates and notes
   where clusters form — this is data for the debrief.
3. *(10 min)* Pairs. Each person explains to their partner in 1 min
   why they placed where they did. Partner asks: "What's the
   scariest part of that quadrant?" Then swap. Instructor circulates
   and listens for: students who placed themselves in the freelance
   quadrant by default (because it's the "design school assumption"),
   vs. students who made a genuine trade-off argument.
4. *(5 min)* Whole-class share-out: call on 2–3 pairs. Ask the
   partner to report what they heard ("my partner placed themselves
   here because..."). This builds listening practice and gives shy
   students a low-stakes entry point.

**Anticipated student responses:**
- Most students will cluster in the freelance or agency quadrant
  because those are the models design programs implicitly train
  toward. That clustering is the lesson — name it without
  embarrassing anyone.
- Students who came from in-house experience (internships, family
  businesses) may push back. Encourage this — they have real data
  the room needs.
- Some students won't have a strong preference. That's fine. Ask
  them: "What information would you need to decide?"

**Variations / scaling:**
- *Larger class (20+):* Run as a gallery walk — students post first,
  then walk the grid and leave a sticky-note question on someone
  else's placement. Share-out moves faster.
- *Shorter time:* Drop the pair step; go directly from individual
  placement to 3 whole-class volunteers.
- *More challenge:* Add a third axis — "Designer-centered ↔
  Community-centered" — to surface the Design Justice dimension
  more explicitly. The 3D framing is messier but richer.

## Author's notes (for the instructor)

- The clustering in the freelance quadrant is almost inevitable in
  a first-week class. Don't treat it as a teaching failure. It's the
  result of years of design education that implicitly modeled the
  freelancer as the endpoint. Naming it openly — "most of you are
  here, and here's why the curriculum brought you here" — is more
  useful than trying to redistribute the dots.
- The Design Justice primer can feel abrupt if dropped in without
  framing. The move that works: "This course will teach you what
  most business-of-design courses teach — freelance mechanics, client
  comms, contracts. But we're going to spend a few minutes now
  asking who that default is built for, and then we'll come back to
  that question throughout the course." That framing gives students
  permission to engage without feeling lectured at.
- The onboarding task (book selection) is intentionally light. Don't
  let it eat class time. Handle it as a take-home; the goal is
  simply to get students into a book early.
- Energy management: Week 1 has the highest anxiety. Students don't
  know each other, don't know you, and aren't sure what this course
  demands. The positioning activity works because it's low-stakes
  (no right answer) and requires brief speaking, which breaks the
  silence. If the room is very quiet, volunteer your own placement
  on the grid first — as the instructor, where do you practice? —
  and model the trade-off reasoning.
