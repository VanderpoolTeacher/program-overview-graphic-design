# A Day in the Life of an Agency Designer

> Pre-class reading for **DSGN 410, Week 1**.
> Time required: ~15 minutes.
>
> This is a fictional but realistic day in the life of a mid-level
> designer at a brand agency — two years in, three years of total
> professional experience. Diego is composite: pieces of three real
> agency designers I (your instructor) know. The financials are
> realistic for a mid-to-large US city in 2026.
> Read it once for the story, then look at the prompts at the end.
> You don't need to bring anything written. Just bring noticing.

---

## Wednesday

**8:52 a.m.**

Diego gets in eight minutes before the stand-up, which is enough
time to get coffee from the kitchen on the third floor and open
his laptop but not enough time to get into anything real. The
office is an open floor plan with exposed brick and a lot of very
good task lighting. Thirty-two people work here. Diego has learned
most of their names.

He pulls up the Keynote deck he was working on last night. Three
brand directions for a regional healthcare network — call them
Anchor, Current, and Meridian. Anchor is the safe one. Current is
the one Diego thinks is interesting. Meridian is a stretch he put
in because Marcus, the senior creative, always asks why there are
only two directions.

**9:00 a.m. — stand-up**

The whole studio gathers near the white-painted wall they use as a
projection surface. Stand-ups run ten minutes, almost to the second.
Marcus runs it. Today: Hartfield Foods brand refresh entering review
phase (that's Diego's project), a pitch for something called Loomis
Capital happening Friday (that's Priya and Ji-Won's project), a
deadline crunch on Vanterra (that's the whole studio's problem —
three people got pulled in to help). Diego says his thing: "I'm
presenting three directions to Marcus at eleven, then the client
presentation is at two." Marcus nods. The room moves on. Ten
minutes.

Diego drinks the coffee standing up because the mug is too full to
carry back to his desk without spilling.

**9:20 a.m.**

Back at his desk. Diego has a window seat, which he got because
Kayla moved to the Loomis project and left the spot open, and
Diego asked the studio manager directly if he could take it. He
is pleased about this in a small, specific way every morning when
he sits down.

He opens Current. The color system is a dusty teal and a warm
near-black. The typography is a condensed grotesque paired with a
wide-set geometric serif — an unusual combination, which is the
point. Diego has been thinking about what "regional healthcare
in the Mountain West" looks like when it doesn't borrow from
coastal urgent-care aesthetics, and this is his answer. He
moves one detail in the secondary lockup. Better. He moves it
back. The first version was right.

**10:50 a.m.**

Diego sends Lena, the copywriter, a Figma link to the three
directions. She's been writing brand voice exploratory copy
alongside the visual work, which is unusual for this stage and is
actually a Marcus initiative Diego appreciates. The note he sends:

> *Sending over the three visual directions before the Marcus
> review at 11. Current is the one I think maps to the copy you
> wrote for the "trustworthy but not bureaucratic" voice. Anchor
> might be easier to sell. Meridian might be too much.*

Lena replies in four minutes: *Current. Absolutely Current.*

Diego logs that.

**11:00 a.m. — creative direction review**

Marcus's office has a glass wall. Diego plugs into the monitor and
goes through the deck. He presents Anchor first, methodically, the
way Marcus prefers. Then Current. When he gets to the type pairing,
Marcus leans forward and says, "Where did this come from?" Diego
explains the thinking — Mountain West, not coastal, the client has
a lot of older patients who associate the condensed grotesque with
legibility in signage. Marcus is quiet for a long moment. "This is
better than what I was going to do," Marcus says. "I had a whole
different direction in my head. This is better."

Diego registers the sentence. Puts it away.

They spend another twelve minutes going through Meridian. Marcus
thinks it's right for a tech client, wrong for this one. Diego
agrees. They land on Current as the presentation lead, with Anchor
as the alternative story.

Then Marcus says, "I want to add a rationale slide at the top.
I'll send you the points I want to hit." Diego nods. The deck is
Diego's deck. The rationale slide will be Marcus's framing over
Diego's work.

Diego does not say anything about this.

Walking back to his desk, Diego thinks: *I know he means well.
I know this is how it works. But that slide is going to say "we"
in a way that doesn't map to where the thinking came from.*

He thinks about it for the rest of the day.

**12:30 p.m.**

Diego eats lunch at the studio with Priya, who is two years older
than him and has been at the agency for four years. They eat at the
long table near the windows. The conversation migrates to bonuses.

The agency has a discretionary bonus structure. End-of-year,
based on studio performance plus manager evaluation. Diego got
$3,400 last year, which was technically above average for his
level. Priya got $4,800. Diego knows this because Priya told
him, because they have an agreement about transparency, which
started when Diego mentioned he was thinking about leaving for
a different agency and Priya said, *tell me the number and I'll
tell you mine.*

"I looked up what Hartfield would have invoiced at the rates
they bill clients," Diego says. "Conservative estimate on hours,
just my hours, not the team. The margin on that project alone is
more than my annual salary."

Priya does not look surprised. "Yeah," she says.

"I'm not saying I should get all of it. Obviously."

"No."

"I'm just saying."

"You're not wrong," Priya says. "The math doesn't get more
comfortable the longer you look at it."

Diego finishes his sandwich. "I like the work, though."

"Me too," Priya says. "That's the thing."

**1:20 p.m.**

A junior designer named Bram, three weeks into his first design
job out of school, stops by Diego's desk. Bram has been working
on a small internal project — a one-pager template for the studio's
case study format. He shows Diego a version on his laptop. The
layout is clean, the hierarchy is wrong, and the type is working
too hard.

Diego finds himself saying things he did not know he knew. "The
headline is doing the job the image should be doing — scale that
down and let the photo breathe. The caption's competing with the
body text; they should be in the same family but at two different
sizes, not two different faces." He pauses. Bram is writing this
down on his phone. "Try it. Show me again Friday."

Walking away, Diego has a strange, specific thought: *I'm the
person he's going to remember from his first month.*

**2:00 p.m. — client presentation**

The Hartfield clients join via video: three people, including a
VP of Marketing named Sandra. Marcus runs the call. Diego is there
to advance slides and answer technical questions if Marcus calls
on him. Diego has done four client presentations in this role. He
is good at staying still and reading the room.

Marcus presents Anchor first. Sandra nods along. Marcus pivots to
Current. He uses a phrase Diego has not heard before — *"a system
rooted in regional identity rather than category convention"* —
and Diego realizes it's the rationale slide, verbatim. The point
is accurate. It is also Diego's framing, rephrased.

Sandra asks about the type system. Marcus turns to Diego: "Diego,
do you want to take this one?"

Diego explains the condensed grotesque, the signage logic, the
pairing. Sandra says, "I really like this. This is the first
time I've seen a healthcare identity that doesn't look like
every other healthcare identity." She is talking to Marcus when
she says it.

Marcus says: "That was all Diego."

It is a small acknowledgment. Diego notes it. It is not nothing.

The call ends at 2:44. They have a go-ahead on Current to develop
into a full system.

**3:15 p.m.**

Diego files his hours in Harvest — the agency's time-tracking tool.
He does this every day, which puts him in the top third of the
studio for time-logging compliance. He knows this because the
studio manager mentioned it once in a way that was meant as a
compliment.

He opens the rationale slide Marcus sent over and reads it again.
*We developed three directions...* Diego sits with this. The
"we" is technically accurate. It is also technically incomplete.

He does not change the slide. He adds the file to his personal
work archive — a folder on his personal hard drive, not on the
studio server, where he keeps versions of things he's made. He
started the archive six months ago, after a different project,
a different slide, the same feeling.

**6:10 p.m.**

Diego leaves the studio. His roommate Tomás is already home when
he gets to the apartment, reheating something on the stove. They
talk for twenty minutes. Diego does not talk about work. They talk
about the weekend.

**8:30 p.m.**

Diego opens his personal laptop at the kitchen table. A friend
from college — Valentina — launched a small startup three months
ago and asked Diego to help with the brand. They have a
loose arrangement: Diego does it, Valentina pays him when she
can. Last month was $400 for a logo. This month is a landing-page
template in Figma, which Diego works on for two hours.

The agency's employment contract has a non-compete clause. Diego
knows it exists. He has not read it since the day he signed it,
which was the day he was hired and very relieved to have the job.
He does not think about it tonight. He is just making something.

The landing page is coming together. The type is a different
system than anything he's worked on in the studio — warmer,
less architectural. Diego finds the work easy in a way that
agency work sometimes isn't. Nobody is going to ask him to
revise this tomorrow. The client is asleep.

**10:40 p.m.**

Diego saves the Figma file, closes the laptop, and goes to
find his phone charger. He is thinking about the Hartfield
color system — not the rationale slide, not the bonus math,
just the color. Whether the teal is the right teal. Whether
it will hold at small sizes on medical forms.

He makes a note in the Notes app: *Check Hartfield teal at
8pt on a white-background form mockup — is it still warm or
does it read cold?*

He plugs in the phone and goes to sleep.

## After-reading prompts

Don't write anything down. Just bring noticing. Hold these
questions while you read; we'll surface them in class.

1. **What surprised you about Diego's day?** Was anything
   different from the "agency designer" image you had in your
   head?
2. **Where did Diego's professional judgment show up?** Name three
   moments — small or large — where Diego made a *decision* that
   shaped the work, the relationship, or his own practice.
3. **What was unbillable but essential?** What did Diego do that
   didn't get logged in Harvest, that nonetheless was the work of
   being an agency designer?
4. **Whose agency experience is this?** What is true for Diego that
   wouldn't be true for an agency designer with a different
   background, body, geography, or family situation? Name two or
   three specifics.
5. **Honest gut-check:** does this look like a life you want?
   Why or why not? *(There is no wrong answer. The point is
   to notice.)*

We will use prompt #4 to ground the in-class spectrum exercise.
Most courses on agency work imagine one type of agency designer;
we want to start the semester knowing which one this scenario
imagines and which ones it doesn't.
