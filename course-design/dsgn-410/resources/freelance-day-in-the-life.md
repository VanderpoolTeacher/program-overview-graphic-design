# A Day in the Life of a Freelance Designer

> Pre-class reading for **DSGN 410, Week 1**.
> Time required: ~15 minutes.
>
> This is a fictional but realistic day in the life of a solo
> freelance brand designer — four years into independent practice.
> Sam is composite: pieces of three real designers I (your instructor)
> know. The financials are realistic for the US Midwest in 2026.
> Read it once for the story, then look at the prompts at the end.
> You don't need to bring anything written. Just bring noticing.

---

## Tuesday

**7:45 a.m.**

Sam wakes a few minutes before the alarm. The window AC unit is
loud. Outside, the alley smells like rain on concrete. Sam reaches
for the phone — first reflex, four years in, still not great — and
clears the lock screen. Eleven new emails. The first one, from a
prospective client Sam pitched on Friday, is a three-line response.

> *Sam — thanks for the proposal. We loved the energy. But we'd
> need to bring the price down to about half. Can you make it
> work? — Devon*

Sam sets the phone face-down on the nightstand and stares at the
ceiling for thirty seconds. Half. Four hours of work, on the
proposal alone, plus the two-hour intake call. Half. The proposal
was already lean. There is no half here. There is "yes for less"
or "no" or "what if we cut scope."

Sam gets up.

**8:30 a.m.**

Coffee. Oat milk that's almost out. Standing desk in the spare
bedroom that's also the office that's also where the cat sleeps
when it's hot. The cat is not here. Sam opens the laptop, opens
Notion, opens the project tracker. Three active clients, two pitches
out, one invoice unpaid for forty-five days.

Sam pulls up the Devon proposal. Reads it again. The brief was for
a brand identity refresh — wordmark, color system, three secondary
mark variants, basic guidelines doc. Four-week timeline. $7,200.
Sam had quoted on the lower side already because Devon's company is
small and Sam liked them on the call. Half is $3,600. That's $80 an
hour for what Sam estimated as 45 hours of work. Sam's actual
target rate is $125 an hour. Sometimes Sam takes $100 for a client
they want a relationship with. $80 is below the line.

Sam types a reply. Deletes it. Types another. Saves to drafts.

**9:15 a.m.**

Stand-up with Maya, Sam's standing accountability partner — another
freelance designer, a friend from school, who works from her
apartment in a different city. They video-chat for fifteen minutes
twice a week. Maya is dealing with a client who wants a logo by
Friday but won't approve any of the four directions Sam — sorry,
Maya — has shown. Sam tells Maya about the Devon email. Maya: "Send
him the cut-scope option. If he says no, you say no, and you don't
spend the next three days hating yourself for not trying."

Sam laughs. Stand-ups exist mostly so Sam doesn't talk to a cat
all day.

**9:30 a.m. — billable time begins**

Sam starts the timer in Toggl. Today's deep-work block is for Client
A — a regional bakery doing a full identity update. Sam is in
Illustrator, refining the secondary lockup. The pastry illustration
the bakery sent over is genuinely great; Sam needs to make a
typographic system that doesn't fight it. Three rounds of the
display face haven't worked. Round four is closer. Sam puts on
headphones and stops thinking about Devon.

**11:48 a.m.**

A Slack notification breaks the flow. Client B (a B2B SaaS doing
a brand refresh — three months in, two extensions, paying late but
paying):

> *Hey Sam, quick one — can you join a call at 1pm? Marketing
> wants to talk through the typography choices for the deck
> template.*

Sam's calendar is open until 2:30. Sam types **Yes, can do.** and
goes back to Illustrator. The flow is gone but the file is in a
better state than it was. Sam closes the timer at 1:46 of recorded
work and resets for lunch.

**12:30 p.m.**

Sandwich. The rain has stopped. Sam answers a few non-urgent emails
while eating: a no to a recruiter looking for a full-time hire, a
short reply to a former client asking if Sam takes intern referrals
("yes, but I'm not hiring this quarter"), and one cold email from
someone Sam doesn't know, asking for "twenty minutes of your time
to pick your brain." Sam writes a polite redirect and includes a
link to a paid 30-minute consult option on the calendar tool. The
brain-pickers are constant; Sam used to say yes to every one.

**1:00 p.m. — Client B call**

Three people on the call: Sam, the marketing lead (Priya), and a
new face — someone introduced as "the new Director of Brand."
Director of Brand wants to know why the deck template uses a serif
display face when "the website uses sans." Sam takes a breath. The
website was designed two years ago, by someone else, and the brand
refresh Sam is currently doing is supposed to bring the system into
coherence — including changing the website. Sam explains this. The
Director looks unimpressed. Priya is quietly mortified.

The call ends at 1:34. Sam logs off and writes a follow-up email
within ten minutes — a recap of decisions made, a flag for the
website-versus-deck question, and a clear ask: *Priya, can you
confirm with the Director that the brand refresh includes the
website? If the website is out of scope, we need to talk about
how the deck template aligns to the existing site instead of the
proposed new system.* Sam saves the email as a draft and waits an
hour before sending. Sam has learned not to send anti-Director
emails immediately.

**2:45 p.m.**

Admin block. Sam invoices Client B for the milestone passed last
week. Sam follows up on the unpaid Client C invoice — third
follow-up, polite but firm. Sam updates the project tracker. Sam
adds a journal entry about the Director-of-Brand call so future-Sam
remembers the dynamic. Sam files three receipts (a stock-photo
subscription, the Adobe bill, a coffee shop where Sam met a
prospect last week) and tags them in the bookkeeping app for the
quarterly tax estimate that's coming due in six weeks.

Admin is unbillable. Today's admin block is fifty-five minutes.

**3:40 p.m.**

Sam sends the Devon email. Cut scope. Sam wrote three options:

1. Half the scope at half the price — wordmark + one mark
   variant + a one-page guidelines summary. $3,600.
2. Original scope at original price.
3. Phase 1 now ($3,600) and Phase 2 in Q2 if budget recovers.

Sam likes Option 3. It treats Devon like a partner, not a problem.
Sam sends the email and closes Gmail for an hour. The reply will
be there when it's there.

**4:30 p.m.**

A new pitch lands in the inbox via the contact form on Sam's site.
A nonprofit working on community land trusts wants a refresh of
their visual identity. They have a small budget — $4,500 quoted —
and a six-week timeline. The work is the kind Sam does well, and
the cause is the kind Sam wants to support. But $4,500 is below the
rate. Sam stars the email. *Decide tomorrow. Don't decide tired.*

**5:20 p.m.**

The cat is back. Sam closes the laptop. Today: 4 hours 12 minutes
billable, 55 minutes admin, one tense client call, one budget
negotiation in progress, one new pitch to weigh. The bakery
typographic system is one round closer to working. Devon hasn't
replied yet.

**8:45 p.m.**

Sam is making dinner with a partner who works a salaried job and
genuinely doesn't understand why Sam can't "just charge more." Sam
has stopped trying to explain it. The conversation is about the
weekend instead.

**10:18 p.m.**

Bed. Phone face-down. The dishwasher is running. The cat is on the
foot of the bed. Sam thinks about the bakery wordmark for forty-
five seconds before falling asleep — the *wrong* version, the one
that didn't work, but Sam has just realized why it didn't work.
Sam reaches for the phone, makes a note ("display face: lose the
ligature on the descender"), turns the phone face-down again, and
falls asleep in three minutes.

## After-reading prompts

Don't write anything down. Just bring noticing. Hold these
questions while you read; we'll surface them in class.

1. **What surprised you about Sam's day?** Was anything different
   from the "freelance designer" image you had in your head?
2. **Where did Sam's professional judgment show up?** Name three
   moments — small or large — where Sam made a *decision* that
   would shape the engagement, the practice, or the relationship.
3. **What was unbillable but essential?** What did Sam do that
   nobody paid for, that nonetheless was the work of being a
   freelance designer?
4. **Whose freelance is this?** What is true for Sam that wouldn't
   be true for a freelancer with a different background, body,
   geography, or family situation? Name two or three specifics.
5. **Honest gut-check:** does this look like a life you want? Why
   or why not? *(There is no wrong answer. The point is to notice.)*

We will use prompt #4 to ground the in-class spectrum exercise.
Most courses on freelance work imagine one type of freelancer; we
want to start the semester knowing which one this scenario imagines
and which ones it doesn't.
