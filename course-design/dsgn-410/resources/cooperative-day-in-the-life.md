# A Day in the Life of a Cooperative Designer

> Pre-class reading for **DSGN 410, Week 1**.
> Time required: ~15 minutes.
>
> This is a fictional but realistic day in the life of a designer
> who is a worker-owner at a small design cooperative — three years
> into that arrangement, one year of solo freelance before it. Mei
> is composite: pieces of three real cooperative designers I (your
> instructor) know, drawn from worker-owned studios in Portland,
> Pittsburgh, and Brooklyn. The financials are realistic for a
> five-person US design co-op in 2026. Read it once for the story,
> then look at the prompts at the end. You don't need to bring
> anything written. Just bring noticing.

---

## Thursday

**8:10 a.m.**

Mei's alarm doesn't go off until 7:45, but they're already awake.
The apartment is a one-bedroom in a northeast Portland neighborhood
that's still half what it costs closer in. Mei's partner, Dae,
is already at the kitchen table with a mug of coffee and an open
laptop — they work remotely in UX research and, like Mei, keeps
morning hours loose by choice. The co-op has a standing norm: no
internal meetings before 11:00. Deep-work mornings are protected.
This was a real negotiation when Kindling Studio formed two years
ago, and it is the policy Mei most defends in governance calls
when a client asks for a 9:30 sync.

Mei makes oatmeal and opens Figma.

**8:35 a.m.**

The active file is a rebrand for Eastside Tenants Coalition — a
nonprofit that does housing advocacy and organizing work in three
Portland neighborhoods. The budget is $9,000, split across three
co-op members. Not large. But the coalition has 1,400 households
on their email list, and the old logo is a clipart house from
approximately 2003. This matters.

Mei is working on the primary mark. The brief asked for something
that "feels like us — community, not charity." Mei has been turning
that phrase over for two weeks. Yesterday's version was close;
this morning Mei sees why it's not there yet. The stacked type
treatment is too formal — it reads like a law firm. Mei pulls
the type apart. Runs it horizontally. Tightens the tracking on
the coalition name and loosens the neighborhood descriptor below.
Adds a modest rule — not decorative, structural — between the two
lines. Opens the color palette. The coalition uses a warm terracotta
that Mei argued for in the discovery session. It's not the color a
consultant would recommend for a nonprofit. It's the color of the
brick buildings they're trying to keep affordable.

Mei works for ninety minutes without checking Slack.

**10:05 a.m.**

A Slack message from Raina, the co-op member Mei is paired with
on the Eastside project. Raina has been developing the secondary
type system:

> *Hey — I've got three display options to share. One's pretty
> outside the brief but I think it's right. Can I send you a
> Figma link before the governance call?*

Mei types back: *Yes, send it. I want to see the outside-the-brief
one first.*

This is what Mei likes about co-op work. Raina is not a junior
waiting for sign-off. She is a co-equal member with a perspective
and the authority to share it. Mei looks at the link. The
outside-the-brief option uses a condensed grotesque that the brief
didn't mention, but it fits — it has a density that the other
options lack, something that reads less like design-for-nonprofits
and more like design-for-a-movement. Mei sends back: *The condensed
grotesque. Let's bring it to the governance call for the others to
see before we commit, but I think it's right.*

**11:00 a.m. — Weekly governance call**

All five members. Raina, Mei, Dov, Prisha, and Claudette. Video
on. The agenda lives in a shared Notion page that members update
asynchronously before the call. Today's items:

1. Eastside Tenants Coalition: type system review (Mei + Raina, 15
   min)
2. Potential new client: Cascade Freight Logistics — Dov's pitch
   (20 min)
3. Q2 operations summary (Mei, 10 min)
4. Quick flags (5 min)

Item 1 goes smoothly. Mei and Raina walk through the three
options. Claudette asks about legibility at small sizes — a real
question; coalition materials often print at A5 for door-knocking.
They test it live on-screen. The condensed grotesque holds. The
co-op agrees: proceed with that direction. Actual decision, five
minutes.

Item 2 is harder.

Dov has been in conversation with Cascade Freight Logistics, a
regional trucking and distribution company that wants a brand
refresh ahead of a facilities expansion. The budget is substantial
— Dov is using $40,000 as the working estimate. That's the
co-op's largest single prospect in two years. Dov walked the
intake call two weeks ago, and he's been careful: he likes the
project scope, the timeline is realistic, and the internal contact
seems organized.

But Prisha has done some research. Cascade was cited in two Labor
Board complaints in 2023, both settled without admission. One
involved a classification dispute with their delivery contractors;
the other was a wage claim that was resolved confidentially. The
public record doesn't say much, but it's there.

Prisha is not saying no. She's saying: *I want to understand it
before we pitch.*

Dov acknowledges it. "I know about the complaints. I looked at
them before I sent the summary. The company says the contractor
situation was a misclassification issue they've since remediated.
I can't verify that."

"We'd be the ones putting a clean face on it," says Prisha.

"We'd also be $40,000 closer to the year we want to have," says
Claudette, and she doesn't say it unkindly.

Mei is quiet for a moment, then speaks. "I don't think we should
vote today. I think we should ask Dov to set up one call with
the internal contact — not a pitch call, a due-diligence call —
and come back with what he finds out. Then we vote."

This is the co-op's consensus practice: if three members have
reservations, the motion waits. Two members raise hands. The vote
is deferred. Dov will schedule the call.

The call takes forty seconds longer than it should because Claudette's
cat walks across her keyboard. The governance call ends at 11:52.

**12:10 p.m.**

Mei closes the laptop and goes to the kitchen. Dae is making lunch
— a grain bowl situation, leftovers from Tuesday. They eat at the
table. Dae asks about the governance call and Mei tells them about
the Cascade conversation, editing for brevity. Dae says: "That
sounds exhausting." Mei says: "It's actually not, compared to just
saying yes and worrying about it alone." They talk about a show
they've been watching. The lunch break is forty minutes.

**1:00 p.m. — Operations block**

Mei is the co-op's ops lead this quarter. The role rotates among
members every three months; Dov held it last quarter, Prisha the
quarter before. It means Mei handles bookkeeping reconciliation,
invoice tracking, member time-tracking review, and anything that
falls under "running the studio" rather than "doing client work."
Today's ops block is about ninety minutes.

Mei opens the shared bookkeeping app — they use Wave for the co-op's
accounts — and reconciles the week's transactions. Two expense
receipts from Raina need categorization. One invoice to a past
client from February is forty-three days out. Mei drafts a follow-up
email: the third, polite, with a PayPal link appended. Then checks
the member time-tracking log in Harvest. Dov logged three hours
to business development last week on the Cascade prospect; that's
correctly tagged as unbillable. Claudette's hours on a brand
standards project are slightly over the estimated ceiling; Mei
flags it in the project notes with a message — *Claudette, can you
check this? We may need to talk to the client about scope creep.*
— and leaves it for the next working session.

Ops is not billable. Ops takes about seven hours a week when Mei is
in the rotation. The co-op agreed when they formed that this load
should be distributed rather than outsourced to a manager they can't
afford, because a hired manager introduces a hierarchy they don't
want. Mei believes this. It still eats into their Thursday.

**2:30 p.m.**

An email arrives from the co-op's shared inbox. It's from a
community organizer at a city-wide renter advocacy coalition —
not the Eastside project, a different organization, newer, with
no design budget at all. They're launching a rapid-response
campaign about an eviction moratorium that expires in six weeks.
They want to know if Kindling can help with print materials and a
one-page web asset on an expedited pro-bono basis.

Mei reads it twice. The request is real and the need is real. The
co-op has a pro-bono policy: up to 15% of member hours per quarter,
allocated by governance vote. This quarter is already at 11%.

Mei does not reply yet. Mei drafts a note to add to next week's
governance agenda: *Pro-bono request — urgent, deadline-driven.
Discuss capacity and whether we pursue.* Mei attaches the email.
The decision is not Mei's to make alone. This is also the policy.

**3:15 p.m.**

Figma again. Mei returns to the Eastside primary mark. The lunch
break and the ops work created distance, which is sometimes what
a file needs. Mei looks at it differently now. The terracotta feels
right. The rule between the type lines is right. There's something
in the mark's proportions that's still off — the gap between the
wordmark and the neighborhood descriptor. Mei adjusts the tracking
by two units. Looks again. Three units. Looks again. Two units was
right. Mei exports a review PDF for Raina.

**4:00 p.m.**

The co-op's shared email notifier pings. A message from the client
who ran the community land trust rebrand Kindling shipped four weeks
ago — their biggest community-sector project of last year. The
client has shared the work publicly on Instagram and in their
newsletter, with a caption that reads: *"Designed by Kindling Studio,
Portland — a worker-owned design cooperative."*

In the last three hours, two new project inquiries have landed in
the inbox from organizations who found the post. One is a housing
advocacy organization in Seattle. One is a food co-op in Northeast
Portland looking for packaging help.

Mei reads both messages. They are good leads. They are leads that
came in because the credit named the co-op — not "designed by the
team" and not one member's portfolio piece. Mei drops a link in the
Kindling Slack channel with a single line: *Two inquiries off the
land trust post. Both look real. Added to the leads log.*

Three emoji reactions appear within minutes. The five-person studio
does not have a boss to tell. The five-person studio tells itself.

**5:10 p.m.**

Mei closes the laptop. By co-op norm — and Mei's own strong
preference — work ends when work ends. No client calls after 5:00.
No late-night Slack unless something is on fire. This boundary
was negotiated in the co-op's operating agreement and Mei has
defended it twice in client conversations this year: *We work
a 40-hour week and we do not do rush charges — we do proper
timelines.* One prospective client walked away over it. Mei does
not regret this.

Today's tally: about four and a half hours billable to client
projects, ninety minutes ops, twenty minutes on the governance
call, ten minutes on the pro-bono flag. The ops and governance
time are structurally unbillable; they are also why Kindling is
an actual functioning studio rather than five freelancers using
the same letterhead.

**7:30 p.m.**

Dinner with Dae. Dae made pasta. They eat slowly. Mei tells Dae
about the two new inquiries. Dae says: "That's good." Mei says:
"Yeah." They watch something. Mei does not open the laptop again.

**10:45 p.m.**

Reading. Not about design. Bed.

## After-reading prompts

Don't write anything down. Just bring noticing. Hold these
questions while you read; we'll surface them in class.

1. **What surprised you about Mei's day?** Was anything different
   from the "cooperative designer" image you had in your head?
2. **Where did Mei's professional judgment show up?** Name three
   moments — small or large — where Mei made a *decision* that
   would shape the engagement, the co-op, or the work itself.
3. **What was unbillable but essential?** What did Mei do that
   nobody paid for, that nonetheless was the work of being a
   member of this co-op?
4. **Whose cooperative practice is this?** What is true for Mei
   that wouldn't be true for a cooperative designer with a different
   background, body, geography, or family situation? Name two or
   three specifics.
5. **Honest gut-check:** does this look like a life you want? Why
   or why not? *(There is no wrong answer. The point is to notice.)*

We will use prompt #4 to ground the in-class spectrum exercise.
Most courses on cooperative work imagine it as an idealized
alternative; we want to start with what this specific co-op
actually is, and which designer it's realistic for.
