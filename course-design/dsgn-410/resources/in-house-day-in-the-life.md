# A Day in the Life of an In-House Designer

> Pre-class reading for **DSGN 410, Week 1**.
> Time required: ~15 minutes.
>
> This is a fictional but realistic day in the life of a senior
> brand designer at a mid-size company — eight years in design,
> three years in her current in-house role. Imani is composite:
> pieces of three real in-house designers I (your instructor)
> know. The financials are realistic for a mid-to-large US city
> in 2026.
> Read it once for the story, then look at the prompts at the end.
> You don't need to bring anything written. Just bring noticing.

---

## Thursday

**7:04 a.m.**

Imani's daughter, Zoe, does not want to put on her shoes. This
is not unusual. Imani stands in the hallway of their condo, one
arm through her bag strap, and waits while Zoe negotiates the
left shoe. The negotiation takes four minutes. They leave at
7:11, two minutes behind the window that gets Zoe to school
before the good parking spots fill up.

Imani drops Zoe off at 7:38, drives back through the morning
traffic, and is logged on at 8:02. The commute is a car ride
and a parking garage. It takes forty minutes door-to-desk on a
bad morning. One of the things Imani negotiated when she took
this job was a 9:00 a.m. calendar block — no meetings before 9.
Most days this holds. Today it holds.

**8:02 a.m.**

Slack: 47 unread messages since she logged off last night.

Imani reads down the list without responding. She's learned to
triage by sender and channel before she starts clicking into
threads. Seven are in #design-feedback, which is manageable.
Four are in #brand-questions, which usually aren't. Three are
from a product manager named Tariq who sends messages in
installments. Imani reads all three of Tariq's messages and
understands he has a question about button-state colors in
the design system. She'll get to it.

The 47th message is from the CEO, Renata, in the #brand-system
channel at 11:46 p.m. last night. It is a single emoji — a fire
emoji — in reaction to the brand-system documentation post Imani
published Monday. A CEO fire emoji is ambiguous as praise, but
the context makes it legible: good fire.

Imani notes this and moves on.

**8:45 a.m.**

The Figma notification comes in through email: someone edited
the primary button component in the design system library. Not
the expected-edits channel that goes through her. Just edited
it. Imani opens Figma and checks the version history. The
component was modified at 10:23 p.m. last night by a designer
named Felix, who joined the product team six weeks ago.

The modification looks well-intentioned. Felix renamed the
auto-layout padding variable to match a naming convention from
his previous company. The rename propagated across 23 instances.
Every instance is now detached from the main component.

Imani makes a note to talk to Felix, not a Slack message, an
actual conversation. She reverts the library version and runs
a quick audit to confirm the detachments are repaired. This
takes twenty-two minutes. The twenty-two minutes are not on
her calendar.

**9:00 a.m. — 1:1 with design manager**

Preethi manages Imani and four other designers. They meet
Tuesdays and Thursdays for thirty minutes. Today's agenda is
prioritization: three things are fighting for Imani's time this
week, and one of them has to move.

The three things: (1) a refresh of the company's external
careers page, requested by HR two weeks ago; (2) a campaign
asset batch for a product launch, Marketing's highest priority;
(3) the brand-system documentation sprint that Imani has been
running on her own time, no official sponsor, because she
believes the system needs it and no one else is going to do it.

Preethi: "What do you need from me on the brand system work?"

Imani: "I need it to be a real project, not a side project.
It needs a ticket, a stakeholder, and time on my allocation."

Preethi: "Let me talk to Omar." Omar is the Head of Marketing.

This is not a yes. It is also not a no. Imani has learned that
Preethi's "let me talk to X" is usually a yes with a two-week
lag. She moves to the next item.

**10:15 a.m.**

A Slack message from a brand manager in Marketing named Claire:

> *Hey Imani — working on Q3 campaign and I want to use the
> cobalt H1 text on a dark teal background. I know that's not
> in the brand guidelines but it looks really strong in this
> comp. Can we get a one-time exception?*

Imani pulls up the comp Claire linked. The cobalt-on-teal
combination has a contrast ratio of 2.8:1. WCAG AA requires
4.5:1 for normal text. The H1 is large text, so the threshold
is 3:1, which it still doesn't hit.

Imani types the reply and then reads it before sending. The
reply explains the contrast ratio, links the WCAG spec, and
offers two alternatives: cobalt on white, which passes and
hits the brand palette, or the near-black on teal, which also
passes and has more visual weight than Claire's comp. She does
not say "that's not allowed." She says: "Here's why it doesn't
work for our users, and here are two directions that give you
the same energy."

Claire replies in six minutes: *This is so helpful. Going
with near-black on teal. Can you update the deck template
when you get a chance?*

Imani adds it to her queue. The deck template update is a
fifteen-minute task that will take four days to get to.

**12:00 p.m.**

Imani closes her laptop at noon. She walks to a sandwich place
two blocks from the office and sits outside for twenty-eight
minutes. She does not check Slack. She thinks about a documentary
she watched last weekend and about whether Zoe is going to want
to do soccer again this fall.

She goes back to the office. This is a boundary she has worked
on for two years and now mostly keeps. She came from agency
work, where lunch was often billable hours and eating at your
desk was normal. In-house has predictable hours — the phrase
her agency friends always say — which is true if you mean
"you know roughly when the day starts and ends." It is less
true if you count the Slack that runs until midnight and the
feeling of always being slightly available.

The lunch break is real. She takes it.

**1:30 p.m.**

Imani's mentee, Jordan, ships their first big internal project
today — a redesigned onboarding checklist flow for new employees.
Jordan is eighteen months into their first design job. The flow
is clean, tested with five actual new hires, and already getting
positive responses from the HR team. Imani has been meeting with
Jordan every other week for six months, which means she's seen
this project through six rounds of iteration.

She writes a Slack post in #design-wins:

> *Huge shoutout to Jordan for shipping the new employee
> onboarding flow today. If you've joined the company in the
> last month, you've already seen it. Jordan ran real user
> sessions to test the logic, rewrote the copy twice to get
> the tone right, and pushed it across the finish line when
> it would've been easy to ship the MVP and call it done.
> This is what good design practice looks like. Go Jordan.*

Forty-two reactions in eleven minutes.

**2:45 p.m. — VP meeting**

The meeting is with Derrick, VP of Internal Operations. He has
a vision for a redesign of the company's internal project-tracking
tool — a legacy system that everyone complains about. He wants
to move fast. He says the word "fast" four times in the first
five minutes. He has heard that Imani did great work on the
employee onboarding flow and wants that energy on this project.

Imani knows two things Derrick doesn't know he doesn't know.
First, Jordan did the onboarding flow; Imani mentored it, she
didn't build it. Second, the engineering team is at capacity
through the end of the quarter — she saw the sprint planning
doc in #eng-announce last week.

"Derrick," she says, "I want to be honest with you about
the timeline." She explains the engineering capacity situation.
She is direct and does not hedge. She does not say "I just
think maybe possibly we might want to consider." She says:
"Engineering can't take this on before Q4. If we start design
now, we'll produce something great that sits in a queue for
three months. I'd rather start design six weeks before
engineering has the capacity, so the work is fresh when they
pick it up."

Derrick pushes back for a few minutes. Then he says, "Okay.
Walk me through what six weeks before Q4 looks like."

Imani opens her laptop and puts a rough timeline on the screen.
The conversation shifts from whether to when. This is the
outcome she wanted.

**4:20 p.m.**

Imani has forty minutes before she needs to leave to pick up
Zoe. She uses it on the Figma library — specifically, she
writes a one-paragraph contribution guide at the top of the
library file that explains what Felix should have done last
night instead of editing the component directly. She tags Felix
in the Figma comment and sends him a short Slack:

> *Hey Felix — added a contribution guide to the design library.
> Mostly for future reference; no worries on the thing last
> night, happens to everyone. If you want to propose a change
> to a component, tag me and we can do it together. Happy to
> walk through the naming convention anytime.*

Felix responds: *Oh wow, yes, sorry — thank you.*

Imani closes the laptop at 4:58. She is two minutes late leaving.

**5:20 p.m.**

Zoe's aftercare pickup. Zoe has strong opinions about what they
listen to in the car on the way home. Tonight it is the same
three songs it has been for the last two weeks. Imani knows all
the words.

**6:45 p.m.**

Dinner. Pasta. Homework — Zoe has a worksheet about fractions
that Imani remembers being confused by at the same age, and
she is somehow still slightly confused by it now, which she
does not admit out loud. Zoe gets it on the third try.

**9:15 p.m.**

Zoe is asleep. Imani opens the laptop at the kitchen table and
spends thirty minutes clearing notifications — a few Slack
messages that need brief replies, a comment in Figma from
Claire about the deck template, an email from Preethi that
says Omar agreed to sponsor the brand-system documentation
as an official project. That last one lands solidly. Imani
writes back: *This is great. I'll have a scope and timeline
to you by end of next week.*

She closes the laptop at 9:47.

The thirty minutes after Zoe goes to bed has been her pattern
for most of the year. She knows it's a pattern. She knows it
is partly the job and partly her own difficulty leaving things
in an incomplete state. She is not sure yet how much of it to
change and how much to accept.

---

## After-reading prompts

Don't write anything down. Just bring noticing. Hold these
questions while you read; we'll surface them in class.

1. **What surprised you about Imani's day?** Was anything
   different from the "in-house designer" image you had in
   your head?
2. **Where did Imani's professional judgment show up?** Name
   three moments — small or large — where Imani made a
   *decision* that shaped the work, the relationship, or her
   own practice.
3. **What was invisible but essential?** What did Imani do
   that won't appear on a project ticket or performance review,
   that nonetheless was the work of being an in-house designer?
4. **Whose in-house experience is this?** What is true for
   Imani that wouldn't be true for an in-house designer with
   a different background, body, geography, or family situation?
   Name two or three specifics.
5. **Honest gut-check:** does this look like a life you want?
   Why or why not? *(There is no wrong answer. The point is
   to notice.)*

We will use prompt #4 to ground the in-class spectrum exercise.
Most courses on in-house design imagine one type of in-house
designer; we want to start the semester knowing which one this
scenario imagines and which ones it doesn't.

  produces institutional value without being institutionally

- The Jordan mentorship is investment: six months, every other
  week, Imani's calendar, Imani's attention. The Slack post
  is public recognition. In-house designers at Imani's level
  often discover they're managing upward (the VP meeting) and
  sideways (Claire, Felix) and downward (Jordan) simultaneously.

- Do not let discussion drift toward "Imani is doing too much
  because she's a single mom." The scenario is about an
  in-house designer navigating a real job. Her parenting is
  context, not explanation. She has structural support (the
  9:00 a.m. block she negotiated, aftercare, a condo she
  owns) and structural pressures (the always-on Slack
  expectation). Both are worth naming.
