# DSGN 410 — Curriculum Resources

> Curated reference pack for the instructor. Pull from here when
> authoring lessons, picking readings, building assignments. Not for
> direct distribution to students — the instructor curates which
> pieces appear where.

---

## How to use this pack

Pull selectively: no single lesson needs more than two or three items
from any one section. Prioritize balance — mix practitioner-voice
canon (Monteiro, Enns) with community-grounded critique (Costanza-Chock,
Design Justice Network, Futuress), and resist the pull toward a
default list dominated by US freelance solo-practice voices. At least
one item in each major lesson sequence should center a non-default
practice model or a BIPOC, queer, disabled, or non-Western designer's
perspective. Refresh links and tool entries on a yearly cycle —
especially the AI tools section and any invoicing/contract software,
which change pricing and features faster than books do. When a link
goes dead, find a current equivalent rather than leaving a broken
citation in the course; a dead resource erodes student trust in the
materials.

## Books

**1. Monteiro, Mike. *Design Is a Job.* A Book Apart, 2012. ISBN 9781937557041.**
Paywalled (purchase or library reserve; ~$25 paperback).
The practitioner-voice spine of the operational side of this course.
Monteiro's chapters on contracts, client relationships, and saying
no are direct predecessors to Weeks 4, 6, and 8. The opinionated,
first-person register models the kind of professional voice students
should be building. Note the default reader Monteiro assumes —
US freelance, primarily agency-adjacent — and surface that
assumption explicitly when assigning. CLOs 2, 3, 4.

**2. Enns, Blair. *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto.* Gegen Press,
2nd ed. 2018. ISBN 9781999523503.**
Paywalled (~$20); free summary content on winwithoutpitching.com.
Enns's 12 proclamations for creative firms that want to position
themselves as experts rather than vendors. Especially useful for
Week 3 (positioning and rate logic) and Week 5 (proposals and
scoping). The positioning argument — specialize to gain leverage —
is actionable and specific, not generic. CLOs 1, 2.

**3. Enns, Blair. *Pricing Creativity: A Guide to Profit Beyond the
Billable Hour.* RockBench Publishing, 2018.**
Paywalled (~$30 via winwithoutpitching.com). The most rigorous
treatment of value-based pricing available in practitioner-facing
form. Chapter-by-chapter companion to the rate-setting conversation
in Week 5 and the budget/proposal work in the summative. Pairs well
with the Graphic Artists Guild Handbook for students who want the
nuts-and-bolts alongside the argument. CLOs 2, 5.

**4. Baker, David C. *The Business of Expertise: How Entrepreneurial
Experts Convert Insight to Impact and Wealth.* RockBench Publishing,
2017. ISBN 9781605440606.**
Paywalled (~$25). Baker advises creative agencies and consultancies;
this book is the positioning and business-model companion to Enns.
Where Enns focuses on selling, Baker focuses on what you sell and
to whom — the distinction between a generalist and an expert practice.
Directly relevant to Week 3 (practice positioning) and the summative
Part A. CLOs 1, 2.

**5. Costanza-Chock, Sasha. *Design Justice: Community-Led Practices
to Build the Worlds We Need.* MIT Press, 2020. ISBN 9780262043458.**
Open access at https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001.
The theoretical anchor for this course's Design Justice frame.
Chapter 1 is the most immediately applicable — it names the default
assumptions embedded in "design for good" and "design thinking"
that the course inherits and should interrogate. Chapters 2 and 3
cover community-led practice and design sites, which support the
non-default practice models discussion in Weeks 1–2. CLOs 1, 4.

**6. Graphic Artists Guild. *Graphic Artists Guild Handbook:
Pricing and Ethical Guidelines,* 16th ed. MIT Press, 2021.
ISBN 9780262542395.**
Paywalled (~$50 paperback); widely held in academic libraries.
The profession's reference standard for pricing, usage rights, and
ethical standards. The pricing tables are illustrative of range;
students should treat them as floor/ceiling reference, not formulas,
since rates vary by region, specialization, and client type. The
ethics sections pair directly with Week 8. CLOs 2, 3, 4.

**7. Arceneaux-Sutton, Tasheka, Pierre Bowins, and Silas Munro.
*Black Design in America: African Americans and the African Diaspora
in Graphic Design, 19th–21st Century.* Princeton University Press,
2023.**
Paywalled (~$40 paperback); check library.
The first comprehensive graphic design history book centering Black
American designers across 200 years. Not a business manual, but
essential for the course's Case Study work in Weeks 1–2: students
profiling non-default working designers need to know there is a
canon of Black design practice, and this book is the primary entry
point. Use alongside the BIPOC Design History course sequence
(bipocdesignhistory.com). CLOs 1, 4.

**8. Battle-Baptiste, Whitney, and Britt Rusert, eds.
*W.E.B. Du Bois's Data Portraits: Visualizing Black America.*
Princeton Architectural Press, 2018. ISBN 9781616897062.**
Paywalled (~$35 hardcover).
Du Bois's 1900 Paris Exposition charts are the most powerful
argument available that data visualization is a design problem with
political stakes — and that designers from historically excluded
communities have been using the form to fight for recognition for
more than a century. Use in Week 9 as the counter-canon to the
default Edward Tufte framing. A single class showing of two or three
of the data portraits is sufficient; students do not need to read
the full book. CLOs 4, 5.

## Articles + Essays

**1. Costanza-Chock, Sasha. "Design Justice: Towards an Intersectional
Feminist Framework for Design Theory and Practice." *Proceedings of
DRS 2018,* Limerick, Ireland.
DOI: 10.21606/drs.2018.679. Open access.**
A condensed academic statement of the design justice argument —
useful as a reading assignment because it is shorter (12 pages) and
more citable than the full book. Students can engage the theoretical
framing without reading 300 pages. Pair with the Design Justice
Network Principles for Week 1 or 8. CLOs 1, 4.

**2. Ansari, Ahmed. "The Work of Design in the Age of Cultural
Simulation, or, Decoloniality as Empty Signifier in Design."
*Medium / personal essay,* 2021.
URL: https://aansari86.medium.com/the-symbolic-is-just-a-symptom-of-the-real-or-decoloniality-as-empty-signifier-in-design-60ba646d89e9.
Open access.**
Ansari pushes back on tokenistic uses of "decolonizing design" and
argues for more rigorous engagement with the actual conditions of
design practice. Useful for Week 2 or 8 to complicate the default
diversity-and-inclusion framing. Advanced reading; consider for
students who want to push deeper into the theoretical frame.
CLO 4.

**3. Carroll, Antionette. "Equity-Centered Community Design: Why
Design Thinking Isn't Enough." Interview and overview at
Creative Reaction Lab (equitabledesign.com) and via multiple
conference talks, including her TED-adjacent work.
Open access.** Carroll is the founder of Creative Reaction Lab
and co-pioneer of Equity-Centered Community Design (ECCD), which
reframes design from a problem-solving tool to an equity-building
practice. Her work directly addresses CLO 1's question of what
practice models are possible, and CLO 4's question of whose ethics
govern professional decisions. Use in Weeks 2 or 8; her Clever
Podcast appearance (Ep. 140, listed in Podcasts section) pairs well.
CLOs 1, 4.

**4. Vit, Armin. Ongoing critical commentary on brand work and ethics
in *Brand New* (UnderConsideration).
URL: https://www.underconsideration.com/brandnew/.
Open access.**
Brand New is the best available archive of practitioner commentary
on logo and identity work, including ethical controversies (rebrands
of organizations with problematic histories, designers who've faced
public backlash, etc.). The instructor should locate two or three
current cases each semester rather than citing a single article here
— the archive is the resource. Pairs directly with Week 8's ethics
scenario work (Scenario 3 material). CLOs 4, 5.

**5. Eye on Design / AIGA. "It's Time for Graphic Design to Embrace
the Radical Potential of Salary Transparency."
*Eye on Design,* 2021.
URL: https://eyeondesign.aiga.org/its-time-for-graphic-design-to-embrace-the-radical-potential-of-salary-transparency/.
Open access.**
A practitioner-facing argument for why salary opacity harms
designers — especially those from communities that historically
negotiate lower rates. Directly relevant to the rate-research and
budget-setting work in Week 5. Use as a framing piece alongside the
rate-research activity. CLOs 2, 4.

**6. "What Does It Mean to Decolonize Design?" *Eye on Design / AIGA.*
URL: https://eyeondesign.aiga.org/what-does-it-mean-to-decolonize-design/.
Open access.**
A roundtable of practitioners discussing what decolonizing design
means in concrete professional terms — not just representation, but
pricing, client selection, studio structure, and whose communities
design work serves. Pairs well with Weeks 1–2 for broadening the
practice-model survey. CLOs 1, 4.

**7. Owen, Wickersham and Erickson, P.C. "Legalities 33: Do You Have
to Give Your Freelance Client Your Digital Files?"
URL: https://www.owe.com/resources/legalities/legalities-33-do-you-have-to-give-your-freelance-client-your-digital-files/.
Open access.**
A lawyer-authored explanation of the legal dimensions of file
delivery, work-for-hire, and IP transfer in design engagements.
Written for practitioners, not lawyers, and uses specific scenarios.
Use as a supplement to Week 6 (contracts and IP) when students
want the legal grounding behind the AIGA SFA clause language.
CLO 3.

**8. Indy. "Guide to Design Copyrights and Intellectual Property for
Freelance Designers."
URL: https://weareindy.com/blog/guide-to-design-copyrights-and-intellectual-property-for-freelance-designers.
Open access.**
A practitioner-level overview of copyright, work-for-hire, and
licensing basics for graphic designers. Well organized and
current; covers the scenarios students encounter in early freelance
practice. Use as pre-reading for Week 6, alongside the AIGA SFA.
CLO 3.

**9. Creative Boom. "Creatives Reveal Their Rates and Share Top Tips
for Pricing Your Work."
URL: https://www.creativeboom.com/tips/creatives-reveal-their-rates-and-share-top-tips-for-pricing-your-work/.
Open access.**
A practitioner survey of actual freelance rates across disciplines
and regions, including peer-shared reasoning about how rates were
set. Especially valuable because it includes voices outside the US
and includes designers at different career stages. Use alongside the
AIGA/Aquent Salary Survey for Week 5. CLOs 2, 5.

**10. Enns, Blair. "The Intake Process." Win Without Pitching Blog.
URL: https://www.winwithoutpitching.com. Open access (select posts).**
Enns's framing of intake as the most important stage of the client
relationship — before the proposal, before the contract. The
vocabulary (scoping before committing, qualifying the client) is
directly adopted in Week 4's content delivery. Pull the specific
intake-focused posts from the blog archive; they are freely
accessible. CLO 2.

**11. "Decolonizing Means Many Things to Many People — Four
Practitioners Discuss Decolonizing Design."
*Eye on Design / AIGA,* 2020.
URL: https://eyeondesign.aiga.org/decolonizing-means-many-things-to-many-people-four-practitioners-discuss-decolonizing-design/.
Open access.**
A short roundtable of practitioners wrestling with what
decolonization means in applied design work — in studios, in client
relationships, in hiring. Useful for Week 2's non-default practice
model discussion and for the course's critical-pedagogy throughline.
CLOs 1, 4.

## Podcasts

**1. *2Bobs — with David C. Baker and Blair Enns.* Ongoing series,
~230 episodes.
URL: https://2bobs.com. Free.**
The most consistently useful podcast for the business side of
creative practice. Baker and Enns cover positioning, pricing, client
relationships, selling, and firm strategy from the perspective of
practitioners who advise creative agencies. The whole back catalogue
is searchable. Recommended episodes for specific weeks: "Pricing
Creativity" (Season 1, Ep. 17 — pairs with Week 5), any of the
positioning episodes for Week 3. The podcast assumes a
firm/agency context more than a solo-freelance one; useful precisely
because it opens the non-default model conversation. CLOs 1, 2, 5.

**2. *The Futur with Chris Do.* Ongoing series.
URL: https://thefutur.com/podcast; also at
https://www.youtube.com/@thefutur. Free.**
The most widely consumed design-business content available. Chris Do
covers pricing, clients, positioning, and business strategy with a
practitioner directness students respond to. For this course,
prioritize: "How to Price Design Work," "How to Find Your Niche as
a Designer," and "How to Handle Late-Paying Clients." Note the
default: Do's audience is primarily solo freelancers and small
agencies; the framing is US-centric. Surface that as a lens, not
just as content. CLOs 1, 2, 5.

**3. *Clever with Amy Devers.* Ongoing series.
URL: https://www.cleverpodcast.com. Free.
Host Amy Devers interviews designers, architects, and creatives
across disciplines with a candor and depth that trade publications
rarely achieve. Episode 140 (Antionette Carroll, Creative Reaction
Lab) is directly relevant to CLO 4 and the equity frame. Use
selected episodes in Weeks 2 or 8 for non-default practice-model
profiles. The show earned a 2024 Silver Signal Award in its
category. CLOs 1, 4.**

**4. *Design Observer: The Observatory.* Ongoing, hosted by Michael
Bierut and Jessica Helfand.
URL: https://designobserver.com. Free.**
Wide-ranging conversations on design history, culture, and criticism
— not a business podcast, but useful for CLO 5 (how design
communicates) and for CLO 1 (the historical and cultural context of
professional practice). The newsletter companion (20,000+ subscribers)
covers broader design discourse. Use for Week 9 framing
or as optional reading for students who want conceptual grounding
beyond the operational. CLOs 1, 5.

**5. *Diseño y Diáspora (Design and Diaspora).* Multilingual,
ongoing.
URL: https://disenoydiaspora.org. Free.**
A multilingual podcast dedicated to design for social change, with
a global geographic range that is rare in design-business content.
Relevant episodes include the Indian Design series (Episodes
651–652), which covers community-driven design, indigenous authorship,
and decolonizing design practice — not as theory, but as practiced
work. Use in Week 2 or as supplemental material for students
interested in non-US practice contexts. CLOs 1, 4.

**6. *Futuress Podcast / Learning Platform.* Ongoing.
URL: https://futuress.org. Free (some content member-supported).**
The audio and written content from Futuress — a queer intersectional
feminist design platform launched in 2020 — covers feminist design
history, decolonial pedagogy, and underrepresented perspectives.
The platform includes essays, interviews, and podcast-style content.
The Decolonising Design community page (https://futuress.org/community/decolonising/)
is a good entry point. Use in Weeks 1–2 or 8. CLOs 1, 4.

## Videos + YouTube Channels + Courses

**1. The Futur YouTube Channel.
URL: https://www.youtube.com/@thefutur. Free.**
The highest-production design business video content available for
free. Prioritize playlists: Business Bootcamp, Pricing, and Client
Communications. Specific videos verified: "How to Price Design Work"
(15+ million views); "How to Find Your Niche as a Designer"; "How to
Handle Late-Paying Clients." Assign specific videos per week rather
than pointing to the channel broadly; students need focus, not a
catalogue. CLOs 1, 2, 4, 5.

**2. Jelly Helm, "Purpose and the Love-Based Economy." TEDxBend.
URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mye5p4MOZxw. Free.**
Helm, founder of Studio Jelly in Portland and former ECD at
Wieden+Kennedy, makes a practitioner argument for purpose-driven
practice — work grounded in values, not just market positioning.
Useful in Week 3 (positioning) as a counter to the purely
transactional framing of client relationships. The TEDx format
keeps it accessible (~15 min). CLOs 1, 4.

**3. Antionette Carroll, "Equity-Centered Community Design."
YouTube via various conference recordings.
URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X7CsBGryOck. Free.**
Carroll presents the Equity-Centered Community Design framework —
an alternative to the dominant "design thinking" paradigm — with
direct application to how designers choose clients, structure
engagements, and define success. Use in Week 2 or 8. Pairs with
her Clever Podcast appearance. CLOs 1, 4.

**4. Inclusive Design 24 (#id24). Annual free 24-hour online event.
URL: https://inclusivedesign24.org. Free archive.**
The annual inclusive design conference publishes all sessions free.
Archives from 2023 and 2025 are available. Session recordings
range from technical accessibility to the professional ethics of
designing for disability communities. Use for Week 8 (ethics) or
to expand the practice-model survey beyond able-bodied default
assumptions. CLOs 1, 4.

**5. LinkedIn Learning — "Running a Design Business:
Designer-Client Agreements." Paywalled (free with many
institutional library subscriptions).
URL: via LinkedIn Learning search. Check library access.**
A structured course covering AIGA SFA use, contract clause walkthroughs,
and agreement negotiation. The AIGA SFA section is directly applicable
to Week 6. Many students already have LinkedIn Learning access via
their institution; check before directing them elsewhere. CLO 3.

**6. Coursera — "Design Thinking for Innovation." University of
Virginia, Darden School. Free to audit.
URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uva-darden-design-thinking-innovation.**
Not a business-of-design course, but the framing module on what
design is and who it's for provides useful activation for Week 1.
The course is free to audit; students do not need to pay for a
certificate. CLOs 1, 4.

## Newsletters

**1. Eye on Design (AIGA).
URL: https://eyeondesign.aiga.org / subscribe via the AIGA website.
Free.**
Weekly digest of design criticism, practitioner interviews, and
industry reporting. The most authoritative English-language design
editorial platform. Relevant to CLOs 1, 4, and 5 throughout the
course. The archive is fully searchable; the instructor should pull
specific articles for each week rather than assigning the newsletter
broadly. Tell students to subscribe for professional development,
not as a reading assignment. CLOs 1, 4, 5.

**2. Creative Boom.
URL: https://www.creativeboom.com/newsletter/. Free.**
UK-based weekly newsletter reaching 55,000 creatives; known for
practitioner rate-sharing, career advice, and business tips alongside
creative inspiration. The rate-transparency pieces and business-tips
articles are particularly useful for Weeks 3 and 5. Founded by Katy
Cowan in Manchester; broader geographic perspective than US-only
resources. CLOs 1, 2.

**3. Futuress.
URL: https://futuress.org. Free (some subscriber content).
Email list via site.**
The Futuress newsletter covers feminist and decolonial design
history, underrepresented designers, and critical design pedagogy.
Consistently publishes original work by designers and scholars
outside the dominant Western canon. Subscribe for a window into
design discourse that Eye on Design and Creative Boom rarely access.
CLOs 1, 4.

**4. Design Observer.
URL: https://designobserver.com. Free.**
Weekly essay and roundup from Michael Bierut and Jessica Helfand's
long-running platform. More criticism and less business than the
others, but useful for CLO 5 (how visual communication functions
in culture) and for contextualizing CLO 1 (what professional design
has historically been). CLOs 1, 5.

## Tools — Legal + Contracts + Invoicing

**1. AIGA Standard Form of Agreement for Design Services (AIGA SFA).
URL: https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-standard-form-of-agreement-for-design-services.
Free.**
The profession's baseline contract template. Modular structure
covers Basic Terms, IP Provisions, and discipline-specific add-ons
(print, interactive, environmental). Updated 2022. Students should
read it before Week 6, use it as the annotation substrate in the
Contract Clause Annotation activity, and refer to it when drafting
their own contract excerpt for the summative. This is the floor,
not the ceiling — real-world contracts often go further. CLO 3.

**2. Bonsai (hellobonsai.com).
Free trial; paid plans from ~$25/month.**
All-in-one freelance business management: proposals, contracts,
time tracking, invoicing. Well designed for solo practitioners.
The contract module auto-integrates with proposals — when you turn
a proposal into a contract, line items carry over. Strong for
students building a solo-freelance practice. Recommend for students
in Week 5–7 and the summative AI workflow section. CLOs 2, 3.

**3. HoneyBook (honeybook.com).
Free trial; paid from ~$16/month.**
Client pipeline management, contracts, invoices, and branded portals.
More structured than Bonsai; better for designers running a small
client-facing business with repeatble workflows. The branded client
portal is useful for students building the CLO 5 pitch presentation
component. Pairs with the practice model discussion: HoneyBook fits
the freelancer-as-business model more than the cooperative or in-house
model. CLOs 2, 3, 5.

**4. Wave (waveapps.com).
Free (Starter plan; Pro at ~$19/month).**
Free invoicing, expense tracking, and basic accounting. The Starter
plan is genuinely functional for solo designers beginning freelance
work. Unlimited invoicing, professional PDF output, online payment
acceptance. Direct reference in Week 7 lesson plan. Students who
are not yet running an active practice can use Wave for the sample
invoice assignment without creating a paid account. CLO 3.

**5. Zoho Invoice (zoho.com/invoice).
Free for up to 1,000 invoices/year.**
Wave alternative with stronger international tax handling; useful
for students in markets outside the US or for the CLO 1 discussion
of non-US practice. More feature-rich for multi-currency work.
CLO 3.

**6. Dropbox Sign (formerly HelloSign) (sign.dropbox.com).
Free plan: 3 documents/month; Essentials ~$15/month.**
E-signature platform for sending and signing contracts. The free
plan is sufficient for students signing one or two practice
engagements. Industry-recognized and widely used by independent
designers. Pairs with contract module discussions in Week 6.
CLO 3.

**7. Wethos (wethos.co).
Free proposal calculator; paid plans for full platform.**
A proposal and scope-of-work platform built specifically for creative
freelancers, with crowdsourced pricing data from 50,000+ freelancers.
The proposal price calculator (free, no account required) is directly
useful for the Week 5 Rate-Research Race — students can compare
AI-generated rate estimates against Wethos community data as their
verification source. The scope-of-work templates cover brand identity,
campaign work, and other common design project types with
bi-weekly price updates. CLOs 2, 3.

**8. Hectic (formerly Indy) (weareindy.com).
Free tier available; paid from ~$9/month.**
Freelance CRM: proposals, contracts, invoices, time tracking, and
client management. Strong for the student who wants one tool rather
than a stack. The contracts module includes customizable templates
with standard IP and kill-fee language. CLOs 2, 3.

## Tools — Project Management + Time Tracking

**1. Notion (notion.com).
Free personal plan; Team plan ~$10/month.**
Flexible all-in-one workspace. Designers use it as a project
tracker, client CRM, rate calculator, process documentation system,
and AI workflow log. For this course, the most relevant use is
building the AI workflow disclosure document (summative Part D) and
a client/project tracker template. Free templates for freelancers
are available in the Notion marketplace. CLOs 2, 6.

**2. Toggl Track (toggl.com/track).
Free for up to 5 users; Starter ~$10/user/month.**
Time tracking with one-click timers, billable-time flagging, and
client/project-level reporting. The free plan covers everything
most students and early-career freelancers need. Directly relevant
to Week 7 (tracking billable time) and to rate-setting logic
(understanding how long work actually takes before setting hourly
or project rates). CLOs 2, 6.

**3. Harvest (getharvest.com).
Free for 1 user, 2 projects; Pro ~$12/user/month.**
Time tracking with native invoicing: track time on a project and
invoice directly from those records, with Stripe/PayPal payment
processing. Tighter integration than Toggl for designers who bill
by the hour. The free plan is limited (2 active projects) but
enough for class assignments. Harvest's freelance rate calculator
(getharvest.com/calculators/average-freelance-rates-by-industry)
is a useful secondary source for the Week 5 rate-research activity.
CLOs 2, 3.

**4. Asana (asana.com).
Free for up to 15 users; Premium ~$11/user/month.**
Task and project management with timeline views and milestone
tracking. More structure than Notion; better for students who
want a dedicated project-management tool rather than a general
workspace. Relevant to Week 5 (proposal timelines) and the
summative Part B.2. CLOs 2, 5.

**5. Trello (trello.com).
Free; Premium ~$5/user/month.**
Visual board-based project management (Kanban). Lower learning
curve than Asana; well suited for students managing a small number
of concurrent client projects. Less powerful for timeline
visualization, but adequate for the formative assignments. CLOs 2.

## Tools — AI for Operations

**6. Claude (claude.ai) — Anthropic.
Free tier; Pro ~$20/month.**
Strong for long-document analysis (reading a contract draft,
reviewing a brief, synthesizing a proposal) and for drafting
structured professional documents (proposals, scope of work). The
context-handling capacity is larger than most competitors, making
it well suited for the summative tasks in DSGN 410 — e.g., "here
is my client brief, proposal draft, and contract; give me a
verification list of things to check." Disclosure note: this
resource pack was assisted by Claude in research and drafting.
CLO 6.

**7. ChatGPT (chat.openai.com) — OpenAI.
Free (GPT-3.5); Plus ~$20/month for GPT-4.**
Best-known general LLM. Strong for rate research, generating
email drafts for verification, brainstorming proposal structures,
and producing multiple headline or positioning variants quickly.
The default tool for the Week 5 Rate-Research Race and Week 9
AI Verification Round. Students should practice using it for
business operations, not creative generation, in this course.
CLO 6.

**8. Microsoft Copilot (copilot.microsoft.com).
Free with Microsoft account; integrated in Microsoft 365.**
If students or the program use Microsoft 365, Copilot's integration
into Word (drafting), Excel (budget modeling), and Outlook
(client email) makes it the lowest-friction AI operations tool in
that ecosystem. Particularly relevant for students who plan to
work in corporate in-house or agency settings where Microsoft
tools are standard. CLO 6.

**9. Gemini (gemini.google.com) — Google.
Free; Advanced ~$20/month.**
Best-integrated AI for Google Workspace users. If students work in
Google Docs/Sheets/Gmail, Gemini's native integration reduces
context-switching. Useful for building budget tables in Sheets,
drafting client emails in Gmail, and generating proposal first drafts
in Docs. Less strong than Claude on long-document analysis.
CLO 6.

**10. Perplexity (perplexity.ai).
Free (basic); Pro ~$20/month.**
AI-powered search with cited sources — unlike ChatGPT or Claude,
Perplexity returns live web results with citations. Directly useful
for the Week 5 Rate-Research Race: students can ask Perplexity for
current rate benchmarks and get sourced results, then verify against
AIGA/Aquent or Wethos data. The Deep Research feature produces
comprehensive sourced reports on market conditions. CLOs 2, 6.

**11. Notion AI (notion.com/product/ai).
Add-on to Notion, ~$10/month.**
AI drafting, summarizing, and formatting natively inside Notion.
For students building their AI workflow document (summative Part D)
in Notion, this allows them to demonstrate AI-integrated operations
in a single tool. CLO 6.

## Communities + Professional Organizations

**1. AIGA — the Professional Association for Design.
URL: https://www.aiga.org. Free membership available; paid
professional membership ~$50/year for students.**
The largest professional design organization in the US. Relevant
resources: AIGA Standard Form of Agreement (free), AIGA/Aquent
Salary Survey (via designsalaries.org), Eye on Design editorial
platform, local chapter events. Student membership is low-cost and
opens access to job boards, events, and the professional network.
CLOs 1, 2, 3, 4.

**2. Design Justice Network.
URL: https://designjustice.org. Free to sign as a signatory.**
Community of practitioners committed to the Design Justice
Principles. Emerged from the Allied Media Conference in 2015.
Provides the principles framework that anchors this course's
critical pedagogy (read at designjustice.org/read-the-principles).
Students interested in community-engaged practice models can explore
the member network and working groups. CLOs 1, 4.

**3. Where Are the Black Designers (WATBD).
URL: https://www.watbd.org. Free to join community (Slack,
~11,000 members).**
A global, volunteer-run nonprofit launched in 2020 that makes
space for Black creativity and connects designers, educators, and
creative leaders. Grew from a single event during the 2020 Black
Lives Matter moment into a sustained international community.
According to the 2019 AIGA Design Census, 3% of designers identify
as Black — WATBD is the primary professional community working to
change that. Students from any background can participate; students
who identify as Black designers will find professional community
and career support. CLOs 1, 4.

**4. Creative Reaction Lab.
URL: https://www.equitabledesign.com. Contact via site.**
Antionette Carroll's St. Louis-based nonprofit that trains youth in
Equity-Centered Community Design and operates as an example of
non-default design practice at the intersection of community health,
economic equity, and design. Not a membership organization, but the
public resources (methodology, talks, published work) are openly
available. Useful as a case study for Week 2's non-default model
survey. CLOs 1, 4.

**5. Decolonising Design Collective.
URL: https://www.decolonisingdesign.com. Open publishing platform.**
Founded in 2016 by eight design researchers and activists from the
Global South (including Ahmed Ansari, Mahmoud Keshavarz, Luiza Prado,
Tristan Schultz, and others). Provides a platform for research,
essays, and visual work at the intersections of design, postcoloniality,
and power. Not a membership organization; the website serves as a
reading/publication archive. Use in Weeks 2 and 8. CLOs 1, 4.

**6. Futuress Community and Learning Platform.
URL: https://futuress.org. Free and member-supported content.**
A queer intersectional feminist design platform that hosts learning
programs, fellowships, and published essays. Founded in 2020 by
Nina Paim and Corin Gisel. Beyond the published content, Futuress
maintains a community of practitioners committed to decolonial and
feminist approaches to design education and practice. Students
interested in collective practice or community-engaged work may find
professional peers and learning resources here. CLOs 1, 4.

**7. Graphic Artists Guild.
URL: https://graphicartistsguild.org. Paid membership; student rates
available.**
The professional union for graphic artists — as opposed to AIGA,
which is an association, the Guild is a labor organization. Publishes
the Handbook (cited in Books), advocates for fair pay, usage rights,
and ethical treatment of design workers, and provides contracts and
legal resources. Particularly relevant for CLO 3 and CLO 4. The
Guild's framing — designer as worker with labor rights, not just
entrepreneur with market positioning — is a useful counterweight to
the dominant business-school framing of most "business of design"
content. CLOs 3, 4.

## Ethics + Design Justice + Decolonizing Design Specific

**1. Costanza-Chock, Sasha. *Design Justice: Community-Led Practices
to Build the Worlds We Need.* MIT Press, 2020.
Open access: https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/12255.001.0001.**
Full citation in Books section above. Chapter 1 (the design justice
framework) and Chapter 3 (design sites — who is present, who is
excluded) are the most directly applicable to CLO 4. The book as a
whole is the theoretical spine of the course's critical frame.
CLOs 1, 4.

**2. Ansari, Ahmed. "Decolonizing Design Through the Perspectives of
Cosmological Others." *XRDS: Crossroads, the ACM Magazine for
Students,* vol. 26, no. 2, November 2019.
DOI: 10.1145/3368048. Institutional library access likely.**
A peer-reviewed academic argument for an ontological turn in design
research and practice — not just who is represented in design, but
whose worldviews are recognized as valid bases for design decisions.
More theoretical than most readings in this course; best assigned
to advanced students or used as background for instructor preparation.
CLOs 1, 4.

**3. AIGA. "Design Business and Ethics."
URL: https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-design-business-and-ethics.
Free.**
AIGA's formal ethics resources, including the Code of Ethics and
Professional Conduct. Pre-reading for Week 8. The Code is organized
around designer responsibilities to clients, to the public, and to
other designers — the three-way structure is useful for the Ethics
Triangle activity. Not a substitute for the messier real-world ethics
conversation, but provides the profession's formal baseline.
CLO 4.

**4. Design Justice Network Principles.
URL: https://designjustice.org/read-the-principles. Free.**
Ten principles for design practice that center community
accountability, historical context, and collective liberation. The
language is accessible; principles 1, 2, and 6 are most directly
applicable to the professional ethics scenarios in Week 8. Available
in multiple languages. CLOs 1, 4.

**5. Carroll, Antionette. Multiple public talks and publications
via equitabledesign.com and Creative Reaction Lab.
Free access to most content.**
Carroll's Equity-Centered Community Design framework directly
addresses CLO 4's question of what ethical professional practice
looks like when the designer is in community with, not just working
for, the people affected by design decisions. As AIGA's Founding
Chair of the Diversity and Inclusion Task Force and CEO of the
Institute of Equitable Design and Justice, Carroll's work bridges
professional standards and justice-centered practice. CLOs 1, 4.

**6. Futuress. "Navigating the Past, Envisioning the Future:
Unearthing Feminist and Decolonial Threads in Design Education."
URL: https://futuress.org/learning/navigating-past-envisioning-future/.
Free.**
A learning resource that traces feminist and decolonial thinking
through design history and pedagogy — useful for grounding the
course's critical frame in a longer history. More accessible than
academic decolonial design theory; the Futuress format bridges
between practitioner voice and scholarly framing. CLOs 1, 4.

**7. Polymode Studio. "BIPOC Design History" course series.
URL: https://bipocdesignhistory.com. Paid (per course).**
A series of courses covering Black, Indigenous, and People of Color
design histories. The flagship "Black Design in America" sequence
is codesigned with Silas Munro, Tasheka Arceneaux-Sutton, and Pierre
Bowins and is the curriculum companion to the 2023 book of the same
name. Not free, but relevant for the instructor's preparation and
for students who want to deepen their practice in non-default design
history. CLOs 1, 4.

## Pricing + Business Strategy Specific

**1. Enns, Blair. *Pricing Creativity.* RockBench Publishing, 2018.**
Full citation in Books section. The definitive practitioner treatment
of value-based pricing for creative work. The core argument: stop
pricing by the hour and start pricing by the value delivered.
Chapter 6 (pricing options) and Chapter 10 (handling pricing
pushback) are directly applicable to the Week 5 proposal activity
and the Week 12 Event 1 budget-cut scenario. CLOs 2, 5.

**2. Enns, Blair. *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto.* 2018.**
Full citation in Books section. Proclamation 3 (Diagnose Before
You Prescribe) and Proclamation 5 (Charge More) are the most
directly applicable to the intake and proposal sequences in Weeks
4 and 5. CLOs 1, 2.

**3. Baker, David C. *The Business of Expertise.* 2017.**
Full citation in Books section. Baker's argument for specialization
and positioning — why a generalist practice is structurally
disadvantaged, and how to narrow a positioning claim without losing
clients — is the business-strategy complement to Enns's pricing
argument. Use in Week 3 for the positioning workshop. CLOs 1, 2.

**4. AIGA / Aquent Survey of Design Salaries. Annual.
URL: https://www.aiga.org/salary-survey (redirects to designsalaries.org).
Free.**
The profession's primary salary benchmarking tool. The interactive
calculator at designsalaries.org allows filtering by role, region,
experience level, and practice type. Updated annually. Use in Week
1 (practice-type breakdown table) and Week 5 (rate research). The
2026 Aquent Salary Guide (aquent.com/lp/salary-guide) is a related
resource with current data. CLOs 1, 2.

**5. Wethos Community Pricing Data.
URL: https://www.wethos.co/proposal-price-calculator. Free.**
Crowdsourced pricing from 50,000+ creative freelancers; updated
bi-weekly. The best available real-time rate verification source for
the Rate-Research Race in Week 5. Not a salary survey — it reflects
project-rate data (how much designers charge per project type), not
annual compensation. Pair with AIGA/Aquent for a more complete
picture. CLOs 2, 5.

**6. Creative Boom. "Creatives Reveal Their Rates."
URL: https://www.creativeboom.com/tips/creatives-reveal-their-rates-and-share-top-tips-for-pricing-your-work/.
Open access.**
Full citation in Articles section. Particularly useful for the
rate-setting conversation in Week 5 because it includes voices
outside the US and at multiple career stages. CLOs 2, 5.

**7. Harvest. "Average Freelance Rates by Industry."
URL: https://www.getharvest.com/calculators/average-freelance-rates-by-industry.
Free.**
A data tool (not an article) that allows designers to look up
average freelance rates by industry sector. One of the verification
sources students can use in the Rate-Research Race alongside
AIGA/Aquent and Wethos. Update note: Harvest pulls from its own
user data, which skews toward designers with active invoicing
practices. CLOs 2, 5.

**8. Graphic Artists Guild. *Handbook: Pricing and Ethical Guidelines,*
16th ed. MIT Press, 2021. ISBN 9780262542395.**
Full citation in Books section. The GAG Handbook is the union-backed
alternative to the market-rate framing of the other pricing resources.
Where Enns emphasizes leverage and value, the GAG emphasizes floor
protection — minimum rates that protect workers against exploitation.
The contrast is pedagogically useful: these two framings are in
genuine tension, and students should know both. CLOs 2, 3, 4.

## Contracts + Legal Specific (Deeper)

**1. AIGA Standard Form of Agreement for Design Services.
URL: https://www.aiga.org/resources/aiga-standard-form-of-agreement-for-design-services.
Free. Updated 2022.**
Full citation in Tools — Legal section. The profession's baseline
contract. Students should read it in full before Week 6 (focusing
on Sections 2, 5, 9, and 14). The modular structure (Basic Terms,
IP Provisions, discipline-specific add-ons) models good contract
architecture. CLO 3.

**2. Owen, Wickersham and Erickson, P.C. "Legalities" column.
URL: https://www.owe.com/resources/legalities/. Open access.**
A long-running column by an attorney who represents photographers
and visual artists. Covers IP, work-for-hire, file delivery, and
usage rights in plain language written for practitioners. The
"Legalities 33" entry (Do You Have to Give Your Freelance Client
Your Digital Files?) is directly applicable to Week 6's IP clause
discussion. The full archive is searchable by topic. CLO 3.

**3. Indy. "Guide to Design Copyrights and Intellectual Property for
Freelance Designers."
URL: https://weareindy.com/blog/guide-to-design-copyrights-and-intellectual-property-for-freelance-designers.
Open access.**
Full citation in Articles section. Covers copyright basics, work-for-hire
versus license, and file-delivery rights in a format accessible for
students who have not studied law. A good pre-reading assignment for
Week 6. CLO 3.

**4. Bonsai. "Design Copyright: Protect Your Creative Work."
URL: https://www.hellobonsai.com/blog/design-copyright. Open access.**
A practitioner-level overview of copyright and IP in the context of
design contracts. Includes sample contract language and common
scenario walkthroughs (what happens when a client claims your
process work). Pair with the AIGA SFA annotation in Week 6. CLO 3.

**5. LinkedIn Learning — "Running a Design Business:
Designer-Client Agreements."
Paywalled (free with institutional library access).
URL: via LinkedIn Learning search.**
Full citation in Videos section. The AIGA SFA walkthrough module is
directly applicable to Week 6. Worth assigning as optional deeper-dive
for students who want video instruction alongside the annotation
activity. CLO 3.

**6. Dropbox Sign (formerly HelloSign) contract templates.
URL: https://sign.dropbox.com/templates/. Free access to templates.**
Dropbox Sign provides a library of freelance contract templates,
including a freelance graphic designer agreement template, that
can be customized and sent for e-signature. Useful as a starting
point for the summative contract template; students should compare
to the AIGA SFA and annotate the differences. CLO 3.

**7. Hectic (Indy). Contract templates.
URL: https://weareindy.com. Free tier includes template access.**
Freelance contract templates with customizable IP, kill-fee, and
payment-schedule clauses. Relevant for the summative contract excerpt
and as a practical tool students can use after graduation. CLO 3.

## Resource-Currency Notes

The following entries are most likely to go stale and should be
verified at the start of each semester:

- **AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, Perplexity, Notion AI):**
  Pricing, feature sets, and free-tier limits change every 3–12 months.
  Verify current pricing before Week 5 and Week 9. The tool names
  themselves may change (e.g., Bard became Gemini; HelloSign became
  Dropbox Sign). The underlying pedagogical use — drafting, verifying,
  disclosing — remains constant even when the specific tools change.
  Recommended: the instructor reviews the AI tools section at the
  start of each academic year and replaces or annotates any tools
  that have been discontinued, radically repriced, or superseded.

- **Invoicing and contract tools (Bonsai, Wave, HoneyBook, Hectic,
  Dropbox Sign, Wethos):** These are software businesses that
  may be acquired, discontinued, or significantly repriced. AND.CO —
  cited in some external resources — became Fiverr Workspace in 2021;
  IxDA dissolved its nonprofit legal entity in 2024. When tools
  disappear, replace rather than leave dead entries.

- **Salary surveys and rate data (AIGA/Aquent, Wethos, Harvest,
  Creative Boom rates articles):** Annual updates change
  the numbers. The methodology stays relevant; the specific figures
  age quickly. Direct students to the live tools rather than to
  printed excerpts.

- **Eye on Design, Brand New, Creative Boom specific articles:**
  URLs for editorial articles are stable (these publications do not
  aggressively rotate URLs), but articles become less current as
  industry conditions change. The Brand New ethics cases in particular
  should be replaced with current controversies each year — the
  archive is permanent, but students engage better with current cases.

- **Podcast episodes:** Show-level recommendations (2Bobs, The Futur,
  Clever, Diseño y Diáspora) are stable. Specific episode
  recommendations may become less relevant as the shows continue.
  Re-verify specific episode relevance annually.

- **Recommended annual refresh cycle:** AI tools section — review
  fully each year. Pricing/rate data — update links/numbers each
  year. Books and foundational articles — review every 2–3 years.
  Ethics case studies — refresh current examples each year.
  Contracts/legal column entries — verify URL stability annually.
