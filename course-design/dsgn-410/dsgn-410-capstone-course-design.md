> **Purpose:** The pedagogical contract for DSGN 410. Built on Backward
> Design (Wiggins & McTighe 2005) — *what should students leave with,
> and how will I know?* — with all seven pedagogy frameworks

> materials (assignments, rubrics, weekly schedule) must align with.

## Stage 1 — Desired Results (begin with the end in mind)

### Transfer goals

After DSGN 410, a graduate can:

1. **Independently scope and price a freelance design engagement** —
   read a brief, draft a proposal with timeline and budget, write the
   contract, send the invoice, and deliver in a way that protects
   both the client relationship and their own labor.
2. **Diagnose ethical and legal risk in a design opportunity** before
   accepting it — recognizing when a brief, client, or business model
   conflicts with their values or the law, and respond
   professionally either way.
3. **Communicate business strategy through design artifacts** —
   translate revenue, market, or operational decisions into branded
   content, data visualizations, and presentations that move
   stakeholders.
4. **Use AI tools as operational infrastructure** — for estimating,
   drafting, client comms, and market research — while disclosing
   their use and verifying their outputs.

These are not one-time deliverables. They are durable abilities
exercised throughout a career.

### Enduring understandings

1. **Design is a business, not just a craft.** Pricing, contracts,
   marketing, and ethics determine which designers stay in practice;
   craft alone does not.
2. **Whose problems you choose to solve is itself a professional
   decision.** Saying "yes" to a client is an alignment of values,
   not a neutral transaction.
3. **Process and operations are part of the deliverable.** A
   well-run project — clean comms, reliable timelines, transparent
   billing — is a design output that affects whether you get the
   next project.
4. **AI tools amplify, but do not replace, professional judgment.**
   They reduce drudgery in business operations; they do not
   substitute for understanding contracts, taxes, or strategy.
5. **Visual communication of business is a graphic-design problem,
   not just a "deck."** Data visualization, branded internal
   documents, and pitch materials are part of the discipline, not
   adjacent to it.

### Essential questions

1. **What does it mean to make a living from this work?** (Not just
   "earn money," but "structure a sustainable practice.")
2. **When should you say no to a brief, and what does saying no
   professionally look like?**
3. **Who gets to be a working designer, and what would expand that
   pool?**
4. **How do you know an AI-generated draft is good enough to send to
   a client?**

### Course Learning Outcomes (verbatim from `source/program-data.md`)

1. Describe key business models and career pathways in the graphic
   design industry, including freelancing, agency work, and
   entrepreneurship.
   → MLOs: 6, 8
2. Develop project budgets, timelines, and proposals in response to
   client or market needs.
   → MLOs: 3, 6, 8
3. Draft contracts, invoices, and usage rights agreements in
   alignment with industry standards.
   → MLOs: 6, 7, 8
4. Apply ethical, legal, and professional practices to client
   relationships, self-promotion, and content creation.
   → MLOs: 1, 6, 7, 8
5. Communicate business strategy through branded content, data
   visualizations, and presentations.
   → MLOs: 5, 6, 7, 8
6. Use AI tools to support business operations such as estimating,
   content drafting, client comms, or market research.
   → MLOs: 2, 6, 8

### Bloom's verb / knowledge analysis

| CLO | Bloom verb | Cognitive level | Knowledge type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Describe | Understand | Conceptual |
| 2 | Develop | Create | Procedural |
| 3 | Draft | Create | Procedural |
| 4 | Apply | Apply | Procedural + Metacognitive |
| 5 | Communicate | Apply / Create | Conceptual + Procedural |
| 6 | Use | Apply | Procedural + Metacognitive |

**Coverage observations:**

- **Cognitive level:** Mostly Apply and Create. CLO 1 sits at
  Understand. **No CLO at Analyze or Evaluate** — this is a
  significant gap for a Capstone-level course. Designers running a
  business need to *evaluate* a brief, *analyze* a market, *evaluate*
  a contract draft. Stage 2 and 3 should explicitly add evidence /
  instruction at Analyze and Evaluate even though the CLO list does
  not name them.
- **Knowledge type:** Heavy Procedural, with some Conceptual and
  Metacognitive. **Almost no Factual knowledge** — that's
  appropriate for a course this short, but means students must enter
  with shared vocabulary (acquired earlier in the curriculum or
  through the assigned readings).

### Whose competence are we certifying? (Design Justice lens)

DSGN 410 implicitly certifies the competence of a designer running a
**Western, individualized, market-based** practice. The default
"freelancer" the course imagines is a US-based independent
contractor with the time, capital, and legal literacy to register a
business, draft contracts, and pursue paying clients.

This is a real and useful default — most students will operate in
this market — but it is not neutral. It excludes:

- **Cooperative or collective models** (designer-owned co-ops,
  worker-owned studios) that are common outside the US/UK design
  market.
- **In-house designers in nonprofits or community-based
  organizations**, where the "client" is the public and the "fee"
  may be a salary or a grant.
- **Pro bono / community-engaged practice** as a sustaining mode,
  not just a side activity.
- **Designers whose first language is not English**, for whom
  contract drafting and client comms in English are themselves
  substantial barriers.
- **Designers from communities historically excluded from
  professional design** (Black, Indigenous, Latinx, disabled,
  first-generation), for whom "networking" and "self-marketing" land
  differently than the dominant default assumes.

**What this course should do about it:**

- Surface this default explicitly in Week 1. Don't pretend the
  syllabus is universal.
- Make at least one assignment require students to articulate a
  *non-default* business model (cooperative, community-engaged,
  in-house at a nonprofit, etc.) and what its trade-offs are.
- In ethics scenarios (CLO 4), use cases where the "professional"
  response is contested — saying no to a client whose values
  conflict, charging less when the client cannot pay more, declining
  work that exploits a community.

This isn't an add-on. It's how MLO 8 (entrepreneurial thinking)
becomes intellectually serious rather than uncritical.

## Stage 2 — Acceptable Evidence

### Performance task

The course is 1 credit. The headline deliverable is a **Freelance
Practice Portfolio** — a single integrated artifact that bundles:

- A 1-page **business plan** for the student's own design practice
  (model, target clients, services, rates)
- A **proposal + budget + timeline** template, filled in for one
  realistic prospective project
- A **contract template** (or annotated review of an existing
  contract), tailored to the practice model
- A **data-driven presentation** that makes the case for the
  practice — to a hypothetical investor, partner, or self
- A short **AI-tools workflow document** showing how AI is integrated
  into the operations
- A **reflection** (300–500 words) addressing one of the essential
  questions above

The portfolio is structured as a Notion / Figma / PDF deliverable
that the student could plausibly hand to a real client or mentor.
That authenticity is the test.

### Multiple measures

| Measure | What it shows | CLO(s) |
|---|---|---|
| Freelance Practice Portfolio (final) | Integration across CLOs | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 |
| Process documentation (drafts + iterations) | How the work developed; AI use disclosure | 4, 6 |
| Mid-course pitch (5-min presentation) | CLO 5 in the wild | 5, 7 |
| Peer review notes (giving + receiving) | Hattie & Timperley feedback in practice | 4, 7 |
| Reflection (in portfolio) | Engagement with essential questions | 4 |
| Internship cross-reference (if DSGN 491 is concurrent) | Real-world transfer | 1, 4 |

### Course-level rubric

**Total: 24 points** (6 criteria × 4-point competency scale)

| # | Criterion | CLO(s) | Exemplary (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Business model articulation | CLO 1 | Practice model is specific, sustainable, and grounded in market awareness; trade-offs are surfaced | Model is specific and viable; minor gaps in trade-off awareness | Model is generic or under-specified; trade-offs not engaged | No coherent model |
| 2 | Project economics | CLO 2 | Proposal/budget/timeline are realistic, audience-aware, and defensible | Realistic with minor gaps | Surface-level; numbers don't pencil out | Missing or unrealistic |
| 3 | Legal artifacts | CLO 3 | Contract/invoice/IP terms are accurate, customized to the practice, and clearly explained | Accurate, lightly customized | Generic; copy-paste with minor edits | Missing or substantively wrong |
| 4 | Ethical / professional judgment | CLO 4 | Engages a real values-conflict scenario; demonstrates principled, professional response | Solid ethical reasoning; minor gaps | Generic ethics language; no real conflict engaged | No engagement |
| 5 | Business communication (branded + data viz + pitch) | CLO 5 | Pitch is persuasive; data visualization adds insight; branding consistent | Persuasive; data viz adequate | Pitch is informational, not persuasive; data viz weak | No data viz; pitch unclear |
| 6 | AI as operational tool | CLO 6 | AI is integrated as workflow infrastructure with clear disclosure and verification | Integrated; some gaps in disclosure | AI used but as one-off tool, not workflow | No AI use, or undisclosed |

### Hattie & Timperley feedback structure for milestones

DSGN 410 is short (1 credit) but should still have at least **3
milestone touchpoints** beyond the final:

1. **Week 2 — Practice-model sketch.** Feed-up: "What kind of
   practice are you imagining?" Feed-back: peer + instructor at
   process level. Feed-forward: student names what they'll refine.
2. **Week 4 — Pitch dry-run.** Feed-up: "You have 5 minutes to make
   the case for this practice. What's the one thing the audience
   should remember?" Feed-back: peer process-level. Feed-forward:
   student names the change for final.
3. **Week 6 — Portfolio walkthrough (final).** Feed-up: "Could you
   hand this to a mentor tomorrow?" Feed-back: instructor + (if
   possible) practitioner guest. Feed-forward: not for revision —
   for *next* practice (career trajectory).

Crit format: dialogic, not verdict. Student speaks first to restate
the goal; peers reflect what they observed; student names the next
move; instructor closes with framing, not judgment.

### Stakes and authenticity

Authenticity comes from the **DSGN 491 internship running in
parallel**. Whenever possible, students should anchor their portfolio
in their internship context — write a contract for a project they
actually did, build a budget for a real client they're working with.
This makes the work real-with-stakes and shortens the simulation
distance.

For students whose internship doesn't lend itself to this (e.g.,
salaried in-house intern), provide a roster of fictional but
realistic client briefs that still raise the same ethical and
operational questions.

## Stage 3 — Learning Plan

### Course arc (1-credit, ~8 weeks at half-time pacing)

| Phase | Roughly when | Focus | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Orientation | Weeks 1–2 | Survey of practice models; surface defaults; sketch *your* model | Practice-model sketch |
| 2 — Operations | Weeks 3–4 | Proposals, budgets, contracts, invoicing; AI as ops tool | Proposal + contract draft |
| 3 — Communication | Weeks 5–6 | Branded presentation, data viz, pitch | Pitch deck + dry run |
| 4 — Integration | Weeks 7–8 | Portfolio assembly + reflection + final review | Final freelance practice portfolio |

### Merrill's First Principles applied

| Principle | How it shows up |
|---|---|
| 1. Problem-centered | Each student's *own* practice model + (where possible) their concurrent DSGN 491 internship as the anchor problem |
| 2. Activation | Week 1 surfaces what students already know about freelance work, what they assume, what they fear; explicit conversation about the default model and its limits |
| 3. Demonstration | Case studies of 3–4 different practice models (independent freelancer, in-house at nonprofit, design co-op, contract studio); each with a real designer's voice (interview, podcast clip, or guest) |
| 4. Application | Students apply techniques (proposals, contracts, pitches) to their own model with at least one iteration cycle (sketch → feedback → revise) |
| 5. Integration | Final portfolio is structured to be usable beyond the course — handed to a mentor, attached to a job application, referenced in their professional bio |

### Kolb cycle across the course

| Kolb stage | Where in this course |
|---|---|
| Concrete Experience (CE) | Drafting actual artifacts (proposal, contract, pitch); cross-referencing the DSGN 491 internship |
| Reflective Observation (RO) | Peer crit at each milestone; reflection paragraph in the final portfolio; in-class debrief after the pitch dry-run |
| Abstract Conceptualization (AC) | Brief instructor-led syntheses after each phase ("What did we learn about contracts that generalizes?"); essential-question discussions |
| Active Experimentation (AE) | Iterating drafts; trying a different model framing; final portfolio structured for *use* (Integration); transfer to internship context |

### Schön's reflective practicum

- **Tacit knowing made visible:** Bring in real designers' artifacts
  — a real freelance contract (anonymized), a real budget for a
  project the instructor or guest worked on. Walk through *why*
  each clause exists. The thinking-behind-the-artifact is the lesson.
- **Reflection-in-action prompts:** Briefs include real ambiguity
  ("the client wants more work for the same budget — what do you
  do?"). Students must reframe in the middle of the work, not just
  execute a known answer.
- **Hall of mirrors (dialogic crit):** Crit format described in
  Stage 2 — student speaks first, peers reflect, student names next
  move, instructor frames. No "instructor delivers verdict" format.

### Critical Pedagogy / Design Justice in practice

- **Brief framing:** Briefs in the course explicitly include
  non-default scenarios (cooperative practice, community-engaged
  work, designing for clients with limited capital). Students aren't
  forced to choose these but they must be visible options.
- **Canon:** Case studies and guest voices include designers from
  underrepresented backgrounds running viable practices — Indigenous
  design studios, Black-owned agencies, designer-led co-ops, queer
  design collectives. *Not as case studies of difference, but as
  contributors to the canon of "what a working design practice looks
  like."*
- **Crit norms:** First-mover protocols (the student presents and
  speaks first; the instructor speaks last) protect quieter voices.
  Feedback structured at Process / Self-regulation level (Hattie &
  Timperley); no Self-level praise or criticism.
- **Community partner / external stakeholder:** Where possible,
  invite a working designer with a non-default practice as guest
  speaker / pitch-day reviewer. If unavailable, anchor at least one
  case study in their published voice.

### Course connections

- **Prerequisites:** DSGN 412 (Portfolio Design, 1 cr Fall 2028)
  immediately precedes — students enter DSGN 410 with a portfolio in
  hand.
- **Paired courses (Spring 2029):**
  - **DSGN 491 — Design Internship** (3 cr) runs concurrently. The
    internship provides the lived-experience layer (CE in Kolb
    terms); DSGN 410 provides the framework / business knowledge
    (AC). Cross-pollination is a *design feature* of the curriculum,
    not an accident — leverage it.
  - **DSGN 498 — Senior Capstone** runs in Fall 2028, also
    immediately precedes. Some capstone projects may have a
    business-model dimension that DSGN 410 then formalizes.
- **Downstream:** DSGN 410 is the last DSGN course before graduation.
  Its outputs (portfolio + practice plan + contract template) should
  be usable on Day 1 after graduation.

## Open questions and constraints

These need to be resolved before the course is implemented:

1. **Credit-load mismatch.** The course is 1 credit, which suggests
   ~45 hours of student work over the semester. The deliverable
   load proposed here (portfolio + 6 component artifacts + pitch +
   reflection) is closer to 60–80 hours. Either reduce scope, raise
   credits, or accept that students will not complete all
   deliverables to Exemplary level.

2. **Internship coordination.** Leveraging DSGN 491 requires the
   internship to be (a) concurrent and (b) the kind that produces
   client-like work. Students whose internships are pure
   in-house-salaried may not have transferable artifacts. What's
   the fallback?

3. **Guest speakers / community partners.** Bringing in working
   designers from non-default practice models requires a network
   the program may not yet have. Who is the program's outreach
   contact for this?

4. **AI tool currency.** The AI landscape shifts every 6–12 months.
   What's the maintenance plan for keeping CLO 6 current — naming
   specific tools (estimating apps, contract drafters) that go in
   and out of the market?

5. **Assessment calibration with multiple instructors.** If DSGN 410
   is taught by different faculty in future cohorts, how do we keep
   the rubric calibrated? Is there a sample grading session built
   in?

6. **Faculty load.** Running this course as designed requires
   ongoing relationships (guest speakers, anonymized real
   contracts), case-study curation, and current AI-tool literacy.
   That's more than a typical 1-credit course usually carries.
